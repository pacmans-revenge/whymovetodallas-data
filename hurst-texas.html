Getting Your Bearings

Where Is Hurst, Texas?

Hurst sits in the heart of the Mid-Cities corridor — that stretch of Tarrant County between Dallas and Fort Worth that outsiders often underestimate and locals quietly love. Sandwiched between Bedford and North Richland Hills, with Euless to the south, Hurst covers roughly 10 square miles and sits directly on Highway 183, one of the most important commute arteries in North Tarrant County. That highway is the key to understanding why Hurst works so well for working families: DFW International Airport is about 8 miles east, Downtown Fort Worth is 14 miles west, and Las Colinas and Irving's major employer corridor are about 10 miles in the other direction.

For families relocating for aviation, aerospace, defense, or healthcare roles — sectors with heavy footprints at American Airlines, Lockheed Martin, Bell Textron, and Cook Children's — Hurst is genuinely one of the most practical addresses in Tarrant County. You get HEB ISD schools without paying Colleyville prices, and your commute runs along one of the region's better-maintained highway corridors. The tradeoff is a denser, more established housing stock compared to newer master-planned suburbs to the north — but for many families, that's not a tradeoff at all.

Education

Hurst-Euless-Bedford ISD — Schools Deep Dive

Hurst-Euless-Bedford ISD — universally shortened to "HEB ISD" — is one of Tarrant County's most respected multi-city school districts, serving students across three communities with approximately 22,000 students on 39 campuses. The district earns an A+ overall grade from Niche (2026) and a B+ from the Texas Education Agency (88/100, 2025). That combination means families get both the perceived quality signal that A+ communicates and the state accountability backing that a 88 TEA score represents. For relocating families, HEB ISD is one of the most underrated districts in North Texas — excellent schools at a price point well below what Southlake or Keller command.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Hurst

Active Youth Sports Culture

The Mid-Cities Athletic Association (MCAA) runs one of the most active youth league programs in the area — soccer, baseball, softball, and basketball leagues run spring through fall. HEB ISD's athletics program is consistently strong. Kids here have no shortage of competitive options, and most fields and complexes are a short drive within the city.

Bear Creek Greenway & 29 Parks

Hurst has 29 parks distributed throughout the city, with Bear Creek Park as the flagship — a large multi-use facility with sports fields, covered pavilions, and playgrounds. The city's park system is well-maintained and genuinely usable for everyday family life, not just weekend visits.

North East Mall & Central Drive Dining

North East Mall anchors retail in the city — not a luxury destination, but a practical full-service option with anchor stores, dining, and services. The Central Drive and Pipeline Road corridors have a solid mix of local restaurants, chains, and coffee shops. Most daily errands stay in Hurst without a highway trip.

Hurst Celebration Festival & City Events

The annual Hurst Celebration Festival draws tens of thousands of residents each year — live music, food vendors, carnival rides, and fireworks. The city also runs regular programming at Hurst Recreation Center and the library for families and kids. It's not a nightlife city, but community events fill the calendar.

8 Miles to DFW Airport

Hurst is one of the closest residential suburbs to DFW International Airport — roughly 12 minutes in normal traffic. For families with a parent who travels frequently for work, or anyone relocating for an aviation or airport-adjacent role, this is a practical advantage that most families from California or New York genuinely didn't expect to care about until they're catching a 6 a.m. flight.

Stable, Established Neighborhood Fabric

Hurst doesn't have a HOA-managed master-planned community aesthetic, and that's kind of the point. The neighborhoods are lived-in, the yards are mature, the neighbors have been there a while. Families from California or New York often describe the community feel here as more genuine than in newer developments — less curated, more real. People wave. Kids play outside.

Honest tradeoff: Hurst is not a dining destination or a weekend entertainment hub. There's no signature restaurant row, no walkable downtown, and after 9 p.m. the city is quiet. Fort Worth's cultural scene — the Near Southside, Sundance Square, the Cultural District — is 20–25 minutes away and makes up for everything Hurst doesn't have locally. Most families find that trade-off more than acceptable.
Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & City Amenities

Hurst is more walkable than most DFW suburbs — that's the honest starting point. A Walk Score of 76 ("Very Walkable") reflects the dense Mid-Cities grid: businesses, parks, and services are closer together here than in newer master-planned suburbs built around cul-de-sacs and collector roads. In practice, that means a coffee run, a park visit, or a quick errand often doesn't require getting on the highway. But a personal vehicle is still essential — this is not a transit city.

Crime & Safety

How Safe Is Hurst?

Hurst's safety profile is mixed — which is worth being upfront about. The violent crime rate sits well below the U.S. average, which is the number that matters most for family safety. Property crime is more elevated, running close to the Texas average. Niche gives Hurst a C+ for crime and safety, reflecting the reality of a denser, older Mid-Cities suburb that's safer than Dallas or Fort Worth proper but not in the same tier as Southlake, Colleyville, or Keller. The Hurst Police Department maintains an active community policing presence, and residents generally describe the city as feeling safe day-to-day.

New Construction

New Homes in Hurst

Hurst is a fully established suburb — the kind of place where the neighborhoods are set, the trees are mature, and the community feel is already there when you move in. There's little vacant land left for large-scale new residential development, so most families buying here are focused on the resale market. That's not a disadvantage: Hurst has strong inventory across a range of price points, lot sizes, and neighborhood styles, and resale homes often come with finished landscaping, custom touches, and HOA communities that are already thriving. If you're weighing Hurst against a suburb with active new construction, the honest trade-off is this — you're giving up the ability to choose your finishes and get a builder warranty, and gaining a suburb where the character and school patterns are well-established and predictable. For many relocating families, that's exactly what they're looking for. Pros & Cons of Living in Hurst

I won't sell you on Hurst. I'll give you the real picture so you can decide if it's right for your family.

✓ What Hurst Does Well

  • HEB ISD A+ school district — One of the top-rated ISDs in the Mid-Cities, accessible at a sub-$400K median price. That combination is increasingly hard to find anywhere in North Texas.
  • 8 miles from DFW Airport — One of the closest major suburbs to the airport. A real advantage for frequent business travelers and aviation workers that most families underestimate until they're catching a 6 a.m. flight.
  • No MUD or PID assessments — Hurst is a fully built-out city. No hidden special district fees tacked onto your tax bill, which is a meaningful difference vs. new construction suburbs where MUD rates can add 0.3–0.5% on top.
  • Mid-Cities central location — 20 minutes to Fort Worth, 20 minutes to Downtown Dallas, 12 minutes to DFW Airport. You're not committing to either major city and you can pivot either direction.
  • Affordable district entry — Access to A+ HEB ISD at ~$360K median vs. $600K–$900K+ in Frisco, Southlake, or Colleyville. For families doing the math after years of California or New York housing costs, this is a genuine unlock.
  • No state income tax — Texas saves households $10,000–$25,000+ per year compared to California, New York, or Illinois for most income levels.

— What to Weigh Carefully

  • Older housing stock — Most homes were built between 1960 and 1995. Expect to budget for updates: roof, HVAC, kitchen, bathrooms. New construction is essentially nonexistent inside Hurst city limits.
  • Crime C+ (Niche) — Property crime runs near the Texas average, and the overall Niche safety grade reflects a more urban Mid-Cities reality. Meaningfully safer than Dallas or Fort Worth proper, but not in the same tier as Southlake, Colleyville, or Keller.
  • No master-planned community feel — Hurst doesn't have a manicured trail system, a resort-style amenity center, or a HOA governing the streetscape. It's a working city, not a lifestyle brand. Some families love that; some miss the polish.
  • Car-dependent — No meaningful public transit. A personal vehicle is essential. Not ideal if you're used to public transportation.
  • SH-183 rush hour congestion — The Airport Freeway backs up eastbound toward Dallas in the morning and westbound toward Fort Worth in the afternoon. Budget 10–15 extra minutes during peak commute hours if your route follows the corridor.

Neighborhoods near HEB ISD's top-rated elementary campuses are the most sought-after. The area around Hurst Hills Elementary in north Hurst is popular for its walkability to school. Bellaire Addition offers well-maintained ranch homes near parks at prices in the $300K–$380K range. Neighborhoods near Precinct Line Road in northeast Hurst provide proximity to Chisholm Park and newer retail. Most Hurst neighborhoods were built 1970s–1990s — expect traditional ranch-style and brick homes rather than new construction.

Hurst's market moves at 24 median days on market — fast enough that waiting too long in a rental can cost you the home you want. That said, Hurst, Bedford, and Euless are all within the same HEB ISD, so if you're deciding between all three Mid-Cities communities, renting for 3–6 months lets you narrow the neighborhood decision without worrying about changing school districts when you eventually buy. For families relocating from out of state, a short-term rental in the area gives you time to understand which neighborhoods fit your commute and lifestyle before committing.

Hurst's violent crime rate sits at approximately 3.1 per 1,000 residents — meaningfully below the U.S. average of 4.0 — which is reassuring for families. Property crime is more elevated at around 21 per 1,000 residents, close to the Texas average of 22.4. Niche gives Hurst a C+ for crime and safety. The honest assessment: it's significantly safer than Dallas or Fort Worth proper, but not in the same league as Southlake, Colleyville, Keller, or Flower Mound for safety rankings. Neighborhood selection within Hurst matters — some corridors are quieter than others.

Hurst has no rail service and limited bus coverage. Trinity Metro operates bus routes through the Mid-Cities corridor, but service is not comprehensive enough to replace a car for most daily needs. A personal vehicle is essential — as it is in virtually every DFW suburb. If public transit is a hard requirement, Hurst (and most of the Mid-Cities) is not the right fit. Budget for two vehicles for a two-adult household.

Hurst sits within 20–25 minutes of two major employment corridors. Fort Worth is home to American Airlines (~26,000 employees at the headquarters campus), Lockheed Martin Aeronautics (~14,000), Bell Textron (~8,000), Alcon, and BNSF Railway HQ. Las Colinas and Irving — accessible via SH-183 in 15–20 minutes — host Celanese, Kimberly-Clark, and Fluor Corporation. DFW Airport is just 8 miles from Hurst, making it one of the most convenient suburb locations for aviation-related employment. Within Hurst, North East Mall, HEB ISD, and several healthcare facilities are the primary local employers.

SH-183 (Airport Freeway) is the main artery through Hurst and gets congested during peak hours — eastbound toward Dallas in the morning, westbound toward Fort Worth in the afternoon. I-820 runs along Hurst's southern edge and provides a useful north-south alternative. Outside rush hour, Fort Worth is an easy 18–22 minutes and Las Colinas is 15–18 minutes. Budget an additional 10–15 minutes during rush hour if your commute follows the SH-183 corridor. DFW Airport is typically reachable in 12–15 minutes at any hour.
