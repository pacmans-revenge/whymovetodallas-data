Location & Commute

Where Is Waxahachie? Commutes & Getting Around

Waxahachie sits ~30 miles south of Downtown Dallas and about ~42 miles southeast of Fort Worth, anchoring Ellis County along the I-35E and US-287 corridors. The city straddles one of the DFW metro's most strategic southern access points — Interstate 35E flows directly north into Dallas's Central Business District, while US-287 connects west toward Fort Worth and the Midlothian industrial corridor. Neighboring Midlothian is just 15 minutes west, and Cedar Hill is roughly 20 minutes north.

The honest story on commuting: Waxahachie is not a close-in suburb. If your office is downtown Dallas or in the Legacy/Plano corridor, plan on 40–60 minutes each way depending on rush-hour I-35E conditions through south Dallas. What you trade in commute time, you typically get back in significantly more home, larger lots, and a quieter pace — a trade that increasingly works for remote workers, healthcare professionals employed locally, and families who commute 2–3 days per week.

No rail transit here. Waxahachie is not a DART member city, and there is no commuter rail connecting to Dallas. Ellis County Transit (ECTS) provides limited demand-response service for local trips, but a personal vehicle is non-negotiable for daily commuting. I-35E is the lifeline, and the Oak Cliff/I-30 interchange is the main bottleneck heading into downtown. If your employer is in the Las Colinas or Mid-Cities corridor along SH-183, your commute avoids downtown Dallas entirely — a genuine advantage from Waxahachie's position.
Education

Waxahachie ISD — Schools in Waxahachie

Waxahachie Independent School District serves roughly 11,000 students across 16 campuses and earns an A- from Niche for overall district quality, ranking #2 in Ellis County. The district's standout differentiators are its specialty magnet programs — including a STEAM Academy and a Biomedical Academy — that give motivated students access to focused career pathways that many larger north DFW ISDs don't offer at the elementary and middle school level. The TEA rated WISD a C (76/100) for 2024-25, reflecting average to above-average performance on state accountability metrics.

Best Neighborhoods in Waxahachie for Families

Waxahachie offers a rare mix in DFW: walkable Victorian-era streets near the historic downtown square, established suburban neighborhoods with mature trees and larger lots, and newer master-planned communities with resort amenities along the US-287 corridor. Most families land in the mid-$300s to mid-$500s range — prices that would be 20–40% higher for equivalent homes in north DFW.

A top choice for families relocating from California or the Northeast who want the feel of a planned community at a fraction of the Frisco price.

Waxahachie vs. Where You're Coming From

Waxahachie is one of the few remaining DFW suburbs where a family can buy a 4-bedroom, 2,500+ sq ft home on a decent lot for under $450,000. While it doesn't have the price-per-square-foot magic of rural Texas, it offers something genuinely rare: a metro-adjacent community with real Texas character, quality schools, and a price point that makes the no-income-tax math work powerfully for families relocating from California, New York, or Illinois.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Waxahachie

Youth Sports Run the Weekend Calendar

Waxahachie Youth Sports Association (WYSA) fields teams in soccer, baseball, and softball across all age groups. WISD competes at the 5A level in football, basketball, baseball, and track. Select clubs and private baseball academies operate locally — once kids are school-age, weekends fill fast.

Lake Waxahachie & Outdoor Life

The 690-acre Lake Waxahachie offers fishing, kayaking, and weekend boating — a waterfront amenity that most DFW suburbs at this price point simply don't have. Getzendaner Memorial Park's fishing pond, disc golf course, and open fields give families a go-to Saturday destination. The Hachie Greenbelt MTB trails attract riders from across Ellis County.

Historic Square & Local Dining

Downtown Waxahachie's courthouse square is lined with local restaurants, coffee shops, and boutiques in Victorian-era buildings — a genuine pedestrian center most north-DFW suburbs can't replicate. Chains cover everyday errands along Highway 287. A Costco opened in 2023, anchoring the newer retail corridor near I-35E and improving the weekly shopping picture considerably.

Scarborough Renaissance Festival

Every spring (late April through June) Waxahachie hosts one of the largest Renaissance festivals in the country — 35+ acres, 250,000+ annual visitors, jousting, artisans, and full cosplay culture. It draws families from across DFW and is a defining event on the local calendar. The Ellis County Fair each October is another all-ages tradition that roots the community.

Hollywood Has Filmed Here

Waxahachie's historic courthouse and Victorian streetscapes have appeared in more than 30 films — Tender Mercies, Places in the Heart, Leap of Faith, and The Trip to Bountiful among them. The city has an architectural identity that no amount of master-planning can manufacture. If character matters to your family, Waxahachie earns it authentically.

A Town, Not Just a Suburb

Waxahachie has its own downtown, its own identity, and a community that predates the DFW metro by generations. Families who move here often report faster social integration than in newer master-planned suburbs — block parties, local churches, WISD athletics, and downtown events create natural connection points from day one.

The honest tradeoff: Waxahachie is south of Dallas — not north. Families used to the Frisco / Allen / McKinney corridor will find the commute longer and the suburban infrastructure more modest. There's no DART rail, and nightlife is minimal. If you work in Plano or Frisco, the daily drive is 55–70 minutes. The trade-off is real home value and a community that doesn't feel factory-stamped.
Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & City Amenities

Waxahachie is car-dependent — there is no DART rail service, and Ellis County Transit (ECTS) provides only limited demand-response paratransit. A personal vehicle is essential for daily life. Where the city compensates is outdoor infrastructure: Lake Waxahachie, Getzendaner Memorial Park, Hachie Greenbelt MTB trails, and an expanding city trail network give families genuine outdoor access within city limits.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Waxahachie maintains more than 20 parks across the city, with Getzendaner Memorial Park and Lake Waxahachie as the flagship outdoor destinations. The Hachie Greenbelt trail network — still expanding — links green corridors across the northeast side of the city and connects into natural creek bottomland.

Getzendaner Memorial Park

The city's signature park features a fishing pond, disc golf course, lighted ball fields, playgrounds, covered pavilions, and walking paths. Well-maintained and genuinely welcoming — the go-to Saturday destination for families across Waxahachie.

Lake Waxahachie

A 690-acre reservoir with fishing access, boat ramps, and shoreline trails. Largemouth bass fishing is popular year-round. Kayak and canoe access make it a practical weekend retreat. The lake park includes picnic areas and shaded walking paths along the water's edge.

Hachie Greenbelt MTB Trails

Waxahachie Creek Greenbelt hosts one of the more developed mountain biking trail systems in Ellis County — natural-surface singletrack for intermediate and advanced riders. The trail network continues to expand. Also used for trail running and nature walking.

Lions Park

A neighborhood-scale park with youth baseball and softball diamonds, playgrounds, and shaded seating. Hosts many WYSA league games during spring and fall seasons. Located near established subdivisions on the city's west side.

Rogers Park & Recreation Center

Adjacent to the Waxahachie Recreation Center, Rogers Park includes basketball courts, open green space, and picnic facilities. The rec center here offers the city's main fitness and aquatic programming — see the facilities table below.

Waxahachie Creek Trail Network

A growing greenway along Waxahachie Creek connects parks and neighborhoods on the city's east side. Trail sections link Getzendaner Park with downstream green corridors. The city's parks masterplan calls for continued trail expansion through 2028.

Safety & Crime

Is Waxahachie Safe?

Waxahachie's crime profile reflects its character as a mid-sized Texas city rather than a wealthy DFW bedroom suburb. Violent crime is near the national average; property crime is the more notable concern. Families should evaluate safety by neighborhood rather than relying on citywide averages — established residential areas in north and west Waxahachie track meaningfully better than the city as a whole.

Violent Crime Rate

~3.9 per 1,000 residents — near the U.S. national average of ~4.0 per 1,000. Not an outsized concern in established family neighborhoods; context and specific street matter significantly.

Property Crime

~22 per 1,000 residents — above the U.S. average (~19). Auto theft and vehicle break-ins are the primary categories. Standard precautions reduce risk significantly in residential areas. Research specific subdivisions before purchasing.

Waxahachie Police Dept.

WPD operates community policing programs and a neighborhood watch network. School resource officers are deployed across WISD campuses. Ellis County Sheriff provides supplemental coverage for outlying areas and rural residential communities.

Sources: NeighborhoodScout · FBI UCR · Niche · City of Waxahachie. Statistics are citywide estimates and vary significantly by neighborhood. Always research specific streets and subdivisions before purchasing.

Versus Cedar Hill and Mansfield (both ~$350K–$430K), Waxahachie differentiates with historic character, lake access, and lower density — but has weaker school ratings. The honest summary: Waxahachie is right for remote workers, south-Dallas commuters, and families who prioritize authentic community and outdoor access over north-DFW suburb polish.
