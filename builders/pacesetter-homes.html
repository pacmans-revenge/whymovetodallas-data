Who Is Pacesetter Homes? Score 87 A

Pacesetter Homes Texas is the Lone Star State division of QUALICO, a Canadian homebuilder founded in 1951 that has spent over 70 years building homes across western Canada and the U.S. The Texas operation launched in 2007 out of Pflugerville — initially serving the greater Austin metro — before expanding into the Dallas–Fort Worth market in 2018. Builder magazine recognized the company as one of the top 10 fastest-growing homebuilders in the U.S. in 2017, the year before the DFW launch. DFW operations are run out of McKinney, with communities spread across Collin County and the outer growth corridor of Forney and Little Elm.

In DFW, Pacesetter focuses on production homes in the entry-to-move-up range — 1,277 to 3,503 square feet, priced from the mid-$250s to the upper-$600s depending on community, plan size, and lot type. The builder holds an A+ rating from the Better Business Bureau (Texas, McKinney profile, accredited) and a TrustBuilder score of 4.3 out of 5 based on 84 verified homeowner reviews in the Dallas market. Pacesetter is a smaller presence in DFW than the national volume builders, with 5 active communities in 5 suburbs as of the most recent ShowingNew data — which is worth understanding both as a limitation (fewer choices) and as a potential advantage (smaller, less-chaotic sales environments).

What Sets Pacesetter Apart

Pacesetter occupies a specific niche in the DFW builder landscape: an established brand with deep institutional history operating in growth-corridor suburbs where land is still affordable. Here's what materially distinguishes them from the national volume builders:

DesignQ Selection Experience

Pacesetter's Dallas Studio Series homes go through a selection process at DesignQ — a dedicated design studio where buyers work one-on-one with experienced interior design specialists in a VIP-style appointment. The session covers cabinetry, countertops, appliances, flooring, and plumbing fixtures with the goal of personalizing the home's interior within the builder's production framework. For families relocating from out of state, Pacesetter offers virtual design appointment options — a meaningful detail when you can't easily fly to McKinney every week during the build.

Live & Earn Program for Renters

Pacesetter's exclusive Live & Earn program converts rent payments into credit dollars that can be applied toward the purchase of a new Pacesetter home. For families currently renting in DFW or relocating before they're ready to buy, this program can meaningfully lower the effective cost at closing. Eligibility and credit amounts are community-specific — ask the sales team for current terms before assuming any particular dollar figure.

Structural Warranty via ProHome (Third-Party)

Pacesetter's 10-year structural warranty is administered by ProHome Warranty Management Services — an independent third-party warranty company rather than Pacesetter itself. This means your structural claim goes through a dedicated warranty management organization, which can make the process more procedural than dealing directly with the builder's own warranty department. The coverage period (10 years on structural) meets the Texas RCLA standard. Buyers should request the ProHome warranty documentation before closing and understand the claims process before they need it.

Pacesetter Homes Warranty — Meets Texas Legal Minimum

Texas law under the Residential Construction Liability Act (RCLA) sets minimum warranty floors for all new home builders: 1 year on workmanship, 2 years on mechanical and distribution systems, and 10 years on structural. Pacesetter Homes Texas meets but does not exceed these minimums. Families who want workmanship coverage longer than 12 months — for issues like trim gaps, drywall settling, or paint adhesion — should factor this into their builder comparison, as some regional builders extend workmanship to 2 years. The structural warranty is administered by ProHome Warranty Management Services, an independent third-party organization; file claims directly with ProHome rather than the builder's office for structural matters after your first year.

How Pacesetter Handles Warranty Repairs

During the first year, warranty service requests go through Pacesetter's own warranty department. The builder schedules vendors to complete repairs, but buyers in online reviews have noted that the process can be slow — particularly for follow-up visits when the initial repair is incomplete. Pacesetter does provide an online portal for submitting warranty requests. After year one for workmanship items, or after year two for systems, direct your claim to the relevant vendor or to ProHome (structural, years 1–10). Request the ProHome warranty booklet and claim instructions from your sales team at closing — don't wait until you have an issue to understand the process.

What the warranty doesn't cover

Standard exclusions apply: homeowner modifications or alterations, normal wear and tear, cosmetic damage not caused by defective workmanship, improper homeowner maintenance (e.g., not caulking around tubs annually), acts of nature, and appliances — which are covered separately under manufacturer warranties. The "distribution systems" coverage for year two is specifically plumbing, electrical, and HVAC distribution — not necessarily individual fixtures, appliances, or components. If a specific item matters to you (e.g., spray foam insulation vs. blown-in, brand of HVAC system), ask before signing the contract, not after.

Honest Assessment — What Families Love and What to Know

Pacesetter Homes scores a 87 (A) on our 100-point builder index. Here's what drives that score — and where relocating families should pay attention.

Strengths

  • A+ BBB rating, accredited — Pacesetter holds an A+ with the Better Business Bureau (Texas, McKinney profile), with 11 complaints filed over 3 years — a low count relative to their build volume and an indicator of generally resolved issues.
  • Attainable DFW pricing — with homes starting in the mid-$250s, Pacesetter serves families who are priced out of the $400k–$500k entry point that many Collin County builders now require. Princeton and Anna communities offer genuine starter-home options in solid school districts.
  • Dedicated design studio (DesignQ) — rather than a builder-run in-house selection center, Pacesetter's Dallas Studio Series buyers work with DesignQ, offering a more curated, VIP-style selection experience with professional interior design guidance. Virtual appointments are available for remote buyers.
  • Responsive sales process noted in reviews — multiple TrustBuilder reviewers cite the Pacesetter sales team as organized, communicative, and willing to walk buyers through the process step by step — a meaningful difference from larger-volume builders where sales staff may be overextended.
  • QUALICO institutional backing — as a division of QUALICO (70+ year history, western Canada's largest private homebuilder), Pacesetter has deeper financial stability than a typical local startup. The brand has survived multiple housing cycles and isn't at risk of a mid-build financial collapse.
  • Live & Earn renter program — unique credit-toward-purchase program for current renters that can meaningfully reduce out-of-pocket costs at closing. Relevant for families renting while they search.

Things to Know

  • Warranty meets Texas minimum — not more — the 1-year workmanship window is what Texas law requires, not a differentiator. Builders like Highland Homes and some regional competitors offer 2-year workmanship. If post-close finish issues (settling, paint adhesion, trim gaps) are a concern, this is worth comparing.
  • Warranty service process draws complaints — a recurring theme in post-close reviews is that getting warranty repairs scheduled and completed takes more effort than it should. Buyers report having to follow up repeatedly and sometimes manage vendor coordination themselves. This appears to be a customer service process issue rather than a refusal to honor coverage.
  • Small DFW footprint limits suburb choices — 5 active communities in 5 suburbs means Pacesetter simply doesn't build in most of the suburbs Kristen's relocation clients typically prioritize (Frisco, Flower Mound, Southlake, Plano, Allen, Coppell). If your target suburb is in that tier, Pacesetter isn't a factor.
  • TrustBuilder responsiveness score is lower — the 3.9/5 responsiveness score (vs. 4.3 overall) suggests the after-sale service experience lags the initial purchase experience. This is consistent with the warranty process feedback above and worth calibrating expectations against.

No current public promotions were listed on the Pacesetter Homes DFW site at time of research (May 2026). Pacesetter Homes Floor Plans & Design Process

Pacesetter's DFW floor plan library ranges from 1,277 to 3,503 square feet, across plans designed for 40-foot and 50-foot lots. The 40-foot lot series (Craftsman-style homes) runs from approximately 1,277 to 2,140 square feet and is the product line most commonly found in the Princeton and Anna communities. The 50-foot lot series runs from approximately 1,487 to 3,096 square feet. In Melissa and Little Elm, where lot sizes and community positioning trend toward move-up buyers, the available plans push closer to the top of the range. All plans are currently priced from the mid-$250s (Princeton) to the upper-$670s (Little Elm, Spiritas Ranch). Most plans offer one- and two-story configurations, with options for home offices, game rooms, and expanded primary suites that reflect the post-pandemic preference for flexible work-from-home space.

Production Plans with Design Center Selections

Pacesetter builds from a fixed production plan library — you choose from their available floor plans rather than designing custom modifications to the structural layout. What is customizable is the interior finish package: through DesignQ, buyers select from curated options across cabinets, countertops, flooring, tile, fixtures, and appliance packages. The breadth of choices at DesignQ is wider than what many entry-level volume builders offer, but it's still selection-within-categories rather than true custom design. Structural modifications (moving walls, adding windows, changing door swings) are generally not available in Pacesetter's production framework.

Design Center

Pacesetter uses DesignQ as their external design studio for Dallas-area buyers — this is a dedicated interior design firm rather than an in-house builder selection center. Buyers in the Dallas Studio Series work with DesignQ specialists in a VIP-style appointment format, which tends to feel more curated and less rushed than a large production builder's in-house warehouse-style selection process. For relocating families who can't be in DFW frequently during the build, Pacesetter offers virtual design appointment options — confirm availability for your specific community when you sign the contract.

Standard features included at base price

Standard features at base price include granite or quartz countertops in the kitchen (community-dependent), stainless steel appliances (refrigerator typically not included), vinyl plank or similar hard-surface flooring in main living areas, covered back patio, and a two-car garage. Energy efficiency features vary by community — ask specifically about insulation type (spray foam vs. blown-in), Low-E windows, and HVAC brand for the specific plan you're considering. These details matter for Texas utility bills and are worth confirming in writing before signing.

Pacesetter Homes DFW — Frequently Asked Questions

Is Pacesetter Homes a good builder?

Pacesetter Homes scores an 87 out of 100 (grade: A) on our DFW builder index, driven by an A+ BBB rating with accreditation, a TrustBuilder score of 4.3 out of 5 from 84 verified homeowner reviews, and competitive pricing for the Collin County outer corridor. The builder has QUALICO institutional backing — a 70-year-old Canadian homebuilder — which provides financial stability that smaller local builders can't match. The main caveat is that after-sale warranty service has drawn complaints about slow response times. Pacesetter is a solid choice for entry-to-move-up buyers targeting Anna, Melissa, Princeton, Forney, or Little Elm — it's not the right fit if your suburb priority is Frisco, Flower Mound, or Southlake, where Pacesetter doesn't currently build.

How much do Pacesetter Homes homes cost in DFW?

As of the most recent ShowingNew data, Pacesetter homes in DFW are priced from approximately $254,990 to $679,980. The lower end of that range is in Princeton (Town Park community), where 3-bedroom homes on 40-foot lots start in the mid-to-upper $250s. The upper end is in Little Elm (The Reserve at Spiritas Ranch), where larger plans push toward the $600s. Melissa's Meadow Run community sits in the $437k–$609k range. Pricing changes with build phase, available lots, and market conditions — always verify current pricing with the sales team or through the ShowingNew portal.

Where does Pacesetter Homes build in DFW?

As of the most recent ShowingNew data, Pacesetter Homes Texas has 5 active DFW communities across 5 suburbs: Walden Pond West in Forney, Leonard Trails in Anna, Town Park in Princeton, Meadow Run in Melissa, and The Reserve at Spiritas Ranch in Little Elm. Pacesetter's DFW footprint is concentrated in Collin County's outer growth corridor (Anna, Melissa, Princeton) and extends into Forney (east) and Little Elm (west). They do not currently build in Frisco, McKinney, Allen, Plano, Flower Mound, Southlake, Coppell, or Colleyville — suburbs where established lots are largely unavailable or priced out of Pacesetter's production range.

What is Pacesetter Homes' warranty?

Pacesetter Homes Texas offers a 1-2-10 warranty: 1 year on workmanship (finishes including drywall, paint, trim, cabinetry, and flooring), 2 years on mechanical distribution systems (plumbing, electrical, and HVAC distribution), and 10 years on structural components including the foundation. The structural warranty is administered by ProHome Warranty Management Services, an independent third-party warranty organization. This meets Texas RCLA minimums but does not exceed them — some competing builders offer 2-year workmanship. Request the ProHome warranty booklet and claim process documentation at closing, before you need it.

Do I need a Realtor to buy a Pacesetter Homes new construction?

You don't need one, but you should have one — and it costs you nothing extra. Pacesetter pays the buyer's agent commission; the builder's price is the same whether you bring a Realtor or not. The builder's sales agent works for Pacesetter, not for you. A buyer's agent reviews the contract, explains upgrade pricing relative to market value, advises on lot selection, recommends independent inspectors, and represents your interests at the pre-closing walkthrough. If you visit a model home without registering a Realtor on your first visit, you may permanently lose the ability to have that Realtor represent you for that community — so call Kristen at (602) 405-4115 before you walk in.

How long does Pacesetter Homes take to build a house in DFW?

Pacesetter hasn't published a DFW-specific build timeline publicly, but production builders in the Collin County outer corridor typically run 6 to 10 months from contract to close, depending on plan size, lot complexity, and the phase of construction when you sign. Spec homes (already under construction or completed) can close in 30–90 days. Custom-start homes where you select your lot and plan at contract start take longer. Supply chain delays have moderated since 2022–2023 but can still extend timelines for specific materials. Ask the sales team for the current community-specific estimate when you're in the contracting conversation — build time estimates can vary meaningfully between phases of the same community.

Does Pacesetter Homes have a preferred lender, and do I have to use them?

Pacesetter works with Service First Mortgage (SFMC) and Benchmark Mortgage as preferred lenders, and certain incentives (rate buydowns, closing cost credits) are typically tied to using one of these lenders. You are not legally required to use a preferred lender to purchase from Pacesetter — Texas law prohibits builders from making a sale contingent on using a specific lender. However, if you opt for an outside lender, you may forfeit any incentives tied to the preferred lender program. The right move is to get a rate quote from the preferred lender and at least one independent lender side by side, then compare the total cost of the deal — not just the interest rate. A good buyer's agent (like Kristen) can walk you through that comparison so you know whether the builder's incentive actually saves you money once you factor in the rate differential.
