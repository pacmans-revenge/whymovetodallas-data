Getting Your Bearings

Where Is Addison, Texas?

Addison is a compact town of 4.3 square miles tucked into Dallas County, sitting just north of Dallas proper along the Dallas North Tollway corridor. Bordered by Farmer's Branch to the north and west, and Dallas to the south and east, Addison punches far above its size. It's not a sprawling suburb — it's a dense, walkable urban village that happens to be surrounded by Dallas.

Its location is one of its best features. LBJ Freeway (I-635) forms the southern boundary, giving residents instant access to virtually every corner of DFW without driving through downtown. The Dallas North Tollway runs along the west edge. The result: you can reach most major DFW employment corridors in 20–30 minutes without ever touching a downtown interchange. For families coming from New York, Chicago, or San Francisco, this kind of centrality without urban traffic is a genuine revelation.

The DART Silver Line regional rail now connects Addison Transit Center to DFW Airport and Plano — a rare transit win in a metro built around the car.

Education

Schools Serving Addison, Texas

Addison is one of the few DFW towns served by two public school districts. Most of the town falls within Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD (CFBISD); the southern portion feeds into Dallas ISD. Neither district is a marquee A+ ISD — but both have improved meaningfully in recent years, and Addison's private school options are genuinely elite.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Addison

170+ Restaurants in 4 Square Miles

Addison has one of the highest restaurant-per-capita densities in the entire country. From Tex-Mex and sushi to steakhouses, farm-to-table, and James Beard-level chefs — this is where North Dallas comes to eat out. Your Friday night problem is choosing, not driving.

Kaboom Town & Community Events

Kaboom Town on July 4th draws 35,000+ people — one of the largest fireworks shows in Texas. Taste of Addison (food festival), Oktoberfest, and Addison Circle events run year-round. The town punches well above its size when it comes to community programming.

Addison Circle & Vitruvian Park

Two signature parks anchor daily life. Addison Circle Park (10+ acres) is the town square — farmers markets, outdoor yoga, and live music. Vitruvian Park (12+ acres) has a spring-fed creek trail and hosts the beloved Vitruvian Lights holiday display each November–December.

Cavanaugh Flight Museum

A unique Addison gem — one of the finest collections of vintage warbirds in the country, located at Addison Airport. Kids (and adults) can tour WWII-era aircraft up close and even arrange rides in flying warbirds. Nothing else like it in suburban DFW.

DART Silver Line — DFW Access Without a Car

The new Cotton Belt regional rail connects Addison Transit Center directly to DFW Airport and Plano. For frequent business travelers, families with one car, or anyone who misses city transit, this is a genuine lifestyle upgrade unavailable in most other North Dallas suburbs.

Young, Diverse, Easy to Connect

Median age 35, 40%+ non-white population, professionals from all over the world. Addison's social scene is notably more diverse and cosmopolitan than typical DFW suburbs. Newcomers from coastal cities often feel at home faster here than anywhere else in North Texas.

The honest tradeoff: Addison doesn't have youth sports leagues built into a master-planned community, a neighborhood pool, or a HOA-organized movie night. What it has instead is a town-wide social infrastructure built around restaurants, parks, and civic events. It's a different kind of community — one that rewards people who step outside their front door.
Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & City Amenities

Addison is more walkable than any other North Dallas suburb — but it is still not a pedestrian city. The Addison Circle core and Vitruvian Park area are legitimately walkable to restaurants, coffee shops, and green space. Beyond those nodes, a car is needed. What Addison does unusually well for DFW is public green space, civic events, and transit — three things most suburban towns completely lack.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Addison has 11+ parks across its 4.3 square miles — a higher park density than most DFW suburbs. Two signature destination parks anchor daily outdoor life. Trail connectivity with adjacent Dallas trail systems is expanding.

Addison Circle Park

10+ acres of open lawn, fountains, and a performance stage in the heart of town. Hosts Kaboom Town, Taste of Addison, Oktoberfest, and dozens of smaller events. The park connects directly to restaurants, retail, and residences — the closest thing to a true town square in DFW.

Vitruvian Park

A 12-acre destination park with a spring-fed creek, winding trails, public art installations, and a dramatic water feature. Famous for Vitruvian Lights (holiday season), live music at Vitruvian Nights (summer), and trail runs. Connected to regional trail network.

Beckert Park

Addison's primary athletic field complex — soccer fields, softball/baseball diamonds, and picnic areas. Home to recreational sports leagues and community sports programming. Good family-friendly green space with active programming.

Quorum Park & Addison Road Green

Quieter neighborhood parks with shade trees, benches, and open lawn. Less event-driven than Addison Circle, more suited to daily dog walks, morning runs, and weekend picnics. Several small pocket parks distributed through residential areas.

Regional Trail Connections

Vitruvian Park connects to the Campion Trail / Dallas trail network, allowing cyclists to travel south into Dallas proper. The DART Silver Line corridor adds pedestrian-accessible infrastructure. Trail network is expanding through NCTCOG regional planning.

Addison Airport (KADS)

Not a park — but the Addison Airport greenway and taxiway-adjacent land create an unusual open-sky character. Home to Cavanaugh Flight Museum. The airport's presence is part of Addison's unique urban fabric that no other DFW suburb shares.

Safety

Crime & Safety in Addison, Texas

Addison's overall crime statistics require honest context. The town's high commercial density — 170+ restaurants, dozens of hotels, and thousands of daily non-resident visitors — inflates its aggregate crime numbers significantly compared to purely residential suburbs. Property crime is the primary concern, particularly motor vehicle theft and parking lot break-ins concentrated in commercial corridors. Residential neighborhoods report much lower incident rates.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions About Addison, Texas

Addison is split between two public school districts. Most of the town falls within Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD (CFBISD), which serves the north and central areas. The southern portion of Addison — roughly below Spring Valley Road — is served by Dallas ISD. Both districts earned a TEA B rating in 2025, an improvement from C the prior year. Always verify your specific address before buying — the district boundary runs through the middle of town and can vary street by street.

Addison also hosts two elite private schools: Greenhill School (PreK–12, nationally ranked) and Trinity Christian Academy (K–12), both located within the town and rated A+ by Niche 2025.

Addison's aggregate crime statistics are elevated compared to pure suburban DFW communities, driven primarily by property crime in the commercial corridor — parking lot break-ins and motor vehicle theft near the restaurant and hotel districts inflate the numbers. Violent crime is moderate at roughly 4 per 1,000 residents, comparable to many DFW suburbs.

Residential neighborhoods within Addison grade significantly better than the aggregate. Families buying in residential pockets — especially in the Quorum/Midway area, Belt Line corridor, and south Addison — report day-to-day safety consistent with other North Dallas suburbs. Standard precautions: don't leave valuables visible in your car, use covered parking when available.

As of mid-2026, the median single-family home price in Addison is approximately $630,000, with homes typically spending around 50–55 days on market. The SFR market has moderated noticeably — months supply sits near 7.4, putting buyers in a stronger negotiating position than in recent years. Condos and townhomes — which make up the majority of Addison's for-sale housing — range from approximately $300,000 to $750,000 depending on location, size, and finishes.

Addison's detached SFR market is genuinely small — roughly 85 closings per year across the entire town. Well-priced homes in good CFBISD feeder zones still attract solid interest, so arriving pre-approved matters even in a buyer-friendly market.

In the Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD zone (most of Addison), the combined property tax rate is approximately $1.906 per $100 valuation — made up of the Town of Addison ($0.6081), CFBISD ($0.9836), Dallas County ($0.1952), and Dallas College ($0.1194). On a $630,000 home that works out to roughly $12,000–$12,100 per year before any homestead exemption.

Texas homeowners who occupy their home as their primary residence qualify for a homestead exemption that reduces the taxable value — saving several hundred dollars annually. Addison has no MUD or PID special district assessments, which is an advantage over many newer DFW suburban communities. Always verify current rates and your specific address's district at addisontx.gov.

The drive from central Addison to Downtown Dallas is typically 15–20 minutes via the Dallas North Tollway or Dallas Parkway. Addison sits only 14 miles north of downtown, and the DNT provides a direct, relatively uncongested route most of the day. Morning rush can add 5–10 minutes heading south.

Addison's central location also means excellent access in multiple directions: 25–30 minutes to DFW Airport via I-635 West, 20–25 minutes to Plano/Legacy West north on the DNT, 15–20 minutes to Richardson via Belt Line Road east, and just 5–10 minutes to Medical City Dallas. For dual-income households commuting in different directions, Addison's position is hard to beat.

For families buying homes in Addison, the best residential pockets are: Addison Circle ($380K–$650K, condos/townhomes, most walkable); Quorum/Midway Corridor ($450K–$720K, executive townhomes and SFR, CFBISD); Belt Line/Greenhill Corridor ($380K–$580K, established SFR, closest to private schools); Vitruvian Park Area ($420K–$700K, park-adjacent condos/townhomes); and South Addison/Spring Valley Pockets ($350K–$540K, value SFR with renovation upside, near Medical City).

ISD zoning varies by street — always verify your specific address with both CFBISD and Dallas ISD before making an offer. Homes in the Belt Line/Greenhill corridor are particularly desirable for families planning to use Greenhill School or Trinity Christian Academy.

Addison is best known for its dining scene — over 170 restaurants in 4.3 square miles, from James Beard-level chef tables to legendary Tex-Mex. The town hosts several major annual events: Kaboom Town (July 4th, 35,000+ attendees, one of Texas' largest fireworks shows), Taste of Addison (spring food festival), and Oktoberfest.

Year-round, residents enjoy Addison Circle Park (farmers markets, outdoor yoga, live music), Vitruvian Park (trail runs, Vitruvian Lights in winter, live music nights in summer), and the Cavanaugh Flight Museum at Addison Airport — one of the finest collections of vintage WWII warbirds in the country, with flight ride opportunities. The Mary Kay Museum at corporate HQ is also a surprisingly engaging free attraction.

Addison and Frisco are fundamentally different products. Frisco offers A+ schools (FISD), master-planned communities with resort amenities, large lots, and a family infrastructure built for youth sports. Addison offers walkability, dining, urban character, and central location — but B-rated public schools, limited SFR inventory, and no master-planned amenities.

Compared to Plano, Addison is smaller, more urban, and more walkable — but Plano ISD (TEA A, Niche A) is significantly stronger than CFBISD. Plano also has far more SFR inventory at similar or slightly higher prices.

The right choice depends on your priorities. If school district rating is the primary driver: Frisco, Plano, or Allen. If walkability, dining, and central location matter more than school zoning (e.g., you're planning private school, you have pre-K kids, or you're a dual-income couple): Addison is the strongest option in North Dallas.
