Location & Commute

Where Is Irving? Commutes & Getting Around

Irving sits about 15 miles northwest of Downtown Dallas and roughly 22 miles east of Downtown Fort Worth, straddling the geographic middle of the Metroplex in Dallas County. The city runs along SH-114, SH-183 (Airport Freeway), and SH-161, providing quick access to the most concentrated employment corridor in all of DFW — Las Colinas — along with DFW International Airport directly to the north.

For anyone whose job is in Las Colinas, at DFW Airport, or anywhere along the SH-114 corridor from Irving toward Southlake, this is simply the most convenient place to live in the entire Metroplex. The honest tradeoff: the northern DFW suburbs — Frisco, McKinney, Allen — are 40–55 minutes away. Irving is not the right base if that's where you work.

SH-114, I-635 (LBJ), and SH-183 all see significant rush-hour congestion — budget 30–40 minutes for peak-hour runs to Dallas even at 15 miles.
Education

Irving ISD — Schools in Irving, Texas

Irving ISD serves the entire city and holds a B- from Niche — below the north DFW suburban norm. The district's average conceals real campus-by-campus variation; the Las Colinas and Valley Ranch zones consistently produce above-average individual campuses. Families choosing Irving for its employment proximity and affordability typically research specific attendance zones carefully, and some opt for private school.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Irving

Toyota Music Factory & Entertainment

Irving's premier entertainment complex in Las Colinas — outdoor amphitheater, indoor arena, 20+ restaurants, and bars all within a walkable district. Concerts, comedy shows, and dining in one place. Exceptional by DFW suburban standards.

Lake Carolyn & Mandalay Canal

The urban waterfront at the heart of Las Colinas — a canal system and lake for kayaking, paddleboarding, and waterfront dining. The Gondola service on the canal is one of DFW's more unusual amenities. Families use it for evening walks and weekend recreation.

International & Diverse Dining Scene

Irving has one of DFW's most genuinely diverse restaurant landscapes — authentic Mexican, Indian, Vietnamese, Middle Eastern, and more, spread across the city. Families from California and the Northeast often find the food variety here a pleasant surprise compared to more homogeneous north suburbs.

Travel Convenience

No suburb in DFW gets you to DFW International Airport in under 15 minutes consistently. For families with members who travel frequently for work — or who fly back to visit family in California, New York, or Illinois — this is a day-to-day quality-of-life advantage that compounds over time.

Mustangs of Las Colinas

The iconic bronze mustang sculpture herd at Williams Square Plaza is one of DFW's most recognizable landmarks. An unusual and memorable piece of public art in the middle of a corporate campus — kids love it, and it anchors the Las Colinas urban identity.

Urban, Diverse, International

Irving is markedly more diverse and cosmopolitan than most DFW suburbs — Niche gives it an A+ for diversity. Families who come from urban environments and want a diverse community often find Irving a better cultural fit than the predominantly homogeneous north suburbs.

The honest tradeoff: Irving delivers urban amenities and employer proximity that most DFW suburbs can't match — but it doesn't have the neighborhood community feel that Frisco, McKinney, or Flower Mound deliver. The population is more transient, turnover is higher, and the suburb-wide school rating adds friction for families with kids. Irving is the right choice for the family that optimizes for commute, price, and urban lifestyle. It's the wrong choice for the family looking for that tight-knit, PTA-and-Friday-night-lights suburban community feel.
Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & City Amenities

Irving is the walkability exception in a car-dependent metro. The Las Colinas urban core has genuine DART rail access and a Walk Score of 80 — among the highest in any DFW suburb. Outside the urban core, Irving remains car-dependent for daily errands. The city compensates with Campion Trail, one of the best urban trail systems in DFW, and Lake Carolyn's urban waterfront.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Irving maintains over 55 parks and more than 26 miles of trails, anchored by Campion Trail along the Elm Fork of the Trinity River — one of DFW's premier urban trail systems. Lake Carolyn adds urban waterfront access unique in the suburban landscape.

Campion Trail

Over 12.5 miles of paved trail along the Elm Fork of the Trinity River, connecting Irving to the broader DFW trail network. Popular for running, cycling, and dog walking. Families use it heavily on weekends — one of DFW's most underrated urban trails.

Lake Carolyn & Mandalay Canal

Urban lake and canal system at the heart of Las Colinas — kayaking, paddleboarding, walking paths, and waterfront dining. Gondola rides on the canal are a genuine DFW novelty. The most urban outdoor recreation setting in any DFW suburb.

Mustang Park Recreation Center

Irving's newest recreation facility — full fitness center, indoor aquatic center, gymnasium, and multi-purpose studios. City-operated with family membership options. Located in far northwest Irving near Valley Ranch.

Arden Lake Park

A neighborhood park with a fishing lake in the northern part of Irving — popular with families for fishing, picnicking, and casual outdoor recreation. Less known than Campion Trail but a solid local option.

Heritage Park

A community anchor in Irving's historic district — green space, events venue, and gathering point for the Heritage District neighborhood. Used for local festivals, farmers markets, and community programming throughout the year.

Andrew Brown Park (nearby Coppell)

Just across the Irving–Coppell border, Andrew Brown Park offers expansive green space, sports fields, and walking trails along Denton Creek. Irving families near Valley Ranch and the northern neighborhoods use it regularly.

Safety & Crime

Is Irving, Texas Safe?

Irving's crime rate is higher than the north DFW suburban average — Niche gives the city a C+ for crime and safety, reflecting real variance across zip codes. The Las Colinas urban core and Valley Ranch typically record lower crime figures than the city-wide average, which is pulled up by higher-crime commercial and industrial zones in other parts of Irving. Families choosing Irving should research their specific neighborhood's crime data, not just the city-wide number.

Violent Crime Rate

~3.5 per 1,000 residents — above the DFW north-suburb average but below the national average of ~4.0/1,000. Higher in some eastern zip codes; lower in Las Colinas and Valley Ranch zones.

Property Crime

~28 per 1,000 residents — moderately above state and national averages. Auto theft and property crime tend to be more concentrated near commercial corridors. Residential neighborhoods in Las Colinas and Valley Ranch track lower.

Irving PD

Irving Police Department operates community policing initiatives and a neighborhood watch program. Crime data is published quarterly. Las Colinas area is actively patrolled given the corporate campus density and event venue traffic from Toyota Music Factory.

Common Questions

Irving, Texas — FAQ for Relocating Families

Yes — Las Colinas is a planned business and residential district located entirely within Irving, Texas. It is not a separate city; it's a development within Irving's city limits. Las Colinas includes the Toyota Music Factory entertainment complex, Lake Carolyn, the Mandalay Canal, and numerous Fortune 500 corporate campuses.

Las Colinas is known for its concentration of major corporate headquarters (ExxonMobil, Nokia, Kimberly-Clark, Celanese, McKesson, 7-Eleven, Michaels, and many others), the Toyota Music Factory entertainment complex, Lake Carolyn and the Mandalay Canal, the Mustangs of Las Colinas sculpture, and DART Orange Line rail access to Downtown Dallas. It's one of the most significant corporate campus districts in the United States.

Irving ISD holds a B- from Niche (2025) and a B (Meets Standard) from TEA. The district average conceals meaningful campus variation — schools in the Las Colinas and Valley Ranch zones consistently score above the district mean, while other areas perform closer to the average. Families should verify their specific address against the Irving ISD boundary map and research individual campus ratings. Private school options including Cistercian Preparatory School are within driving range for families who prioritize academics.

Irving's city-wide crime rate is higher than the north DFW suburban average — Niche gives it a C+ for safety. Violent crime runs approximately 3.5 per 1,000 residents, below the national average of ~4.0 but above comparable suburbs like Coppell or Flower Mound. Safety varies significantly by neighborhood: Las Colinas and Valley Ranch track lower, while some eastern and central Irving zip codes pull the city average up. Research crime data for the specific neighborhood you're considering.

The median single-family home price in Irving is approximately $383K (NTREIS, 2024–2025 rolling). This is the most affordable median among Dallas County's primary family-oriented suburbs. The market is balanced at roughly 3.9 months supply. Las Colinas condos and townhomes offer additional options; Hackberry Creek gated homes reach $400K–$700K+.

Las Colinas houses dozens of major corporate headquarters and campuses including ExxonMobil, Nokia (North America HQ), Kimberly-Clark (HQ), Celanese (HQ), Fluor Corporation (HQ), McKesson Corporation (HQ), Michaels Companies (HQ), 7-Eleven (HQ), and many more. The corridor is one of the most concentrated Fortune 500 and Fortune 1000 employment hubs in the United States.

Irving is approximately 15 miles northwest of Downtown Dallas. Drive time is typically 15–25 minutes without heavy traffic, via SH-114 East or I-35E. DART Orange Line trains also connect Las Colinas to Downtown Dallas. During peak rush hour (7–9 AM and 4:30–7 PM), budget 30–40 minutes for the drive.

Irving is approximately 10 miles from DFW International Airport, with a typical drive time of 10–15 minutes via SH-114 or Belt Line Road. This is the shortest airport commute of any significant DFW suburb — a meaningful quality-of-life advantage for families with frequent business travelers or members who fly back to visit family regularly.

The top neighborhoods for families in Irving are: Las Colinas Urban Center ($380K–$650K, walkable, urban amenities, DART access), Valley Ranch ($350K–$580K, mature master-planned community, large lots, canals), Hackberry Creek ($400K–$700K, gated, golf course, private), and Heritage District ($320K–$500K, historic, entry-level, arts scene). School zones vary by neighborhood — always verify attendance zones before committing to a specific home.

Irving is a good fit for families who prioritize employer proximity (Las Colinas, DFW Airport corridor), affordability, and urban amenities — and are willing to research school zones carefully or budget for private school. It's less well-suited for families who prioritize high-rated public schools across the board or want a tight-knit, community-oriented suburban character. Niche gives Irving an A- for families overall and an A+ for diversity, but a C+ for crime. The right family finds great value here; the wrong fit learns it from the ISD rating too late.
