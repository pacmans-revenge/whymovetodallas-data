Moving to Allen, Texas: What You Need to Know

Kristen Carpentier Licensed TX Realtor®  ·  Updated May 30, 2026  ·  10 min read

Allen doesn't get the headlines that Frisco does. It's not the suburb that shows up in every "fastest growing" list or gets a new football stadium every few years. What Allen has instead is something more useful for a relocating family: a proven track record. Excellent schools, a livable price point, strong infrastructure, and a community that's been doing the family suburb thing well for two decades without the growing pains that come with building a city from scratch.

If you're researching a move to North Dallas and Allen keeps coming up on your list, here's everything you actually need to know — the schools, the real estate market, what the commute looks like, and the honest tradeoffs that most relocation guides skip.

One thing I hear regularly from families comparing suburbs: "Allen feels like Alpharetta." If you're relocating from the Atlanta metro — Alpharetta, Johns Creek, Marietta — that comparison is apt. Both are upscale, master-planned family suburbs anchored by excellent public schools, strong retail corridors, and that well-established, professionally-run community feel. The key difference: Allen's median home price is significantly lower than Atlanta's top suburbs, and the Texas tax math (no state income tax) tends to favor families at nearly every income level. Several families I've worked with in the past year have made exactly that move.

Where Allen Is — and Why It Matters for Your Commute

Allen sits in Collin County, about 25 miles northeast of downtown Dallas. US-75 (Central Expressway) runs straight through the city — it's the main artery that connects Allen south to Plano, Richardson, and eventually downtown Dallas. SH-121 crosses the city's southern edge and links westward toward Frisco and the Dallas North Tollway corridor.

For families where one or both spouses work in Plano or the Legacy West/Legacy Drive corridor, Allen is genuinely close — typically 15 to 20 minutes without traffic, 25 to 30 during rush hour. That's a meaningful advantage over suburbs further north. The commute to downtown Dallas runs 35 to 50 minutes in typical morning traffic, which is real but manageable for most people.

Allen is also well-positioned relative to DFW International Airport — about 30 to 40 minutes via SH-121. For families with one frequent flyer, that matters. The major employer corridors within Allen and immediately adjacent include the State Farm campus (one of Allen's largest employers), the Watters Creek development, and the broader US-75 tech and finance corridor that runs through Plano and Richardson.

Allen ISD — What the A+ Actually Means for Your Kids

Allen ISD earns an A+ from Niche across every category that matters to families: overall, college prep, and good for families. Academics earns an A. This is not a case of a strong headline grade masking weaker underlying data — the district genuinely performs at the top of Collin County.

One structural advantage Allen has over some larger suburban ISDs: it's a single district. Every Allen address feeds Allen ISD — there's no split between two districts depending on which side of a boundary you land on, no anxiety about which ISD your specific street falls in. That consistency simplifies the homebuying decision considerably.

Allen High School is the flagship campus and is worth understanding. It's one of the largest high schools in Texas — enrollment over 6,000 students — with a correspondingly wide range of programs: AP, IB, CTE pathways, nationally competitive athletics, and performing arts. For some families, the scale is a selling point (resources, breadth of opportunity). For others, a 6,000-student high school feels overwhelming, and they'd prefer the smaller campuses of a district like Aledo ISD or Carroll ISD in Southlake. Neither preference is wrong — it's a genuine lifestyle question worth thinking through before you buy.

What Homes Cost in Allen Right Now

The median single-family home price in Allen is approximately $520,000, with homes typically going under contract in about 33 days. Months of supply sits around 3.4 — a balanced market that tilts slightly toward sellers on well-priced homes. That means buyers have some negotiating room but can't wait forever on a home they want.

For the $520K median, Allen buyers are typically getting a 4-bedroom home in an established neighborhood with good schools, a two-car garage, and a manageable yard. The entry point for Allen is roughly $380K for older or smaller homes; the upper end of the established neighborhoods runs $700K–$900K for larger homes on premium lots.

The Out-of-State Perspective

A family I worked with recently relocated from the San Jose area. Their previous home — 3 bedrooms, 1,800 square feet — had sold for $1.4M. In Allen, that same budget would have bought a 5-bedroom home in a top-rated school district with a three-car garage and a pool. They bought at $610K and felt like they were stealing it. That's the recalibration that happens for most families coming from coastal markets.

Property taxes in Allen run a combined rate of approximately 1.75–1.95% (Collin County, Allen ISD, city, community college). On a $520K home, expect roughly $9,000–$10,000 per year before the homestead exemption. Texas's homestead exemption reduces the school district taxable value by $100,000 (per TX Tax Code §11.13(b), effective 2023), so effective annual bills on a primary residence typically land in the $7,500–$9,000 range. Texas has no state income tax — for most households above $100K annual income, that difference offsets most or all of the property tax premium compared to their home state. For families moving from California, Illinois, or Georgia, the combined savings are often substantial.

What Life Actually Looks Like in Allen

Allen is a fully built-out suburb — which is a feature, not a bug, for families who don't want to live next to a construction site for five years. The infrastructure is established: parks, roads, retail, and community facilities are in place and functioning. The city maintains 127 parks and an extensive trail network. The Allen Event Center hosts concerts and minor league hockey. Watters Creek is a walkable outdoor shopping and dining district that gives Allen something most suburbs in its price range don't have: a genuine gathering place.

The youth sports culture in Allen is serious. Allen ISD's football program is one of the most prominent in the state — Allen High School plays in one of the largest high school stadiums in the country. If your kids are athletes, Allen's programs are well-funded and competitive. If sports aren't your family's priority, the same parks and recreation infrastructure that supports athletics also supports everything else: aquatic centers, youth leagues for non-school sports, and community programming.

Shopping and dining options are strong — Allen scores in the top 15% of DFW suburbs for shopping density, anchored by Allen Premium Outlets and the retail corridor along US-75. For restaurants, the selection is good but leans toward chain and fast-casual. The independent dining scene is thin compared to what you'd find in Plano's Legacy West or in Dallas proper — families who prioritize culinary variety often make a short drive to Plano for that.

The Honest Tradeoffs

Allen earns a Walk Score of 67 — "Somewhat Walkable" — which in practice means most destinations require a car. There's no rail service. The bike infrastructure has improved meaningfully in recent years and scores 68, but this is still a car-first suburb. For families coming from walkable urban neighborhoods, the adjustment is real.

The commute grade from Niche is B-, reflecting that US-75 during peak hours is legitimately congested — particularly the stretch between Allen and Plano. It's not the worst commute in DFW, but anyone driving southbound on 75 between 7:30 and 9:00 a.m. will encounter consistent slowdowns. Families whose employers are on the east or west side of the metro (rather than directly south on 75) may find the commute less favorable than the map suggests.

Outdoor recreation is Allen's relative weakness — Niche grades it B-. There are parks and trails, but no lakes within the city, no significant natural areas, and the outdoor experience is fundamentally suburban rather than nature-oriented. Families who want lake access or more expansive green space typically look at Rockwall on Lake Ray Hubbard or Flower Mound near Grapevine Lake.

Kristen's Take

Allen is the kind of suburb that rarely disappoints families once they move in — the problems are mostly visible from the outside and manageable once you're there. The commute is real but predictable. The lack of walkability is the norm across DFW, not specific to Allen. What you're getting in exchange — one of the strongest ISDs in Collin County, a fully built-out community, and $160K less than Frisco's median — is a strong trade for most families I work with.

Choose Allen if you want proven A+ schools, strong retail access, and a more affordable entry point than Frisco — and your employer is on or near the US-75 corridor. Allen's smaller population means it feels slightly less overwhelming than Frisco or McKinney while still having all the amenities a family needs.

Choose Frisco if access to entertainment, The Star, and the broader DNT corridor is important to you, or if you specifically want the most developed suburb in North Dallas regardless of the premium. Frisco is bigger, busier, and more expensive — but the infrastructure investment shows.

Choose McKinney if you want more land, a charming historic downtown, and a slightly quieter suburban feel. McKinney's north side has newer master-planned communities at a median around $496K — slightly below Allen — with more space per dollar. The commute from McKinney to Plano and Dallas is slightly longer but still manageable.

Allen Is Probably the Right Move If...
  • Top school ratings are your priority and you don't want to pay Frisco prices to get them
  • Your employer is in Plano, Richardson, or along the US-75 / Central Expressway corridor
  • You want an established community with infrastructure in place — not a suburb still being built
  • Allen Premium Outlets and Watters Creek cover your retail and dining needs
  • Your budget is $420K–$700K and you want the best value-to-school-quality ratio in Collin County

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Allen, Texas a good place to raise a family?

Yes — Allen earns an A+ overall from Niche with top marks for families, public schools, and jobs. Allen ISD is one of the strongest districts in Collin County, the city is safe (Niche B crime grade), and 42% of households have children. It consistently ranks among the best family suburbs in North Texas.

How much does it cost to buy a home in Allen, Texas?

The median single-family home price in Allen is approximately $520K, with homes selling in about 33 days. For families relocating from California, New York, Georgia, or Illinois, Allen typically offers significantly more space for the money — a $520K home in Allen is usually a 4-bedroom with a yard and a two-car garage, versus a fraction of that square footage in Alpharetta GA ($700K+) or most coastal markets.

How are Allen ISD schools?

Allen ISD earns an A+ from Niche for overall rating, college prep, and good for families — with an A for academics. The district is single-ISD (every Allen address feeds the same district), and Allen High School is one of the most resource-rich high schools in Texas with strong AP, IB, and CTE programs.

What is the commute like from Allen, Texas to Dallas?

Allen is about 25 miles northeast of downtown Dallas, with a rush-hour drive of 35–50 minutes via US-75. The commute to Plano and Legacy West is 15–20 minutes. Allen earns a B- for commute from Niche, reflecting that US-75 congestion is real during peak hours — but manageable for most families whose employers are on the 75 corridor.

Is Allen, Texas safe?

Allen earns a B crime grade from Niche, placing it in the safer half of DFW suburbs. Violent crime rates are well below the national average. Property crime is the more common concern — typical for any suburb with significant retail development. Family neighborhoods in Allen are genuinely safe and quiet.

How does Allen compare to Frisco or McKinney?

Allen is more affordable than Frisco ($520K vs $681K median) with comparable A+ school quality. McKinney's median is slightly lower (~$496K) with more land and newer construction on its north side; Allen is more established with better retail. Frisco has more entertainment and the largest employer campus footprint in North Dallas. Allen is the value-and-schools middle ground in North Collin County — strongest option if your employer is on the US-75 corridor.
