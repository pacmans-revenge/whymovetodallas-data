Is Irving, Texas a Good Place to Live?

By Kristen Carpentier · May 2026 · 8 min read

The short answer is yes — but it depends entirely on what you're looking for. Irving is one of the most misunderstood cities in the Dallas–Fort Worth metro. It's not a typical north-DFW master-planned suburb, and families who arrive expecting Frisco or Flower Mound usually end up surprised — sometimes pleasantly, sometimes not. What Irving actually is: a dense, diverse, walkable-by-DFW-standards city with a world-class corporate hub in Las Colinas, a genuinely affordable housing market, and a few honest tradeoffs that every relocating family should understand before they sign anything.

I've worked with families who fell in love with Irving the moment they saw Las Colinas — the lake, the restaurants, the walkability — and others who toured it expecting Southlake and left disappointed. Neither reaction was wrong. They were just looking for different things. This guide gives you the honest picture.

What Irving Actually Is

Irving is a city of 255,000 people in Dallas County, sitting squarely between downtown Dallas and DFW International Airport. That geography is everything. It means the city has grown up as a major employment center — not a bedroom community — and that shapes the character of the place in ways that matter to families.

The crown jewel is Las Colinas, a 12,000-acre master-planned urban center within Irving that looks nothing like the rest of the city. Lake Carolyn, the Toyota Music Factory, Water Street's restaurants and bars, Campion Trail (26 miles of paved trail), and one of the densest concentrations of Fortune 500 offices in Texas — it's genuinely impressive. ExxonMobil, Kimberly-Clark, Fluor, Celanese, La Quinta Holdings, Michaels Companies, and Christus Health all have significant operations here. If you're transferring to this corridor, living in Irving means you might actually be able to skip the highway commute entirely.

Outside Las Colinas, Irving is a more urban, diverse city — older housing stock, established neighborhoods, and a demographic mix that reflects its history as a gateway city for DFW. Walk Score of 80 (the highest of any major suburb in our dataset) tells you something real about the density of services and walkability here, especially near Las Colinas and Heritage District.

Location & Commutes

Irving's location is genuinely hard to beat on paper. You're 15 miles from downtown Dallas, 10 minutes from DFW Airport, and in the middle of the Las Colinas corporate corridor. For the family where one or both parents travel frequently for work, or where a spouse was just hired at a Las Colinas-area employer, the drive times are legitimately short.

The caveat is I-635 (LBJ Freeway) traffic during rush hour — it's real and it matters. The Las Colinas area sits in the convergence zone of several major highways, and morning and evening backups on SH-183 east toward Dallas can add meaningful time. DART's Orange Line runs through Las Colinas with stations at Las Colinas Urban Center and Irving Convention Center, which is genuinely useful if you're heading downtown and want to skip the highway entirely.

Schools & Irving ISD

Irving ISD earns a B- overall from Niche, with a B+ college prep grade and A- for families. The academics grade of C+ reflects variability across campuses — some schools perform well above the district average, while others serve higher-needs populations. This is a common pattern in larger urban ISDs, and it means the specific school your kids would attend matters more than the district average.

Cost of Living — Irving's Strongest Card

This is where Irving genuinely stands out. A median single-family home price of ~$383K puts Irving in a completely different price tier from its neighbors — and that gap is enormous for families relocating from California, New York, or Illinois.

For a family relocating from the Bay Area or Southern California, the sticker shock of Texas property taxes is real — Irving sits in Dallas County at a combined rate of roughly 1.9–2.0% of assessed value. On a $383K home that's about $7,300–$7,600 per year. No California Prop 13 protection here, but also no state income tax — and the monthly mortgage payment on a $383K home at current rates is dramatically lower than anything comparable in their origin market.

Safety & Crime — The Honest Conversation

Irving's Niche crime grade is C, which reflects crime rates that are meaningfully higher than the north DFW suburbs families often compare it to. This is one of the most common questions I get from families evaluating Irving, and I'd rather you hear it directly from me than discover it after you've made an offer.

Context matters here. Irving is a large city of 255,000 people — crime statistics for the whole city include areas that most relocating families would never consider living in. Neighborhoods like Valley Ranch and Las Colinas have notably lower crime profiles than the city average. When you're evaluating Irving, you're really evaluating the specific neighborhood, not the city as a whole. That's different from how you'd evaluate Flower Mound or Frisco, where the entire city tends to be consistently safe.

What This Means for Your Search

If safety is your top priority and you want the peace of mind of consistently low crime city-wide, Irving is not the right fit — and I'd steer you toward Flower Mound, Coppell, or Allen. If you're open to doing neighborhood-level research and focusing your search on Las Colinas or Valley Ranch specifically, the picture is meaningfully different from the city average.

Valley Ranch is the neighborhood families most consistently ask me about. It was developed in the 1980s around a network of canals, and it has aged gracefully — mature landscaping, a tight community feel, and access to some of Irving ISD's stronger campuses. Las Colinas suits families who genuinely want walkable urban living; it's unlike anything else in DFW at this price point.

Walkability & Lifestyle — Irving's Surprise Advantage

A Walk Score of 80 (Very Walkable) is the highest of any major suburb we track in DFW — and it's not just a number. The Las Colinas area in particular has restaurants, shops, and amenities within walkable distance of the residential towers and corporate campuses. Campion Trail offers 26 miles of paved multi-use trail along the Elm Fork of the Trinity River — one of the best urban trail systems in DFW. DART's Orange Line means you can reach downtown Dallas without ever touching I-35.

Irving's Niche grades reflect the lifestyle reality: A- for nightlife, A- for families, A+ for diversity. This is a city with genuine cultural richness — over 100 nationalities represented, outstanding international dining options, and the Toyota Music Factory bringing major concert and event programming. For families relocating from diverse metro areas like Los Angeles, Chicago, or New York, Irving often feels more comfortable and familiar than the more homogeneous north suburbs.

Frequently Asked Questions About Living in Irving, Texas

Is Irving, Texas safe?
Irving has a Niche crime grade of C, which is below average compared to the safest DFW suburbs like Flower Mound or Coppell. Crime rates are higher than the north DFW suburbs but vary significantly by neighborhood — Las Colinas and Valley Ranch are notably safer than other parts of the city. Always research the specific neighborhood, not just the city average.
Is Irving ISD a good school district?
Irving ISD earns a B- overall from Niche, with a B+ college prep grade and A- for families. It's a solid district with strong individual campuses and dual-language programs, but families prioritizing top-tier ratings typically look at nearby Coppell ISD (A+) — at significantly higher home prices.
What is Las Colinas, and is it part of Irving?
Yes — Las Colinas is a master-planned urban center located entirely within Irving, Texas. It's home to one of DFW's densest corporate corridors (ExxonMobil, Kimberly-Clark, Fluor), Lake Carolyn, the Toyota Music Factory, and Water Street. Living in Las Colinas means living in Irving.
How far is Irving from downtown Dallas?
Irving is about 15 miles from downtown Dallas — roughly 20 minutes via I-35E or SH-183 without traffic. DART's Orange Line also connects Las Colinas directly to downtown Dallas, making it one of the few DFW suburbs with genuine rail transit access to the urban core.
Is Irving, Texas affordable compared to other DFW suburbs?
Yes — Irving's median single-family home price is around $383K, compared to $610K+ in Coppell and $560K+ in Plano. That's a significant gap, especially for corporate transferees working in the Las Colinas corridor who want a short commute without paying north-suburb prices.
What are the best neighborhoods in Irving for families?
Las Colinas and Valley Ranch consistently rank as Irving's most family-friendly neighborhoods. Valley Ranch is a mature, tree-lined community with strong curb appeal and good campus access. Las Colinas suits families who want walkability and urban amenities. Bear Creek and MacArthur Park offer more affordable entry points into Irving.

Irving won't be right for every family — but for the right family, it's genuinely underrated. The Las Colinas corridor is one of the most impressive corporate and lifestyle environments in DFW, the price point leaves real budget for life outside the mortgage, and the diversity and walkability are things you simply don't get in Frisco or Flower Mound. If you're weighing Irving against other suburbs and want to talk through your specific situation, I'm happy to help.
