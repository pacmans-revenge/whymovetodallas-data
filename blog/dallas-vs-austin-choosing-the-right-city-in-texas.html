Dallas vs. Austin: Which Texas City Is Actually Better for Families?

Kristen Carpentier Licensed TX Realtor®  ·  Updated May 30, 2026  ·  9 min read

Dallas versus Austin is the most common comparison I field — from families moving to Texas from out of state, and increasingly from Texans in Austin itself who are weighing a DFW move as Austin's cost of living has caught up with them. The two cities couldn't be more different from each other. One is a sprawling corporate metro with deep suburban roots and some of the best school districts in the country. The other is a smaller, cooler university city with a real tech scene and a traffic problem that Texans joke about with a grimace.

I've lived in DFW for over 12 years and raised four kids here. I help families make this exact choice constantly. Here's the honest version — not the glossy relocation brochure version. If you're leaning toward Austin, I want you to make that call with your eyes open. And if you're leaning toward DFW, I'll tell you where the real value is.

Home Prices & Affordability: Austin Has Been Correcting

Austin's housing market went through the most dramatic run-up and correction of any major Texas metro. During COVID, Austin became the epicenter of remote-work migration — prices peaked in 2022, then corrected. Austin's metro median fell to $415,300 in Q1 2026 (Austin Board of Realtors) — down 3.4% year over year — while the Austin city proper median sits closer to $530,000. Homes are averaging well over 60 days on market. It's a buyer's market, and it has been for a while.

Dallas's market has been far more stable. The Dallas city SFR median is approximately $492,000 (NTREIS May 2026), but the more useful comparison for relocating families is the suburb level. Frisco and Plano run $580,000–$692,000. Allen and McKinney come in at $535,000–$580,000. Prosper and Celina offer newer construction at slightly lower price points.

Schools: DFW Has the Deeper Bench

Austin has good suburban school districts — Round Rock ISD, Lake Travis ISD, and Leander ISD are all solid choices for families who settle in the northwest suburbs. But Austin ISD itself, which covers much of the city proper, is more uneven, and the range between top and bottom campuses in the metro is wider than what you'd find in DFW's north suburbs.

DFW is in a different category. Frisco ISD earns an A+ from Niche. Plano ISD, Allen ISD, and Highland Park ISD are consistently among the top-ranked districts in Texas. DFW holds 8 of the state's top 20 school districts — and critically, those top districts are spread across multiple suburban cities that families can actually afford. You're not paying Highland Park prices to access Highland Park schools. In Frisco, Allen, McKinney, and Prosper, you're getting top-tier districts at a variety of price points.

Kristen's Take

A lot of the families I work with ask me about Austin — they've heard it's the "cool" Texas city. And it is. But when I ask them to rank their priorities — schools, commute, neighborhood stability, space — DFW wins every time for families with kids under 16. Austin's suburban districts are solid, but North DFW has more of them, they cover a wider geographic area, and you can access them at a real range of price points. That's a different conversation entirely.

Jobs: Two Very Different Economies

This is where Austin genuinely wins for certain families. If you work in tech — and particularly if your employer is Apple, NVIDIA, Amazon, Google, Meta, Oracle, Tesla, or Adobe — Austin has become a genuine Silicon Hills. Approximately 16% of Austin jobs are tech-related, far above the national average. These are not IT-support roles at traditional companies; they're core engineering, product, and research positions at major tech firms.

For most non-tech careers — finance, healthcare administration, logistics, corporate management, marketing, law — DFW has the advantage. Dallas's economy is simply more diversified, which also means it's more resilient to sector-specific downturns. Austin's economy rode the tech wave up and felt it more sharply when tech layoffs hit in 2022–2023.

Traffic: Austin's Most Underestimated Problem

I want to be direct here because this is the thing that surprises transplants most: Austin's traffic is genuinely brutal, and it has gotten worse every year for over a decade. I-35 through Austin is one of the worst urban highway corridors in the United States — it's a four-lane interstate handling traffic volumes it was never designed for, running through the middle of a city that's grown by over 700,000 people without proportional highway expansion.

MoPac (Loop 1) isn't much better. For families living in the northwest suburbs — Round Rock, Cedar Park, Leander — a commute to downtown Austin or the tech campuses in the northwest can easily run 45–60 minutes each direction during peak hours. Austin's Project Connect light rail finally received federal approval in early 2026, with construction contracts being awarded through the year — but the system isn't expected to open until 2033. That's seven more years of I-35.

Dallas has real traffic too — I-35E, the DNT, and LBJ Freeway can all be congested. But DART's 90-mile light rail network gives downtown commuters a genuine alternative, and DFW's suburban layout means most North Texas families drive suburb-to-suburb rather than suburb-to-core. The commute patterns are more distributed and often more manageable.

Property Taxes & Cost of Living

Both cities are in Texas — no state income tax for either. That's the headline. Dallas County runs a combined property tax rate of approximately 2.22%, while Travis County (Austin) averages around 1.95–2.1%. The rates are comparable, but because Austin home prices at the city level are higher, your dollar tax bill on a $530K Austin home can exceed what you'd pay on a $490K Dallas home at a slightly higher rate.

Overall cost of living in DFW runs approximately 7% below the national average, while Austin has climbed to near or slightly above the national average after years of rapid growth. Groceries, utilities, and dining are generally cheaper in DFW than in Austin's core. If you're moving from California, both cities are dramatically more affordable on housing and state taxes — but DFW is the more affordable of the two for most household budgets.

Lifestyle & Culture: Two Very Different Texases

This is the one area where personal preference genuinely matters and data can't settle the debate. Austin has a deeply embedded culture around live music, outdoor recreation, and a liberal-leaning university-town identity. If Barton Springs, Sixth Street, the Greenbelt, and SXSW are the kind of weekend activities that light up your family, Austin may feel more like home — especially for families coming from Portland, Denver, or the Bay Area.

Dallas has a different identity: more corporate, more suburban, more cosmopolitan. The dining scene is outstanding — DFW has as many excellent restaurants as Austin, just in a different style. The arts scene is strong (Dallas Museum of Art, Perot Museum, AT&T Performing Arts Center). Professional sports give DFW an advantage — Cowboys, Mavericks, Stars, and Rangers all call North Texas home. And the lake culture around Lake Grapevine, Lake Lewisville, and Lake Ray Hubbard gives families a genuine outdoor weekend option that doesn't require a 90-minute drive.

When I talk to families who've moved to Austin and later looked at DFW, the word I hear most often is "infrastructure." Not highways — I mean the stuff that matters when you have kids: the depth of the school choices, the youth sports leagues, the established neighborhoods where everyone else on the street is also raising a family. Dallas has built that infrastructure over decades. Austin is still building it.

The Honest Verdict

Choose Dallas–Fort Worth if...
  • Schools are your primary filter — DFW has more top-tier ISDs at more price points
  • You work in finance, healthcare, corporate management, or logistics
  • You want established suburban communities with extensive family infrastructure
  • Traffic predictability matters — DFW's suburban layout distributes commutes better
  • You want more housing options at every price point across the metro
  • You're a military family — NAS Fort Worth JRB is in DFW, with strong VA loan resources
Choose Austin if...
  • You work for Apple, Meta, Google, NVIDIA, Tesla, Oracle, or another major Austin tech employer
  • Your family genuinely prioritizes outdoor recreation, live music, and a university-town vibe
  • You're buying now while Austin's market is in a correction — there's more room to negotiate
  • You're coming from the Pacific Northwest, Denver, or a similar culture and want that feel in Texas
  • Your kids are older and school district depth matters less than proximity to UT Austin

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Dallas or Austin better for families?

Dallas is generally stronger for families prioritizing school district depth and suburban community infrastructure. DFW has more top-rated ISDs across a wider range of price points. Austin's suburban districts are solid but fewer. Austin suits families in tech who value its outdoorsy, live-music culture over suburban variety.

Are homes cheaper in Dallas or Austin?

Austin's metro median dropped to $415,300 in Q1 2026 (Austin Board of Realtors) — down from the 2022 peak and still correcting. Dallas city median SFR is ~$492,000 (NTREIS 2026), but DFW suburbs like McKinney and Allen offer comparable quality at $480,000–$550,000. For families, DFW offers more options at more price points. Both metros are above the national median.

Which city has worse traffic — Dallas or Austin?

Austin's traffic is significantly worse, particularly I-35 and MoPac. Austin's infrastructure hasn't kept pace with its population growth. Dallas has heavy traffic too, but DART's 90-mile light rail provides an alternative, and DFW's suburban layout means commutes are more distributed and less concentrated on a few bottleneck corridors.

Is Austin or Dallas better for tech jobs?

Austin is the stronger pure-tech hub — Apple, Meta, Google, NVIDIA, Oracle, and Tesla all have major Austin campuses, and about 16% of Austin jobs are tech-related. DFW has the larger overall tech workforce (6th largest nationally) but its strength is broader: finance, healthcare, and corporate HQs dominate alongside tech, making it less dependent on one sector.

Does Dallas or Austin have better schools?

DFW has the edge. Frisco ISD, Plano ISD, Allen ISD, and Highland Park ISD are among Texas's best, and they're accessible across multiple suburban communities at varying price points. Austin's suburban districts (Round Rock, Lake Travis, Leander) are good, but DFW simply has more top-tier options spread across a wider geographic area.

Are property taxes different in Dallas vs Austin?

Similar, but Dallas County runs slightly higher (~2.22%) than Travis County (~1.95–2.1%). Both Texas metros have no state income tax. The homestead exemption applies in both. Because Austin city homes are priced higher than many DFW suburbs, your actual annual tax bill may be comparable even at a slightly lower rate in Austin.
