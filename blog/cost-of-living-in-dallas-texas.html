The Real Cost of Living in Dallas, Texas — What Families Actually Pay

Kristen Carpentier Licensed TX Realtor®  ·  May 29, 2026  ·  12 min read

Every family researching a move to Dallas-Fort Worth eventually asks the same question: what does it actually cost to live here? The answer is genuinely complicated — not because the data is hard to find, but because "Dallas" encompasses everything from the city itself to a ring of suburbs where median home prices range from $370K to well over $1.3 million. Where you land in that range changes your monthly picture dramatically.

I've helped hundreds of families work through this math. Most arrive with a rough sense that DFW is cheaper than wherever they're coming from — and that's usually true — but they're often surprised by the details: how Texas property taxes work, what utilities actually cost in a Texas summer, and what a given salary realistically buys in each suburb. This guide walks through all of it, using real numbers.

How Dallas Compares to Other Major Cities

The Dallas-Fort Worth metro is consistently rated at or slightly below the U.S. average on national cost-of-living indices — a remarkable position for the fourth-largest metro in the country. That surprises people who assume big city equals expensive. The difference is almost entirely housing: DFW has enough land and enough building activity that prices have stayed relatively disciplined compared to coastal metros.

~55% Less than Bay Area housing
$0 Texas state income tax
~U.S. avg DFW overall cost-of-living
4.3M+ Nonfarm payroll jobs, DFW metro

For families relocating from California, the most striking comparison is housing. A home that would cost $1.2 million in the Bay Area or $900,000 in Los Angeles often has a near-equivalent in Frisco or Allen for $500K–$680K. For families from New York or Illinois, the savings are still substantial — and the no-state-income-tax environment adds meaningful take-home pay on top of lower housing costs.

What DFW does not offer is walkability, mass transit, or urban density. If your family is choosing between DFW and a city like Chicago or New York for lifestyle reasons, the cost math alone won't resolve it — but if you're relocating for a job, remote work flexibility, or to be near extended family in Texas, the financial case for DFW is clear.

At the higher end, Prosper sits around $845K and Southlake exceeds $1.3M — both genuine luxury markets. At the lower end, Denton comes in around $370K, and parts of Dallas proper offer condos and starter homes under $350K if you're open to urban neighborhoods rather than suburbs.

One thing relocating families often miss: DFW has significant new construction inventory at every price point, which means you're not just competing for resale homes. Property Taxes in Texas: The Real Story

Texas has a reputation for high property taxes, and it's earned. But the picture is more nuanced than the headline rate suggests — and it's impossible to understand without comparing it to what you'd pay in your current state.

Frisco, TX ~1.80% ~$9,000 None
McKinney, TX ~1.95% ~$9,750 None
Allen, TX ~1.85% ~$9,250 None
Plano, TX ~1.78% ~$8,900 None

California (avg) ~1.10% ~$5,500 Up to 13.3%
Illinois (avg) ~2.27% ~$11,350 4.95% flat
New York (avg) ~1.50% ~$7,500 Up to 10.9%

Rates are blended estimates for illustrative comparison. Actual effective rates vary by ISD, county, city, and any Municipal Utility District (MUD) or Public Improvement District (PID) overlays — which can add 0.25%–0.75% in newer master-planned communities. Always verify with the county appraisal district or your agent before closing.

A few important details that shift the Texas math in your favor:

Homestead exemption. Texas homeowners who claim a homestead exemption (your primary residence) receive a mandatory $140,000 exemption off the school district portion of your appraised value — effective for 2023 forward under Texas Tax Code §11.13(b). On a $500K home, that reduces the school-taxable value to $360,000 — saving roughly $1,500–$1,900 per year depending on the ISD rate. You file once with the county appraisal district, and it stays attached to the property as long as it's your primary residence.

Appraisal caps. Texas law limits annual appraisal increases on homesteaded properties to 10% per year. After several years of rapid price appreciation, many existing homeowners are paying taxes on an appraised value well below market value — an advantage that accrues over time the longer you stay.

No deductibility at the federal level for most families. Since the 2017 tax law capped the state and local tax (SALT) deduction at $10,000, most families in high-tax states lost the ability to fully deduct their state income and property taxes. That change made the Texas tax structure relatively more attractive than it might appear on paper alone.

Kristen's Take

The property tax number that shows up in your mortgage estimate will shock you if you're coming from California. But once you run the full math — no state income tax, homestead exemption, and a home that costs 50%–60% less — most families end up ahead. I walk through this calculation with every client before they decide on a price range.

Everyday Living Costs in DFW

Outside of housing and taxes, DFW's day-to-day costs are broadly in line with the national average. There are a few standouts worth planning for.

Groceries. Expect $800–$1,200 per month for a family of four, depending on diet and where you shop. DFW has every major chain — HEB (if you're in the western suburbs), Whole Foods, Central Market, Sprouts, Kroger, Tom Thumb, Costco, and Trader Joe's in the denser areas. Prices are generally 5%–10% below what families from the Bay Area or NYC pay.

Utilities — the Texas summer reality. This is where DFW surprises most newcomers. June through September, the electricity bill on a 2,500–3,500 sq ft home runs $250–$400 per month. The combination of 100°F temperatures and continuous A/C use is unavoidable. Winter bills are much more manageable — $80–$150 for electricity. Natural gas (used for heating, water heaters, and cooking in most suburban homes) adds another $40–$90 per month seasonally. Budget $200/month on an annual average, and plan for summer spikes.

Transportation. DFW is a car-dependent metro. Most suburban families need two vehicles. Budget $400–$700/month for two car payments (or factor in ownership costs), plus $200–$300/month for gasoline. Texas gas prices are typically $0.30–$0.50 below the national average due to proximity to refineries. Toll roads (the Dallas North Tollway, the President George Bush Turnpike, SH 121) are a fact of life in the northern suburbs — budget $80–$150/month if you commute regularly on them.

Childcare and activities. Full-time daycare in the suburbs runs $1,200–$1,800/month per child depending on age and center quality. Youth sports leagues (soccer, baseball, flag football) are abundant and inexpensive — most run $80–$200/season. Club sports and travel teams are a different category, running $2,000–$5,000+ annually per child. DFW has exceptionally strong youth sports infrastructure, which matters for families relocating with active kids.

Dining out. A family dinner at a mid-range restaurant typically runs $60–$100. DFW's restaurant scene has expanded dramatically — you'll find genuinely excellent options in most suburbs, not just in Dallas proper. Budget $300–$600/month depending on how often you go out.

Hidden Costs That Catch DFW Newcomers Off Guard

The everyday expenses above are the predictable ones. But several costs consistently surprise families who relocate to DFW — and they can meaningfully shift your monthly picture if you don't plan for them.

MUD and PID district surcharges. Many new-construction suburbs — especially in the outer growth corridors of Celina, Little Elm, Prosper, and Anna — sit inside Municipal Utility Districts (MUDs) or Public Improvement Districts (PIDs). These overlay districts issue bonds to fund roads, water, and amenities, and they add 0.25%–0.75% to your effective property tax rate on top of the base ISD and county rates. A Prosper home in a MUD district might carry an effective rate of 2.7%–2.9% rather than the headline ~2.2%. Always ask your agent for the total tax rate — not just the ISD rate — before making an offer.

Toll roads. If you work in Dallas proper, Legacy West, or Las Colinas from a northern suburb, toll roads are nearly unavoidable. The Dallas North Tollway, the President George Bush Turnpike (PGBT/190), and SH 121/SH 114 all charge tolls through TxTag transponders. Regular commuters budget $80–$160/month. This isn't a reason to avoid the northern suburbs — the roads are excellent — but it's a real line item that doesn't show up in rent or mortgage calculators.

Hail insurance. North Texas sits in Hail Alley. Large hailstorms are a genuine annual risk, not an unlikely catastrophe. Standard homeowner's insurance policies in DFW typically carry a 1%–2% wind/hail deductible — which on a $500K home means $5,000–$10,000 out of pocket before insurance kicks in for roof or siding claims. Premiums have increased in DFW over the last several years as insurers price in the risk. Budget $200–$350/month for a comprehensive policy that includes wind/hail coverage.

Summer electricity spikes. Already mentioned above, but worth repeating: June through September, air conditioning runs nearly continuously in homes above 2,000 sq ft. The difference between an average monthly electricity bill ($200) and a July peak ($350–$450) catches families off guard if they set a monthly budget based on their home state's utility costs. When your agent gives you a home's average utility cost, ask specifically for the summer average.

HOA fees in master-planned communities. Most family-oriented suburbs have HOA-governed communities. HOA dues in DFW typically run $50–$150/month for a standard subdivision, but master-planned communities with resort-style amenities (pools, fitness centers, parks, trails, social events) run $150–$350/month. These are separate from any MUD/PID assessments. They're often worth it — HOA maintenance creates more consistent resale values — but factor them in.

Kristen's Take

The total effective property tax rate — not the headline ISD rate — is the most important number to nail down before you make an offer. I always pull the tax certificate for my buyers so we know exactly what the MUD/PID situation is. It's the number that surprises people most, and it's 100% discoverable before you close.

The No State Income Tax Advantage

Texas's most powerful financial benefit is one that doesn't show up in a mortgage payment or utility bill — it shows up in your paycheck every two weeks. Texas has no state income tax. For families relocating from California, Illinois, or New York, this represents a meaningful and immediate increase in take-home pay.

$0 Texas state income tax
~$7,900 Annual savings vs California (9.3%+) at $120K income
~$5,900 Annual savings vs Illinois (4.95% flat) at $120K income

Here's how the math works at a $120,000 household income:

  • California (9.3% marginal + phase-ins): roughly $7,900–$9,200 in state income tax annually
  • New York State (6.85% + NYC surcharge if applicable): roughly $6,800–$9,000 annually
  • Illinois (4.95% flat): roughly $5,940 annually
  • Texas: $0

That difference — $500–$750 per month in additional take-home pay — significantly offsets the property tax premium Texas carries. For dual-income households earning $160K+, the advantage grows proportionally. Over a 10-year period, the cumulative income tax savings from a California-to-Texas move routinely reaches $80,000–$120,000 for professional households.

Which DFW Suburb Fits Your Budget?

The right suburb depends on your budget, your employer's location, and your school priorities. Here's how I generally frame it for families I work with:

Suburbs for Budgets Under $450K
  • Lewisville — strong value, Lewisville ISD, central DFW location near DFW airport
  • Denton — university town, most affordable major suburb, Denton ISD
  • McKinney (outer areas) — some neighborhoods in the $380K–$430K range with McKinney ISD access
  • Carrollton — dense suburb with strong resale values, Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD
Suburbs for Budgets $450K–$650K
  • McKinney — one of DFW's top family suburbs, A-rated ISD, growing restaurant scene
  • Allen — compact, highly rated Allen ISD, shorter commute than outer suburbs
  • Plano — established suburb, Plano ISD, proximity to Legacy West employers
  • Flower Mound — family-focused, excellent Lewisville ISD feeder schools, lower density
Suburbs for Budgets $650K+
  • Frisco — top-rated Frisco ISD, massive amenities, The Star, premium new construction
  • Prosper — newer, quieter, excellent Prosper ISD, strong appreciation history
  • Southlake — top-ranked Carroll ISD, established luxury market, central Metroplex location
  • Colleyville — established, Grapevine-Colleyville ISD, low density and premium feel

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Dallas cheaper to live in than Austin?

Yes, in most categories. DFW housing runs roughly 10%–15% below the Austin metro on median single-family prices. Both cities have no state income tax and comparable property tax rates. DFW's job market is also larger and more diversified — which matters if one partner is job-searching. Austin has better walkability and a tighter urban core, but for families prioritizing space, schools, and value, DFW consistently wins the comparison.

What salary do you need to live comfortably in Dallas?

For a suburban lifestyle with a home purchase in the $420K–$500K range, two cars, and activities for kids, a household income of $110,000–$130,000 is a reasonable target. If you're looking at Frisco, Flower Mound, or Southlake, budget for $140,000–$180,000 to feel comfortable rather than stretched. For renting and deferring a home purchase, $80,000–$95,000 is workable in most suburbs.

Are property taxes really that high in Texas?

Texas property taxes average 1.7%–2.3% of appraised value — higher than most states. However, Texas has no state income tax, which offsets the difference for most families. A household earning $130,000 saves roughly $8,500–$10,000 per year compared to California, or about $6,400 compared to Illinois (which has a 4.95% flat rate). The $140,000 homestead exemption also reduces the effective school-district rate meaningfully for owner-occupied homes. Run the full math before assuming Texas taxes are a deal-breaker.

How does the cost of living in Dallas compare to California?

DFW housing costs 55%–65% less than the Bay Area and 40%–50% less than Los Angeles for comparable homes in comparable school districts. Texas has no state income tax versus California's 9.3%+ marginal rate for most professional households. Groceries and utilities are broadly similar. The main trade-off is weather (hot summers vs mild California climate), walkability, and cultural density. Most families I work with find the financial difference is not close — it's dramatic.

What is the average utility bill in Dallas, Texas?

Expect $150–$250 per month for electricity on an annual average, with summer months (June–September) often hitting $250–$350 or higher for larger homes. Winter electricity bills are significantly lower — typically $80–$130. Natural gas, water, and internet add another $150–$200 monthly. Budget $350–$450 total for all utilities on an annual average, and plan specifically for the summer spike if you're setting a monthly budget.

What is the cost of living in Dallas, Texas for a family?

A family of four earning $130,000 with a $480K home in McKinney can expect total monthly expenses of roughly $7,200 — covering mortgage, property taxes, insurance, utilities, groceries, two cars, childcare, dining, and healthcare. That leaves $700–$1,000/month for savings. Families wanting more breathing room typically target the $380K–$440K price range or a household income above $150,000.

Are there hidden costs to moving to DFW suburbs?

Yes — several catch newcomers off guard. MUD and PID districts (common in new-construction suburbs like Celina, Prosper, and Little Elm) add 0.25%–0.75% to the effective property tax rate on top of the base ISD rate. Toll roads add $80–$160/month for regular commuters on the DNT, PGBT, or SH 121. Hail insurance is a real line item in North Texas. Always ask your agent for the total effective tax rate — not just the ISD rate — before making an offer.

Which DFW suburbs have the best schools for the money?

Allen ISD, McKinney ISD, and Lewisville ISD offer the strongest school ratings in the $400K–$600K price range. Frisco ISD is consistently top-ranked but commands a price premium ($600K+ median). For families who prioritize both school quality and budget, McKinney and Allen represent the best-value combination in the metro. See our full DFW school districts guide for detailed ratings and comparisons.

Is DFW a good place to move for families?

DFW consistently ranks among the top U.S. metros for family relocation — driven by strong job growth (4.3M+ nonfarm payroll jobs), no state income tax, excellent suburban school districts, affordable housing relative to coastal peers, and abundant youth sports and family amenities. The main trade-offs are car dependency, hot summers, and the absence of urban walkability that some families miss from coastal cities.

Ready to Find Your DFW Suburb?

The cost of living in Dallas depends almost entirely on which suburb you choose and what you're comparing it to. For most families relocating from coastal markets, the financial case is straightforward: lower housing costs, no state income tax, and a large labor market with strong corporate employers. The challenge is finding the right suburb at the right price point for your family's specific priorities — schools, commute, community feel, and new vs. resale.
