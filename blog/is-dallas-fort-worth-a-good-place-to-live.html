Is Dallas-Fort Worth a Good Place to Live?

Kristen Carpentier Licensed TX Realtor®  ·  August 30, 2024  ·  20 min read

Dallas-Fort Worth (DFW) is one of the most dynamic and fastest-growing metropolitan areas in the United States. As of 2024, the DFW metroplex continues to attract newcomers with its strong economy, diverse job opportunities, vibrant cultural scene, and relatively affordable cost of living. But is it the right place for you? This detailed guide aims to provide a comprehensive analysis of what living in DFW entails, covering everything from the pros and cons to the job market, housing, schools, and more.

Why You Should Read This Article

Relocating to a new city is a significant decision that requires careful consideration. This article will help you navigate the complexities of moving to DFW by offering up-to-date information and data on key aspects of life in the metroplex. With insights into the job market, housing trends, crime rates, educational opportunities, and more, this guide will equip you with the knowledge you need to determine whether Dallas-Fort Worth is the right fit for you and your family. Given the ongoing growth and changes in DFW, having the latest information is crucial for making an informed decision.

Pros and Cons of Living in Dallas-Fort Worth, Texas

Pros:

  • Strong Job Market: DFW is home to a diverse economy with robust job opportunities across various sectors. The area has seen consistent job growth, attracting professionals nationwide. Major employers in the region include American Airlines, AT&T, Texas Instruments, and Lockheed Martin, offering positions in industries like aerospace, telecommunications, and technology.

  • Affordable Cost of Living: Despite recent growth, DFW remains more affordable than other major metros like New York, San Francisco, or Los Angeles. Housing, in particular, is more attainable, especially in the suburbs.

  • Cultural and Recreational Opportunities: DFW offers a wide range of cultural experiences, including world-class museums, theaters, parks, and professional sports teams like the Dallas Cowboys and Texas Rangers.

  • Diverse Communities: The metroplex is known for its diverse population, which contributes to a rich cultural tapestry with influences from around the world.

  • Good Schools: Several suburbs in DFW are renowned for their highly-rated public schools, making the area attractive to families.

Cons:

  • Traffic Congestion: Rapid growth has led to increased traffic congestion, particularly during rush hours on major highways like I-35 and I-635.

  • Hot Summers: The climate in DFW can be extreme, with hot and humid summers that might be uncomfortable for some.

  • Urban Sprawl: The sprawling nature of the metroplex can result in long commutes and a sense of disconnection between different parts of the city.

  • Political Division: DFW's political climate can be polarized, with urban areas leaning more liberal and suburban/rural areas leaning more conservative.

Overview of What DFW Has to Offer

Dallas-Fort Worth is a sprawling metropolitan area with something for everyone. Whether you’re interested in a bustling urban lifestyle, suburban tranquility, or rural simplicity, DFW has it all. The area is known for its vibrant arts scene, with numerous museums, theaters, and live music venues. Sports enthusiasts can enjoy games from several professional teams, including the Dallas Cowboys, Texas Rangers, and Dallas Mavericks. Outdoor enthusiasts will find plenty of parks, trails, and lakes for hiking, biking, and water activities.

In addition, the food scene in DFW is diverse and ever-growing, offering everything from fine dining to authentic Texas barbecue. The area’s central location also makes it a hub for travel, with DFW International Airport being one of the busiest in the world, offering flights to destinations across the globe.

What Is the Job Market Like in DFW?

The Dallas-Fort Worth job market is one of the strongest in the United States, driven by a diverse economy that spans multiple industries. As of 2024, the metroplex continues to see significant job growth, making it a popular destination for professionals seeking career advancement.

Major Employers in DFW

1. American Airlines Group - Headquartered in Fort Worth, American Airlines is one of the largest airlines in the world and a major employer in the region, providing jobs in aviation, customer service, and management. [Source: American Airlines Careers]

2. AT&T - Based in downtown Dallas, AT&T is a global leader in telecommunications and media, offering a wide range of job opportunities in technology, engineering, and corporate services. [Source: AT&T Careers]

3. Texas Instruments - A leading technology company specializing in semiconductors and electronics, Texas Instruments has its headquarters in Dallas and offers numerous positions in engineering, research, and development. [Source: Texas Instruments Careers].

4. Lockheed Martin - Located in Fort Worth, Lockheed Martin is a major defense contractor providing jobs in aerospace engineering, manufacturing, and project management. [Source: Lockheed Martin Careers].

5. ExxonMobil - Although ExxonMobil's headquarters moved to Houston, its presence in DFW remains significant, particularly in Irving, where it offers jobs in energy, engineering, and corporate services. [Source: ExxonMobil Careers].

Key Industries

  • Technology: DFW is a growing tech hub, attracting both startups and established tech companies. In addition to AT&T and Texas Instruments, companies like Microsoft, IBM, and Infosys have a significant presence in the region. The tech industry in DFW is bolstered by a skilled workforce and a business-friendly environment.

  • Finance: Dallas is a major financial center, home to large banks and financial institutions like Bank of America, JPMorgan Chase, and Goldman Sachs. The city's position as a financial hub is supported by a robust economy and a growing population, creating demand for financial services.

  • Healthcare: The healthcare industry in DFW is expanding rapidly, with major hospitals and health systems like Baylor Scott & White Health, Texas Health Resources, and UT Southwestern Medical Center providing a wide range of medical services and job opportunities. The region's population growth has led to increased demand for healthcare professionals.

  • Transportation and Logistics: DFW's central location makes it a key hub for transportation and logistics. Companies like American Airlines, Southwest Airlines, and UPS have large operations in the area, contributing to the region's economic growth and providing numerous jobs in logistics, transportation, and supply chain management.

  • Energy: While Houston is known as the energy capital of the world, DFW also plays a significant role in the energy sector, particularly in oil and gas, with companies like ExxonMobil and Pioneer Natural Resources having a strong presence in the region.

Job Growth and Unemployment

As of 2024, the unemployment rate in DFW remains below the national average, reflecting the area's strong job market. According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, job growth in DFW has been consistent over the past several years, with an increasing number of companies relocating to or expanding within the region due to its business-friendly climate and skilled workforce. [Source: U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics].

The demand for skilled professionals in DFW continues to grow, particularly in industries like technology, healthcare, and finance. This trend is expected to continue, making DFW an attractive destination for job seekers across various fields.

An Overview of the DFW Housing Market

The Dallas-Fort Worth housing market has been one of the most dynamic in the country over the past decade. As of 2024, the market remains competitive, though there has been a slight cooling due to recent interest rate hikes and economic uncertainties.

Home Prices

  • Median Home Prices: According to the Texas Real Estate Research Center, the median home price in DFW as of mid-2024 is around $375,000, which represents a moderate increase compared to the previous year. However, prices can vary significantly depending on the area, with suburban homes generally being more affordable than those in urban centers like Dallas and Fort Worth. [Source: Texas Real Estate Research Center]

  • Market Trends: While the DFW housing market remains strong, rising interest rates have begun to slow down price growth and lengthen the time homes spend on the market. This trend is creating opportunities for buyers, particularly first-time homebuyers, as sellers become more willing to negotiate.

Suburbs vs. Urban Areas

  • Suburban Living: The suburbs of DFW, such as Frisco, Plano, Flower Mound, and Southlake, are particularly popular among families due to their highly-rated schools, community amenities, and relatively affordable housing options. These areas have seen significant population growth, leading to the development of new residential communities, shopping centers, and entertainment venues.

  • Urban Living: For those who prefer a more vibrant lifestyle, urban areas like downtown Dallas, Uptown, and Fort Worth offer a mix of modern condos, historic homes, and luxury apartments. While these areas provide easy access to cultural and entertainment options, they come with a higher price tag and, in some cases, less space. Downtown is also home to some of the wealthiest and most expensive real estate in DFW as noted with cities such as Highland Park, and University Park.

Rental Market

The rental market in DFW is also competitive, with demand for apartments and rental homes increasing as more people move to the area. According to RentCafe, the average rent in Dallas is around $1,500 per month, with higher rents in more desirable neighborhoods such as Uptown and Lower Greenville. [Source: RentCafe]

New Construction

DFW is seeing a surge in new construction residential developments, particularly in the suburbs. These developments often include master-planned communities with amenities like parks, pools, and walking trails, catering to families and professionals looking for a balanced lifestyle.

Overview of Crime and How It Compares

Overview of Crime and How It Compares to Other Major Metro Areas

When considering a move to a new city, understanding the local crime rates and safety levels is crucial. The Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex, like any large urban area, has a varied crime landscape that differs significantly depending on the specific neighborhood or suburb.

Crime Rates in DFW

  • Dallas: As the largest city in the metroplex, Dallas has higher crime rates compared to other parts of the region. According to the Dallas Police Department, the city’s crime rate has seen fluctuations, with specific areas experiencing higher incidents of violent crime, particularly in South Dallas and parts of downtown. However, neighborhoods like Highland Park, Lakewood, and Preston Hollow have significantly lower crime rates and are considered very safe. [Source: Dallas Police Department].

  • Fort Worth: Fort Worth generally has lower crime rates than Dallas. The Fort Worth Police Department reports that crime has been relatively stable, with most violent crimes concentrated in specific urban areas. Suburbs surrounding Fort Worth, such as Keller and Southlake, have some of the lowest crime rates in the metroplex. [Source: Fort Worth Police Department].

Suburban Safety

One of the key advantages of living in the suburbs of DFW is the generally lower crime rates compared to urban areas. Suburbs like Plano, Flower Mound, and Colleyville consistently rank among the safest places to live in Texas. These areas have strong community policing efforts, active neighborhood watch programs, and lower incidents of both violent and property crimes.

  • Plano: Known for its excellent schools and family-friendly environment, Plano has a crime rate that is significantly lower than the national average. It’s often cited as one of the safest cities in the U.S., making it a top choice for families relocating to the DFW area. [Source: NeighborhoodScout].

  • Southlake: Southlake, another affluent suburb, boasts some of the lowest crime rates in the region. The community places a strong emphasis on safety, with well-funded police and fire departments ensuring a secure environment for residents. [Source: SafeWise].

Comparison to Other Major Metro Areas

When compared to other major U.S. metropolitan areas, DFW’s crime rates are relatively average. For instance:

- Chicago: DFW has lower violent crime rates than Chicago, particularly in terms of homicides and shootings. While Chicago has struggled with high levels of violent crime in recent years, DFW’s crime issues are more concentrated and not as widespread.

- Los Angeles: Los Angeles also has a higher crime rate compared to DFW, especially in terms of property crimes. However, like DFW, crime in Los Angeles varies widely depending on the neighborhood.

- New York City: New York City, which once had higher crime rates, has seen significant improvements and now boasts lower overall crime rates than many parts of DFW, particularly in Manhattan and certain areas of Brooklyn.

Overall, while DFW does have areas with higher crime rates, particularly in parts of Dallas, many of its suburbs and certain urban neighborhoods are very safe, making it a desirable place for families and professionals alike.

Overview of Public Schools and How They Compare to Other Major Metros

For families with children, the quality of public schools is a major factor in choosing where to live. The Dallas-Fort Worth metroplex offers a variety of educational options, including some of the best public schools in Texas.

Top School Districts in DFW

  • Frisco Independent School District (FISD): Frisco ISD is one of the fastest-growing school districts in Texas, known for its excellent academic programs, sports teams, and extracurricular activities. The district consistently receives high ratings for student performance and is a top choice for families moving to the northern suburbs of DFW. [Source: Frisco ISD].

  • Carroll Independent School District (CISD): Located in Southlake, Carroll ISD is renowned for its high academic standards and competitive athletic programs. The district’s schools regularly rank among the top in the state, making Southlake a highly sought-after community for families. [Source: Carroll ISD].

  • Plano Independent School District (PISD): Plano ISD is another top-performing district, known for its strong academic programs and a wide range of extracurricular activities. The district’s schools are often ranked among the best in the nation, particularly for their STEM programs. [Source: Plano ISD].

Comparison to Other Metros

- New York City: While NYC has some highly prestigious public schools, the overall system is large and varied, with significant disparities in quality. DFW’s top suburban schools generally outperform many public schools in NYC, especially in terms of academic achievement and college readiness.

- Los Angeles: Los Angeles Unified School District is one of the largest in the country, but it struggles with overcrowding and varying quality. DFW’s suburban districts, by contrast, tend to have smaller class sizes and more resources per student.

- Chicago: Chicago Public Schools have faced numerous challenges, including funding issues and disparities in quality across the city. DFW’s schools, particularly in the suburbs, offer more consistency in terms of academic performance and student outcomes.

Charter and Private Schools

In addition to public schools, DFW is home to a growing number of charter schools and private schools. Charter schools like Uplift Education and KIPP Texas provide alternative public education options with a focus on college readiness. Private schools such as The Hockaday School, St. Mark’s School of Texas, and Greenhill School offer prestigious education opportunities, albeit at a higher cost.

Families in DFW have a wealth of options to choose from, ensuring that there is a good fit for almost any educational preference.

Overview of Political Climate and How It Compares to Other Areas

The political landscape in Dallas-Fort Worth is diverse, reflecting a mix of urban liberalism and suburban conservatism. Understanding the political climate is important for potential residents, as it can influence everything from local policies to community culture.

Urban vs. Suburban Political Divide

  • Dallas and Fort Worth: Both Dallas and Fort Worth tend to lean more liberal, particularly in the downtown and surrounding urban neighborhoods. Dallas, in particular, has a strong Democratic presence, with progressive policies on issues like healthcare, education, and social services.

  • Suburbs: In contrast, the suburbs of DFW, such as Plano, Southlake, and Frisco, tend to be more conservative. These areas often support lower taxes, less government regulation, and more traditional values. The political divide between urban and suburban areas can sometimes lead to contrasting policies and community initiatives.

State Politics

Texas is known for its conservative state government, which influences policies across the board, including education, healthcare, and business regulations. However, as DFW continues to grow and diversify, there has been a gradual shift in some areas towards more moderate or liberal viewpoints, particularly in the larger cities.

- 2024 Election Trends: The 2024 elections saw continued strong support for Republican candidates in the suburbs, while urban areas, particularly Dallas, supported Democratic candidates. This political landscape mirrors broader national trends of urban-rural political divisions. [Source: Texas Secretary of State].

Community Engagement

DFW residents are generally very engaged in local politics. Voter turnout in the metroplex is relatively high, particularly in suburban areas where local elections often determine key issues like school funding and city planning. Community involvement extends beyond just voting; many residents participate in local government meetings, community organizations, and advocacy groups.

For newcomers, understanding the political climate is important as it can shape the community’s culture and the types of policies that are prioritized.

Discuss Typical Traffic and Travel Times for Commuters

Traffic and commute times are important considerations for anyone moving to Dallas-Fort Worth. As a rapidly growing metroplex, DFW has seen significant increases in traffic congestion over the years.

Traffic Patterns

  • Rush Hour Traffic: The morning and evening rush hours in DFW can be particularly challenging, with heavy traffic on major highways like I-35E, I-635, and the Dallas North Tollway. According to the Texas A&M Transportation Institute, the average commuter in DFW spends approximately 54 hours per year stuck in traffic, a figure that has been steadily increasing as the metroplex grows. [Source: Texas A&M Transportation Institute].

  • Public Transportation: While DFW does have public transportation options, including the Dallas Area Rapid Transit (DART) system and Trinity Metro in Fort Worth, these services are limited compared to other major metros. Most residents rely on cars for their daily commute, which contributes to traffic congestion.

  • Toll Roads: The metroplex has an extensive network of toll roads, such as the President George Bush Turnpike (PGBT) and the Sam Rayburn Tollway. These roads can help reduce travel times but add to commuting costs. TollTag, the local electronic toll collection system, offers discounts to frequent users. [Source: North Texas Tollway Authority].

Suburban Commutes

  • Commuting from Suburbs: Residents living in popular suburbs like Frisco, McKinney, and Flower Mound often face longer commutes into downtown Dallas or Fort Worth. While these areas offer a higher quality of life, with excellent schools and family-friendly amenities, the trade-off is often a longer drive to work, sometimes exceeding an hour during peak traffic times.

  • Alternative Transportation: With the rise of remote work and flexible work arrangements, many DFW residents are finding alternatives to the traditional daily commute. Some companies in the area are adopting hybrid or fully remote work models, reducing the need for long drives during rush hours. Additionally, biking and ride-sharing services like Uber and Lyft are growing in popularity, particularly in more urbanized parts of Dallas and Fort Worth.

Impact of Population Growth on Traffic

DFW's population growth has directly impacted traffic congestion. As more people move to the area, the demand for road infrastructure has increased, often outpacing the region's ability to expand and improve transportation networks. The Texas Department of Transportation (TxDOT) has several ongoing projects aimed at alleviating traffic congestion, such as widening highways and adding new lanes, but these improvements take time and can temporarily exacerbate traffic during construction.

For those considering a move to DFW, it's important to factor in commute times when choosing a neighborhood or suburb. While the metroplex offers many benefits, traffic can be a significant downside, especially for those who need to travel to and from work during peak hours.

Recap: Who May Enjoy Living in DFW and Who It May Not Be Right For

Dallas-Fort Worth is a diverse and rapidly growing metropolitan area that offers a wealth of opportunities and amenities for residents. However, like any city, it has its advantages and disadvantages. Here’s a recap to help you decide if DFW is the right place for you:

Who May Enjoy Living in DFW:

  • Professionals Seeking Job Opportunities: With its strong and diverse economy, DFW is ideal for individuals looking for job growth in industries such as technology, finance, healthcare, and logistics. The presence of major employers like American Airlines, AT&T, and Texas Instruments provides a wide range of career opportunities.

  • Families: The suburbs of DFW are known for their excellent schools, safe neighborhoods, and family-friendly amenities, making it an attractive option for families with children. Areas like Plano, Frisco, and Southlake offer top-rated school districts and a variety of recreational activities.

  • Cultural Enthusiasts: Those who enjoy cultural activities will find plenty to do in DFW, from museums and theaters to live music and sports events. The metroplex is home to a vibrant arts scene and several professional sports teams.

  • Outdoor Lovers: The region offers numerous parks, trails, and outdoor activities for those who love spending time in nature. From the Dallas Arboretum and Botanical Garden to the Fort Worth Stockyards, there are plenty of outdoor attractions to explore.

  • Homebuyers Seeking Affordability: Compared to other major U.S. cities, DFW offers relatively affordable housing options, particularly in the suburbs. This makes it an attractive destination for first-time homebuyers and those looking for more space.

Who It May Not Be Right For:

  • Individuals Averse to Hot Weather: DFW’s hot and humid summers can be challenging for those who prefer cooler climates. Temperatures often exceed 100°F during the peak of summer, and the heat can be intense.

  • Those Who Prefer Car-Free Living: While DFW does offer some public transportation options, they are not as extensive or convenient as in other major cities like New York or Chicago. Most residents rely on cars for their daily transportation, and the spread-out nature of the metroplex can make car-free living difficult.

  • People Who Dislike Urban Sprawl: The metroplex covers a vast area, leading to long commutes and a sense of disconnection between different parts of the city. Those who prefer more compact, walkable urban environments might find DFW’s sprawl less appealing.

  • Those Sensitive to Political Polarization: The diverse political climate in DFW, with urban areas leaning liberal and suburbs more conservative, may not be comfortable for everyone. This polarization can influence local policies and community culture, making it important to understand the political dynamics before moving.

Conclusion

Dallas-Fort Worth is a vibrant and diverse metroplex that offers a high quality of life, strong job opportunities, and a relatively affordable cost of living. However, it also comes with challenges such as traffic congestion, hot summers, and urban sprawl. Whether DFW is the right place for you depends on your personal preferences and priorities.

This comprehensive guide has provided an in-depth look at the various aspects of living in DFW, from the job market and housing to schools and the political climate. With this information, you can make an informed decision about whether DFW is the ideal place for you to live.

For those who decide to make DFW their home, the metroplex offers a welcoming and thriving community with endless possibilities. Whether you're looking for a suburban lifestyle, urban excitement, or both, Dallas-Fort Worth has something to offer everyone.
