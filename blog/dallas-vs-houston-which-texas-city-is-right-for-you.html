Dallas vs. Houston: Which Texas City Is Actually Better for Families?

Kristen Carpentier Licensed TX Realtor®  ·  Updated May 23, 2026  ·  10 min read

Every week I talk to families who've narrowed their Texas move to two cities: Dallas or Houston. They're usually coming from California, New York, or Illinois — exhausted by high taxes and high prices — and they've heard good things about both metros. So how do you choose?

I've spent nearly 15 years in DFW as a mom, a Realtor, and the wife of an Army Veteran. I know the Dallas suburbs the way you'd know a neighborhood you've actually raised kids in — not from a data sheet. But I also know Houston well enough to tell you what the data misses. This guide gives you the honest comparison, because picking the wrong metro is a mistake that's hard to undo.

Watch: Dallas vs. Houston head-to-head — schools, suburbs, cost of living, and the honest trade-offs for relocating families.

Schools & Education: Where DFW Has a Clear Edge

If you have school-age kids, this is the section that matters most. I say that not as a sales pitch for Dallas — I say it because it's what my clients ultimately care about more than any other single factor. They'll compromise on commute time, on house size, even on neighborhood feel. They don't compromise on schools.

DFW holds 8 of Texas's top 20 school districts, including some of the highest-rated ISDs in the country. Frisco ISD earns an A+ from Niche with a TEA score near the top of the state. Plano ISD, Allen ISD, and Highland Park ISD are consistently ranked among the best in Texas — not just by state metrics but by the outcomes that college-bound families track: AP pass rates, National Merit scholars, post-secondary enrollment.

Houston's suburbs are also home to excellent districts. Katy ISD, Fort Bend ISD (serving Sugar Land and Missouri City), and The Woodlands area schools are genuinely competitive. This isn't a case where one metro has good schools and the other doesn't. But if you're moving from a place with strong public schools and want to land in a district that matches or exceeds that quality, DFW gives you more options at more price points.

Kristen's Take

The families I work with who moved from California are consistently impressed by North Texas school districts — not just the rankings, but the resources. Well-funded programs, low student-teacher ratios, and community investment that you can see in the facilities. That said, I always tell clients: visit the specific campus your kids would attend, not just the district ranking. Frisco ISD has 67 campuses — they're not all identical.

Home Prices & Suburbs: More Purchasing Power in Houston

Let's be direct: Houston is more affordable than Dallas. That gap has widened since 2023. The Houston metro median SFR price sits around $330,000–$335,000 as of early 2026, while the Dallas city median is approximately $492,000 (NTREIS). DFW's top family suburbs push significantly higher — Frisco sits around $692,000 median, Plano around $580,000, and McKinney around $535,000.

Houston's family suburbs — Katy, Sugar Land, The Woodlands, Cypress, Pearland — typically run $350,000–$500,000 for comparable homes. You get more square footage per dollar, and newer construction tends to be more affordable. For families on a tighter budget, that's a real and meaningful advantage.

Property Taxes: Similar Rates, Different Dollar Amounts

Both Texas metros have property taxes that will surprise buyers moving from California's Prop 13 environment. Dallas County's combined effective rate runs approximately 2.22%, while Harris County (Houston) comes in around 2.03%. In practice, because DFW home prices are higher, your actual annual tax bill in Dallas's top suburbs will be larger in dollar terms even if the rates are similar.

Neither metro has state income tax — that's Texas's single biggest financial advantage over California, New York, and Illinois. A household earning $250,000 saves roughly $20,000–$25,000 per year in state income tax versus California. The higher property taxes are real, but the math usually still favors Texas significantly for high-income earners. Texas also offers a homestead exemption on your primary residence that reduces your assessed value for tax purposes.

Weather: The Difference Most People Underestimate

Both cities are hot in summer. That's not where they differ. The real difference is humidity, flooding, and hurricane exposure — and these matter more than most out-of-staters expect when they're making their relocation decision from a kitchen table in California.

Houston averages roughly 239 cloudy or overcast days per year. Dallas averages around 234 sunny or partly cloudy days — effectively the reverse. Houston sits near the Gulf Coast, which means oppressive humidity from May through October. If you've lived in Phoenix or San Diego, Houston summers will feel dramatically different from anything you're used to. Dallas is hot, but it's dry-hot by comparison.

More importantly: Houston has meaningful hurricane risk. Hurricane Harvey in 2017 dropped over 60 inches of rain on parts of the city and caused catastrophic flooding damage. Many Houston neighborhoods flood regularly during heavy rain events — not just during named storms. This affects home insurance costs, flood insurance requirements, and the very real question of what happens to your neighborhood during a bad storm year. Dallas has zero hurricane exposure. You'll deal with occasional ice storms in winter and tornado watches like anywhere in North Texas, but flooding is not a chronic fact of life the way it is in Houston.

Honest Assessment

I've talked to families who moved from Houston to DFW specifically because of flooding anxiety. After Harvey, some of them watched their neighbors lose everything. If you have kids and are choosing between these two metros, the weather risk profile is a real factor — not just a lifestyle preference. Dallas has 100°F summers. Houston has 100°F summers and 18 inches of rain in a weekend.

Safety: Dallas Trending Better, Houston Trending Worse

At the city level, both Dallas and Houston have crime rates above the national average — that's true of most major American cities. What matters for families is the trend direction and the suburban picture.

Dallas's violent crime rate sits at approximately 0.67%, compared to Houston's 1.09%. More significantly, Dallas saw a 44% decline in homicides through 2025, while Houston's homicide rate rose 21% over the same period. That divergence is meaningful. It reflects both policing strategy and underlying neighborhood dynamics.

In the suburbs, both metros look dramatically safer than their city cores. Frisco, Allen, Flower Mound, and Southlake consistently rank among the safest cities in Texas. Houston suburbs like Katy, Sugar Land, and The Woodlands are also safe — genuinely so. Neither metro's suburbs should give families pause on safety grounds. The city-level gap is real; the suburban gap is much smaller.

Getting Around: Both Cities Require a Car

Neither city is walkable in any meaningful sense for suburban living. You will need a car. That's Texas. But DFW has a substantially better public transit infrastructure if that matters for your household.

DART (Dallas Area Rapid Transit) covers 90 miles of light rail with 62 stations serving 13 cities — roughly 96,000 riders on weekdays. Houston's Metro Rail covers 23 miles with 44 stations, serving about 63,000 weekday riders. If anyone in your household commutes into the urban core by transit, DFW's system is more useful for suburban commuters. Both cities have extensive freeway systems, and both have traffic — the DFW Tollway corridor and I-35E handle hundreds of thousands of daily commuters across the metro.

The Honest Verdict: Which City Is Right for Your Family?

After hundreds of relocation conversations, here's how I'd frame it: Dallas is the stronger choice for most families prioritizing schools, safety trajectory, and lower weather risk. Houston is the stronger choice for families in the energy sector or biomedical field, and for families whose budget requires more purchasing power per dollar.

Neither answer is wrong. These are two genuinely excellent metros with distinct identities. The mistake is treating them as interchangeable — they're not.

Choose Dallas–Fort Worth if...
  • Schools are your top priority — DFW has the edge in district depth and consistency
  • Your employer is in tech, finance, healthcare, or corporate management
  • You want to avoid hurricane and flooding risk entirely
  • You have a budget of $450K+ and want access to top-tier suburban communities
  • You're a military family near NAS Fort Worth JRB or a veteran settling post-service
  • You're coming from California and want suburban culture that mirrors what you're used to
Choose Houston if...
  • You work in energy, petrochemical, aerospace, or biomedical research
  • Your budget is under $400K and you want a newer home in a good school district
  • You have family or community ties in Southeast Texas
  • You want proximity to the Gulf Coast for weekend trips
  • You prefer authentic international dining options and a more diverse urban core

I genuinely like Houston. It's a great city. But I know DFW the way you know a place you've actually raised kids in — from the inside. If you're moving from out of state and schools are your first question, the North Texas suburbs are hard to beat.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Dallas or Houston better for families?

Dallas is generally the stronger choice for families prioritizing top-ranked schools and suburban safety. DFW holds 8 of Texas's top 20 school districts and has a declining crime trend. Houston's suburbs (Katy, Sugar Land, The Woodlands) are also excellent and more affordable — but DFW has more top-tier districts at more price points.

Are home prices cheaper in Houston than Dallas?

Yes, meaningfully so. Houston's metro median is around $333,000 versus Dallas city's ~$492,000 (NTREIS 2026). DFW's top family suburbs like Frisco average $692,000. Houston's family suburbs (Katy, Sugar Land) run $350,000–$450,000 — more purchasing power per dollar, though property tax rates are similar.

Which city has better schools — Dallas or Houston?

DFW has a clear overall edge. Frisco ISD, Plano ISD, Allen ISD, and Highland Park ISD are among the top-rated districts in the state. Houston's Katy ISD and Fort Bend ISD are also excellent, but DFW has more top-ranked districts concentrated in a smaller geographic area — giving families more suburban choices without sacrificing school quality.

Is Dallas safer than Houston?

At the city level, yes. Dallas has a violent crime rate of 0.67% vs Houston's 1.09%, and Dallas homicides declined 44% in 2025 while Houston's rose 21%. Both cities' suburbs are significantly safer than their cores — Frisco, Allen, and Flower Mound rank among Texas's safest cities, and Houston suburbs like Katy are similarly safe.

Does Dallas or Houston have better weather for families?

Dallas is generally preferred by families moving from out of state. Dallas gets about 30 more sunny days per year, significantly lower humidity, and zero hurricane risk. Houston's flooding exposure (Harvey deposited 60+ inches of rain in 2017) is a real factor for homeowners. Both cities are hot in summer — Dallas is dry-hot, Houston is humid-hot.

Are property taxes higher in Dallas or Houston?

Dallas County's combined rate (~2.22%) is slightly higher than Harris County/Houston (~2.03%), but both are in a similar range. Because DFW homes cost more, the annual dollar amount will typically be higher in Dallas's top suburbs. Texas's homestead exemption reduces assessed value on your primary residence in both metros.
