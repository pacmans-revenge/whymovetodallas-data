Dallas vs. Houston: Which Texas City Is Actually Better for Families?

Kristen Carpentier Licensed TX Realtor®  ·  Updated May 30, 2026  ·  11 min read

Every week I talk to families who've narrowed their Texas move to two cities: Dallas or Houston. Some are coming from California, New York, or Illinois — exhausted by high taxes and high prices. Others are already in Texas — Houston or Austin — and weighing whether DFW is the upgrade they're looking for. Either way, the question is the same: which metro actually fits your family?

I've spent nearly 15 years in DFW as a mom, a Realtor, and the wife of an Army Veteran. I know the Dallas suburbs the way you'd know a neighborhood you've actually raised kids in — not from a data sheet. But I also know Houston well enough to tell you what the data misses. This guide gives you the honest comparison, because picking the wrong metro is a mistake that's hard to undo.

Watch: Dallas vs. Houston head-to-head — schools, suburbs, cost of living, and the honest trade-offs for relocating families.

Schools & Education: Where DFW Has a Clear Edge

If you have school-age kids, this is the section that matters most. I say that not as a sales pitch for Dallas — I say it because it's what my clients ultimately care about more than any other single factor. They'll compromise on commute time, on house size, even on neighborhood feel. They don't compromise on schools.

DFW holds 8 of Texas's top 20 school districts, including some of the highest-rated ISDs in the country. (See our full DFW school district comparison guide for side-by-side ratings and boundary maps.) Frisco ISD ranks #31 out of 961 Texas districts, earns a 5-star rating from SchoolDigger, and posts a 98% graduation rate. Allen ISD earns an A+ rating and ranked #6 in Texas on Niche's 2026 list. Plano ISD and Highland Park ISD round out a cluster of elite districts concentrated in a surprisingly small geographic area — all within about 30 miles of each other.

Houston's suburbs are also home to excellent districts. Katy ISD ranks in Texas's top tier nationally and draws serious comparison to Frisco ISD in academic outcomes. Fort Bend ISD (serving Sugar Land and Missouri City) has several highly-rated campuses — particularly in the Sugar Land area — making it one of the stronger Houston-area districts for families. The Woodlands area schools are genuinely strong. This isn't a case where one metro has good schools and the other doesn't. But if you're moving from a place with strong public schools and want to land in a district that matches or exceeds that quality, DFW gives you more top-rated options concentrated in a smaller geographic area — meaning you can find excellent schools without pushing 45 miles from the employment core.

Kristen's Take

The families I work with who moved from California are consistently impressed by North Texas school districts — not just the rankings, but the resources. Well-funded programs, low student-teacher ratios, and community investment that you can see in the facilities. That said, I always tell clients: visit the specific campus your kids would attend, not just the district ranking. Frisco ISD has 67 campuses — they're not all identical.

Home Prices & Suburbs: More Purchasing Power in Houston

Let's be direct: Houston is more affordable than Dallas. That gap has widened since 2023. The Houston metro median SFR price sits around $330,000–$335,000 as of early 2026, while the Dallas city median is approximately $492,000 (NTREIS). DFW's top family suburbs push significantly higher — Frisco sits around $692,000 median, Plano around $500,000, and McKinney around $510,000.

Houston's family suburbs — Katy, Sugar Land, The Woodlands, Cypress, Pearland — offer a wide price range. Katy runs ~$340K citywide (with master-planned ZIPs like 77494 running higher), while The Woodlands skews much higher at ~$627K as Houston's premier master-planned suburb. For most families, Katy and Pearland (~$375K) provide the best purchasing power per dollar. DFW also has strong new construction options in outer suburbs like Prosper, Celina, and Aubrey — see our DFW new construction guide for current builder inventory.

Property Taxes: Similar Rates, Different Dollar Amounts

Both Texas metros have property taxes that will surprise buyers moving from California's Prop 13 environment. Dallas County's combined effective rate runs approximately 2.22%, while Harris County (Houston) comes in around 2.03%. In practice, because DFW home prices are higher, your actual annual tax bill in Dallas's top suburbs will be larger in dollar terms even if the rates are similar.

Neither metro has state income tax — that's Texas's single biggest financial advantage over California, New York, and Illinois. A household earning $250,000 could save roughly $20,000+ per year in state income tax versus California (amount varies by filing status and taxable income). The higher property taxes are real, but the math usually still favors Texas significantly for high-income earners. Texas also offers a homestead exemption on your primary residence that reduces your assessed value for tax purposes.

Weather: The Difference Most People Underestimate

Both cities are hot in summer. That's not where they differ. The real difference is humidity, flooding, and hurricane exposure — and these matter more than most out-of-staters expect when they're making their relocation decision from a kitchen table in California.

Houston averages roughly 239 cloudy or overcast days per year. Dallas averages around 234 sunny or partly cloudy days — effectively the reverse. Houston sits near the Gulf Coast, which means oppressive humidity from May through October. If you've lived in Phoenix or San Diego, Houston summers will feel dramatically different from anything you're used to. Dallas is hot, but it's dry-hot by comparison.

More importantly: Houston has meaningful hurricane risk. Hurricane Harvey in 2017 dropped over 60 inches of rain on parts of the city and caused catastrophic flooding damage. Many Houston neighborhoods flood regularly during heavy rain events — not just during named storms. This affects home insurance costs, flood insurance requirements, and the very real question of what happens to your neighborhood during a bad storm year. Dallas has zero hurricane exposure. You'll deal with occasional ice storms in winter and tornado watches like anywhere in North Texas, but flooding is not a chronic fact of life the way it is in Houston.

Honest Assessment

I've talked to families who moved from Houston to DFW specifically because of flooding anxiety. After Harvey, some of them watched their neighbors lose everything. If you have kids and are choosing between these two metros, the weather risk profile is a real factor — not just a lifestyle preference. Dallas has 100°F summers. Houston has 100°F summers and 18 inches of rain in a weekend.

DFW Hidden Cost: Hail & Homeowner Insurance

Dallas avoids hurricanes — but North Texas sits squarely in Hail Alley, one of the most hail-active regions in the country. Texas homeowner insurance averages $4,000–$5,500 per year (roughly 3× the national average), and DFW premiums have risen 20–40% since 2021 as insurers reprice wind and hail risk. Most Texas policies also carry a separate wind/hail deductible of 1–2% of insured value — so a hail claim on a $500K home triggers a $5,000–$10,000 out-of-pocket cost before coverage kicks in. Budget for this on both sides of the comparison. Texas weather is more expensive to insure than most out-of-state buyers expect, and that's true in Dallas and Houston alike.

Safety: Dallas Has Lower Violent Crime; Both Cities Improved in 2025

At the city level, both Dallas and Houston have crime rates above the national average — that's true of most major American cities. What matters for families is the relative rate and the suburban picture.

Dallas's violent crime rate is meaningfully lower than Houston's at the city core level. In 2025, both metros made progress: Dallas murders fell 12% year-over-year (43 fewer homicides), while Houston homicides declined 17% — from 331 in 2024 to 272 in 2025. Neither city is trending in the wrong direction, which is worth noting honestly.

In the suburbs, both metros look dramatically safer than their city cores. Frisco, Allen, Flower Mound, and Southlake consistently rank among the safest cities in Texas. Houston suburbs like Katy, Sugar Land, and The Woodlands are also safe — genuinely so. Neither metro's suburbs should give families pause on safety grounds. The city-level gap is real; the suburban gap is much smaller.

Getting Around: Both Cities Require a Car

Neither city is walkable in any meaningful sense for suburban living. You will need a car. But the commute reality looks very different depending on which specific suburb you choose — and that should factor into your final decision.

In DFW: south Plano to downtown Dallas runs 20–25 minutes; Allen is 30–38 minutes via US-75; Frisco is 35–45 minutes on the tollway; McKinney is 45–60+ minutes in morning traffic. On the Houston side: Pearland to the Medical Center is 20–40 minutes; Sugar Land to downtown is 30–50 minutes; Katy to downtown is 30–45 minutes (and only 15 minutes to the Energy Corridor); The Woodlands is 45–60 minutes in traffic. Both metros have comparable commute profiles from their outer suburbs — the real question is which employment hub you're heading toward.

DART (Dallas Area Rapid Transit) covers 90 miles of light rail with 62 stations serving 13 cities — roughly 96,000 weekday riders. If you live in Plano or Richardson and work downtown, DART is a legitimate option. Houston's Metro Rail covers about 22.7 miles with roughly 39 stations and about 63,000 daily riders — useful for the Medical Center and Midtown corridors. For most suburban commuters in both metros, though, the car is the daily reality. Budget $150–$250/month in tolls if you're regularly on the Dallas North Tollway or SH-121 in DFW; Houston's toll infrastructure is comparable. Factor that into your cost-of-living math alongside the home price difference.

The Honest Verdict: Which City Is Right for Your Family?

After hundreds of relocation conversations, here's how I'd frame it: Dallas is the stronger choice for most families prioritizing schools, safety trajectory, and lower weather risk. Houston is the stronger choice for families in the energy sector or biomedical field, and for families whose budget requires more purchasing power per dollar.

Neither answer is wrong. These are two genuinely excellent metros with distinct identities. The mistake is treating them as interchangeable — they're not.

Choose Dallas–Fort Worth if...
  • Schools are your top priority — DFW has the edge in district depth and consistency
  • Your employer is in tech, finance, healthcare, or corporate management
  • You want to avoid hurricane and flooding risk entirely
  • You have a budget of $450K+ and want access to top-tier suburban communities
  • You're a military family near NAS Fort Worth JRB or a veteran settling post-service
  • You're coming from California and want suburban culture that mirrors what you're used to
Choose Houston if...
  • You work in energy, petrochemical, aerospace, or biomedical research
  • Your budget is under $400K and you want a newer home in a good school district
  • You have family or community ties in Southeast Texas
  • You want proximity to the Gulf Coast for weekend trips
  • You prefer authentic international dining options and a more diverse urban core

I genuinely like Houston. It's a great city. But I know DFW the way you know a place you've actually raised kids in — from the inside. If you're moving from out of state and schools are your first question, the North Texas suburbs are hard to beat.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Dallas or Houston better for families?

Dallas is generally the stronger choice for families prioritizing top-ranked schools and suburban safety. DFW holds 8 of Texas's top 20 school districts and has a declining crime trend. Houston's suburbs (Katy, Sugar Land, The Woodlands) are also excellent and more affordable — but DFW has more top-tier districts at more price points.

Are home prices cheaper in Houston than Dallas?

Yes, meaningfully so. Houston's metro median is around $333,000 versus Dallas city's ~$492,000 (NTREIS 2026). DFW's top family suburbs like Frisco average $692,000. Houston's family suburbs (Katy, Sugar Land) run $350,000–$450,000 — more purchasing power per dollar, though property tax rates are similar.

Which city has better schools — Dallas or Houston?

DFW has a clear overall edge. Frisco ISD, Plano ISD, Allen ISD, and Highland Park ISD are among the top-rated districts in the state. Houston's Katy ISD and Fort Bend ISD are also excellent, but DFW has more top-ranked districts concentrated in a smaller geographic area — giving families more suburban choices without sacrificing school quality.

Is Dallas safer than Houston?

Dallas has a lower city-level violent crime rate than Houston. In 2025, both cities improved: Dallas murders fell 12% year-over-year and Houston homicides declined 17% (from 331 in 2024 to 272 in 2025). Both cities' suburbs are dramatically safer than their cores — Frisco, Allen, and Flower Mound rank among Texas's safest cities, and Houston suburbs like Katy are similarly safe.

Does Dallas or Houston have better weather for families?

Dallas is generally preferred by families moving from out of state. Dallas gets about 30 more sunny days per year than Houston (234 vs. 204 annually), significantly lower humidity, and zero hurricane risk. Houston's flooding exposure — Harvey deposited 60+ inches of rain in 2017 — is a real factor for homeowners. Both cities are hot in summer: Dallas is dry-hot, Houston is humid-hot.

Are property taxes higher in Dallas or Houston?

Dallas County's combined rate (~2.22%) is slightly higher than Harris County/Houston (~2.03%), but both are in a similar range. Because DFW homes cost more, the annual dollar amount will typically be higher in Dallas's top suburbs. Texas's homestead exemption reduces assessed value on your primary residence in both metros.

Which city is better for raising a family — Dallas or Houston?

Dallas–Fort Worth is generally the stronger pick for families who prioritize public school quality, lower crime trajectory, and no hurricane or flooding risk. Houston is better for families in the energy, aerospace, or medical sectors, and for those who need more purchasing power per dollar. Both metros have excellent family suburbs — the right answer depends on your industry, your school priorities, and your budget.

Can I enroll my kids in school before we close on a home in DFW?

Most Texas school districts require proof of residency — a lease, closing disclosure, or utility bill — before finalizing enrollment. In practice, many districts will accept a signed purchase contract or lease as a pending address to start the intake process. When you arrive, bring birth certificates, immunization records, prior grade transcripts, proof of address, and parent ID. Allow 1–2 weeks for mid-year enrollment. Most importantly: verify your specific campus before you make an offer. Living in Frisco ISD doesn't mean you get the campus you toured — your assigned school depends on your exact address within the district's attendance zones, not just the district name.

Does it matter which neighborhood I buy in within a DFW school district?

More than most families realize. Texas schools use address-based attendance zoning — your home address determines which specific elementary, middle, and high school your kids attend. In large districts like Frisco ISD (67 campuses) or McKinney ISD, individual campus culture and program offerings can vary. I run an attendance zone check for every client before they commit to a neighborhood. All DFW ISDs publish zone maps on their websites — search "[district name] attendance zone lookup" to find the tool. Confirm the specific campuses for your target address before making an offer.
