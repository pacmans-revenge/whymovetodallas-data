Best Suburbs for Families in DFW: Matched to Your Budget, Schools & Lifestyle

By Kristen Carpentier, Licensed Texas Realtor®  ·  May 23, 2026  ·  10 min read

The question I hear most from families moving to DFW is some version of this: "We've heard Frisco is great — but is it right for us?" Sometimes the answer is yes. But I've worked with enough families to know that the "best" suburb in Dallas-Fort Worth isn't a fixed answer. It depends on where you're coming from, where you'll be working, what your kids need, and honestly, how much house you can get for your money in Texas versus what you're leaving behind.

I'm Kristen Carpentier — a licensed Texas Realtor® (TREC #760457), mom of four, and wife of an Army Veteran. I've raised my family in DFW, and I've helped relocating families find the right suburb from California, New York, Illinois, and beyond. This guide covers seven of the strongest family suburbs in North DFW, with real school data, real prices, and the honest trade-offs I'd tell any parent sitting across the table from me.

What Families Actually Need to Compare

Most "best suburbs" lists rank cities against each other using aggregate data — school ratings, crime stats, median income. That's a starting point, but it doesn't answer the question a relocating parent actually needs answered: which suburb puts my kids in a great school, keeps me within a reasonable commute of my job, and doesn't blow the budget I've set?

Before you fall in love with a suburb on paper, I'd encourage you to think through four things. First, your commute corridor — the suburb that's 20 minutes from your employer's campus is far more livable than the one that's 45 minutes away, even if the schools rate slightly higher. Second, your school-age windows — families with elementary-age kids have more flexibility to move later than families with a sophomore in high school who needs to finish where they started. Third, your price ceiling — "affordable" in DFW is relative, and what you get for $550K varies considerably between suburbs. And fourth, the pace of life — some DFW suburbs feel like a small town that happens to be growing; others have every restaurant chain imaginable and a packed youth sports calendar every weekend. Neither is wrong. They're just different.

With that context in mind, here's the data on seven of North DFW's strongest family suburbs.

Frisco

Best Overall
A+Frisco ISD
$681KMedian SFR
B–Safety (Niche)
210KPopulation

Frisco is the suburb that most families have heard of before they start their DFW search, and there's a real reason for that. Frisco ISD earns an A+ from Niche and consistently ranks among the top ten school districts in all of Texas. The city itself has grown to over 210,000 residents and offers an infrastructure that most fast-growing suburbs take a decade to catch up to — major hospital systems, extensive park and trail networks, The Star at Frisco (the Dallas Cowboys' headquarters and entertainment district), Legacy West just south in Plano, and a dining and retail scene that gives you almost no reason to drive into Dallas.

The trade-off is price. At a median SFR of $681K, Frisco is the most expensive of the non-luxury suburbs in this guide. You're not paying Southlake prices, but you're paying a meaningful premium over Allen or McKinney. Months supply sits around 4.5, which means the market is roughly balanced — you have negotiating room in a way you don't in Coppell or Flower Mound. If your employer is near The Star, the DNT corridor, or Legacy West, Frisco's commute story is hard to beat. If you're heading to Fort Worth daily, it's a different calculus.

Choose Frisco if…
  • School ratings are your top priority and you want the most name-recognition ISD in DFW
  • Your employer is at The Star, along the DNT, or in the Legacy West/Plano corridor
  • You want the widest selection of dining, shopping, and youth sports programs in North DFW
  • Your budget is $620K–$900K and you want an established neighborhood over new construction

Allen

Best Value — A+ Schools
A+Allen ISD
$520KMedian SFR
BSafety (Niche)
108KPopulation

Allen is the suburb I show families when they love Frisco on paper but wince at the price tag. Allen ISD earns the same A+ from Niche as Frisco ISD — and while rankings between them shift slightly year to year, both are consistently among the best districts in Texas. The academic outcomes are comparable. The price difference is not. At a median SFR of $520K, Allen runs roughly $160K less than Frisco for a similar quality home.

The city itself is smaller and a bit quieter — which some families find exactly right and others find limiting. Allen has a strong youth sports culture, a well-regarded rec center and natatorium, and easy access to US-75 (Central Expressway) for commuters heading south toward Dallas or north toward McKinney. Safety grades are solid at a B from Niche. One honest note: Allen is a more established city, which means the new-construction inventory is thinner than in Frisco or Prosper — you're more likely to find a resale home here.

Choose Allen if…
  • You want an A+ school district without Frisco's price premium
  • You're commuting via US-75 — Allen is one of the best-positioned suburbs for the 75 corridor
  • Your family prefers a quieter, more established community feel over a fast-growing boomtown
  • Your budget is $480K–$650K and you want more home for the money

Flower Mound

Best for Outdoor Families
ALewisville ISD
$619KMedian SFR
B+Safety (Niche)
78KPopulation

Flower Mound earns the best safety grade in this guide — a B+ from Niche — and it's the suburb that consistently comes up when DFW families describe where they feel most at home. Lake Grapevine sits right on the edge of town, giving residents direct access to boating, paddleboarding, and hiking trails without getting in the car and driving anywhere significant. The Flower Mound Trail System winds through much of the city. This is not a suburb that feels like it was poured from a development mold — there's an older, more rooted character to its neighborhoods that the newer planned communities north of Frisco are still building toward.

Lewisville ISD earns an A from Niche — genuinely excellent, though one grade below the A+ districts in Frisco, Allen, Coppell, and Southlake. For most families, that distinction is minor. The practical reality is that Flower Mound sits in a pocket that's roughly equidistant from DFW Airport, the Las Colinas/Irving employment corridor, and the Denton/US-377 corridor — which makes it a strong commuter choice for families with jobs spread across the western half of the metroplex. One honest trade-off: Flower Mound is car-dependent in a way that even other DFW suburbs aren't — a Walk Score of 5. If you want to walk to anything, this is not your suburb.

Choose Flower Mound if…
  • Safety and a nature-connected lifestyle are your top priorities
  • You want lake access and trail systems as part of daily life, not a weekend trip
  • Your employer is at DFW Airport, in Las Colinas/Irving, or along the SH-121/114 corridor
  • You prefer an established neighborhood with character over a brand-new master-planned community

McKinney

Best for Space & Character
AMcKinney ISD
$496KMedian SFR
B–Safety (Niche)
205KPopulation

McKinney is the most affordable suburb in this guide — and it's one of those places where the price doesn't tell the full story. At a median SFR of $496K, McKinney offers significantly more square footage and lot size for the money than any comparable suburb on this list. McKinney ISD earns an A from Niche — not A+, but genuinely excellent, with a growing campus infrastructure to support the city's rapid expansion. If you're moving from a high-cost market and your budget feels like it won't get you much in DFW, McKinney is usually where that feeling changes.

What distinguishes McKinney from other fast-growing suburbs is that it has a real downtown. McKinney's historic district on the square — original storefronts, independent restaurants, weekend farmers markets — gives the city a sense of identity that newer master-planned communities are still trying to build. The trade-offs are honest ones: the new growth areas are far from downtown, and days on market run around 42 days, the slowest in this guide, which reflects a buyer market with more negotiating room. Commutes to downtown Dallas run 35–45 minutes via US-75, which is manageable but the longest in the group for those working in the city core.

Choose McKinney if…
  • Budget is your primary constraint and you want the most home per dollar in a quality school district
  • You value authentic neighborhood character alongside new development
  • You're commuting via US-75 or work remotely and don't need a fast commute every day
  • Your budget is $430K–$620K and you want room to negotiate

Southlake

Best for Top Schools, No Compromises
A+Carroll ISD
$1.35MMedian SFR
BSafety (Niche)
32KPopulation

Southlake is in a category of its own. Carroll ISD — the district serving most of Southlake — consistently earns an A+ from Niche and is widely considered one of the top five school districts in Texas. The district is small by DFW standards (around 9,000 students) and academically intense, with high AP participation rates and college acceptance outcomes that rival prep schools. Southlake Town Square is one of the most well-designed mixed-use developments in North Texas — walkable retail, restaurants, and a genuine gathering place that most suburban downtowns never manage to create. It's the suburb that families from coastal cities often describe as feeling most familiar.

The price of entry is real: at a median SFR of $1.35M, Southlake is roughly double the median of every other suburb in this guide. The market moves fast — days on market average 23 days, and months supply sits around 4.2. If you're relocating on a corporate package from New York or a tech company exit from California, Southlake is often the target. If you're stretching to get there on a $900K budget, you may find yourself in a home that's smaller than what the same price buys you in Frisco or Coppell. I'd rather you be honest with yourself about that trade-off before falling in love with the Carroll ISD test scores.

Choose Southlake if…
  • Carroll ISD's academic outcomes are non-negotiable for your family
  • Your budget is $1M+ and you want a walkable, aesthetically cohesive suburb
  • Your employer is near DFW Airport, in Las Colinas, or Fort Worth — Southlake's midpoint location is a genuine advantage
  • You're relocating from a high-cost city and the price feels proportionate to what you're coming from

Coppell

Best for the DFW Airport Corridor
A+Coppell ISD
$625KMedian SFR
BSafety (Niche)
42KPopulation

Coppell is the suburb that surprises most families who haven't heard of it. Coppell ISD earns an A+ from Niche and is frequently mentioned in the same breath as Carroll ISD and Frisco ISD among Texas parents who have done serious research. The city is small — around 42,000 people — and feels nothing like the sprawling master-planned communities to the north. Neighborhoods are established and well-maintained. The community has a reputation among DFW parents for being genuinely engaged: high parent involvement in schools, strong youth sports leagues, and a community identity that's built around families staying put rather than upgrading and moving on.

The location story is what puts Coppell on a short list for a specific type of family. At a median SFR of $625K, you're paying Frisco-area prices — but you're doing it in a suburb where DFW International Airport is a 10-minute drive, Las Colinas/Irving is 15 minutes, and both Dallas and Fort Worth are under 30 minutes. For families where one or both parents travel frequently, or where a job at American Airlines, ExxonMobil, or a Las Colinas employer drives the relocation, Coppell's position in the metro is almost uniquely efficient. Months supply at 2.5 means this market moves fast — homes sell quickly and at price.

Choose Coppell if…
  • One or both parents travel frequently — proximity to DFW Airport is a genuine quality-of-life advantage
  • You want an A+ school district in a smaller, tighter-knit community
  • Your employer is in Las Colinas, Irving, or along SH-121
  • Your budget is $580K–$800K and you want a well-established neighborhood with real community identity

APlano ISD
$525KMedian SFR
92Walk Score

Plano is the only suburb in this guide with a Walk Score of 92 — and that single number reflects a genuinely different lifestyle from every other option here. The Legacy West development in west Plano has fundamentally changed the suburb's character: it's a walkable, mixed-use district with major corporate headquarters (JPMorgan Chase, Toyota, Liberty Mutual, FedEx, JCPenney), a hotel, restaurants, and shops all within walking distance of each other. If you're relocating for a job at one of those companies, living nearby means no car commute — a real rarity in North Texas. Plano ISD earns a solid A from Niche, and the district serves one of the most educated communities in the state.

At $525K median, Plano's pricing is comparable to Allen — but it buys you a very different product. You're more likely to find a 1990s–2000s home on a smaller lot in an established neighborhood than a new-construction home in a master-planned community. The suburbs further north (Frisco, McKinney, Allen) often pull families who want newer construction; Plano pulls families who want proximity to employment centers, a more urban feel, and the ability to get places without a car. The trade-off is that new inventory is thin and prices per square foot run higher than you'd expect relative to the median.

Choose Plano if…
  • Your employer is at Legacy West — Toyota, JPMorgan Chase, or other major corporate campuses
  • You want the most walkable DFW suburb in this guide and the ability to live without a car for daily errands
  • You prefer an established neighborhood with amenity-rich surroundings over new construction
  • Your budget is $480K–$700K and proximity to employment is more important than new-build finishes

How to Choose: A Simple Framework

Seven good suburbs is still seven choices. Here's how I help families narrow it down in one conversation.

Step 1: Set Your Real Budget

Not the top of your approval range — your comfortable monthly payment after Texas property taxes (1.6%–2.3% effective rate depending on suburb and ISD). That number eliminates a lot of the confusion. At $500K all-in, you're looking at Allen, McKinney, and Plano. At $650K, Frisco, Flower Mound, and Coppell open up. At $1M+, Southlake becomes realistic. Be honest about this before you fall in love with a suburb.

If your budget is $430K–$580K — start here
  • McKinney — most space per dollar, A ISD, negotiating room in the market
  • Allen — A+ ISD at the lowest price point for top-tier schools
  • Plano — walkability, Legacy West proximity, A ISD
If your budget is $580K–$800K — consider these
  • Frisco — A+ ISD, most amenities, DNT and Legacy West corridor
  • Flower Mound — best safety, outdoor lifestyle, DFW/Las Colinas corridor
  • Coppell — A+ ISD, fast airport access, tight-knit community feel
If your budget is $1M+ — there's only one answer
  • Southlake — Carroll ISD, Southlake Town Square, DFW midpoint location
Step 2: Map Your Commute First

Before you fall for a suburb, Google Maps it on a Tuesday morning at 8 AM. Not the no-traffic estimate — the real one. DFW traffic is directional. Heading south on US-75 from Allen to downtown Dallas is different from heading west on SH-183 from Flower Mound to Las Colinas. I've worked with families who chose the right suburb for the wrong commute and spent the first year regretting it. Map it before you commit.

A Note for Families Moving from California or New York

$0 Texas State Income Tax
$18K+ Annual Savings at $200K Income (vs. CA)
~1.8% Typical DFW Effective Property Tax Rate

The number one financial question I get from California families is about property taxes — because Texas property taxes are genuinely higher than most states, and it matters. A home at $625K in Coppell carries an estimated property tax bill of $10,000–$11,500 per year (1.7%–1.85% effective rate, depending on ISD and any applicable exemptions). In California, Proposition 13 caps rates at 1% of assessed value — so a comparable home there might carry $6,250 per year if it hasn't been reassessed recently. That gap is real and I won't sugarcoat it.

What changes the math: Texas has no state income tax. A family earning $200,000 in California pays roughly $16,000–$19,000 per year to the state. In Texas, that's zero. For most California families, the income tax savings more than offset the property tax difference — often by a meaningful margin. New York families see a similar calculation. The other piece is housing cost: a $525K home in Plano or McKinney buys substantially more square footage than a $1.5M–$2M home in a comparable Bay Area suburb. Most families I work with from California or New York feel financially reset within the first year.

Kristen's Take

The families who struggle with the Texas move aren't the ones who miss California weather or New York pizza — they adjust to those things quickly. The ones who struggle are the ones who didn't research the suburbs carefully and ended up in a community that doesn't fit their family's rhythm. That's what this guide is for: matching you to the right suburb before you buy, so the first year feels like a win.

If you want a personalized number — what the income tax savings and property tax difference actually look like for your specific income and target price — that's the kind of thing I walk through on a 30-minute call. No pressure, no pitch. Just the math, so you can make a confident decision.

Frequently Asked Questions

Four suburbs in this guide have A+ ISDs: Frisco (Frisco ISD), Allen (Allen ISD), Coppell (Coppell ISD), and Southlake (Carroll ISD). Carroll ISD is widely considered the most academically intense, with the highest AP rates and college acceptance outcomes. Frisco ISD is the largest A+ district and the most well-known. Allen ISD delivers A+ outcomes at a lower price point than Frisco or Southlake.

Allen and McKinney offer the best combination of school quality and affordability. Allen ISD earns an A+ from Niche with a median SFR around $520K — roughly $160K less than Frisco for a comparable school rating. McKinney offers an A-rated ISD with the lowest median price in this guide at $496K, plus more square footage per dollar and a market with real negotiating room.

Both have A+ ISDs and strong family infrastructure. Frisco is larger, newer, and has more dining, shopping, and entertainment built out — at a $160K price premium over Allen. Allen is quieter, more established, and better positioned for US-75 commuters. If school ratings are equal and budget matters, Allen wins on value. If you want the most developed suburb amenity-wise, Frisco wins.

For a $500K–$600K home (Allen, McKinney, Plano range), a household income of $130,000–$160,000 supports a comfortable mortgage plus Texas property taxes without strain. For Frisco and Coppell at $620K–$700K, $160,000–$190,000 is a more comfortable range. Southlake at $1.35M median realistically requires $250,000+ household income. Remember: no Texas state income tax means your take-home pay is higher than in California or New York at the same gross salary.

Effective property tax rates across these suburbs typically run 1.6%–2.1% of appraised value, depending on the ISD, city, and county. On a $600K home, that's roughly $9,600–$12,600 per year. Texas has a homestead exemption (currently $100,000 off your school district taxable value) that reduces the effective rate for primary residences. These rates are higher than California's Prop 13 cap but lower than most Northeast states.

If Carroll ISD's academic profile is your top priority and your budget supports $1M+, Southlake is genuinely worth it. The district is among the best in Texas, Southlake Town Square offers a walkability that no other suburb in this guide can match, and the suburb's midpoint location between Dallas and Fort Worth is a real commute advantage. If you're stretching to get there, the value-per-dollar is harder to justify versus Frisco or Coppell at half the price.
