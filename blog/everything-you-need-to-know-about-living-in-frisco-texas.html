Everything You Need to Know Before You Move

If you've spent any time researching DFW suburbs, Frisco has probably come up more than once. And for good reason — it's one of the most talked-about cities in North Texas, and has been for the past 15 years. But "talked about" and "right for your family" are two different things entirely.

I've helped hundreds of families relocate to DFW, and Frisco comes up on nearly every shortlist. Some of those families buy there and love it. Others do the research, understand the trade-offs, and choose somewhere that fits their budget or commute better. My job isn't to sell you on Frisco — it's to make sure you understand it completely before you make a decision.

Here's everything you actually need to know.

How Frisco Became North Texas's Most In-Demand Suburb

In 1990, Frisco was a town of 6,000 people with more farmland than subdivisions. By 2010 it had 116,000 residents. Today it's home to over 245,000 — making it one of the fastest-growing cities in American history over a sustained period. That growth story explains almost everything about what Frisco is and isn't today.

What drove it? Schools, jobs, and infrastructure arriving at roughly the same time. Frisco ISD built campuses as quickly as the neighborhoods went in. The Dallas North Tollway extended north, making commutes to the Legacy West and Plano corridor fast and predictable. Major employers followed — T-Mobile, the Dallas Cowboys, PGA of America, Toyota. Then came the amenities that typically only arrive in established cities: world-class sports venues, a genuine dining scene, and master-planned communities with resort-style amenities that most suburbs only promise.

The result is a city that, in 30 years, went from a rural North Texas farming community — straddling what is now both Collin and Denton counties — to one of the most complete family ecosystems in North Texas. That's not marketing copy — it's why the families I work with keep putting it on their shortlist even when the price tags give them pause. The question isn't whether Frisco is good. It's whether it's the right fit for your family specifically.

Schools — Frisco ISD and the Critical ISD Split Issue

For most of the families I work with, schools drive the suburb decision. And Frisco ISD is genuinely outstanding. It earns an A+ from Niche across every category — overall, academics, college prep, and families. The Texas Education Agency rated it an A with a score of 90/100. GreatSchools rates it "above average." The district serves approximately 62,755 students (as of fall 2025) across 77 campuses, with multiple high school options that give students more access to AP programs, athletics, and arts than the mega-campuses you'll find in some other districts.

Reedy High School earns a 9/10 from GreatSchools and consistently appears on state rankings for academic performance. Independence High earns an 8/10. The district runs 12 high schools, each with its own feeder pattern — which is part of why buying in the right community matters so much for school assignment.

⚠️ The ISD Split — Read This Before You Buy

Here's what trips up families more than anything else in Frisco: not every Frisco address is zoned to Frisco ISD. Frisco is a split-ISD city, meaning different neighborhoods serve different school districts — and the differences matter.

Area of FriscoSchool DistrictNiche Grade
Central & South Frisco (majority of the city) Frisco ISD A+
North & Northwest Frisco (Hwy 380 corridor + beyond) Prosper ISD A+
Northeast Frisco (near McKinney border) McKinney ISD A
Far Southeast Frisco (near Allen city limits) Allen ISD A+

The good news is that all four districts are strong — this isn't a situation where one is clearly inferior. But if you have your heart set on Frisco ISD specifically, you need to verify zoning by exact property address before you make an offer, not after. I've seen families fall in love with a house only to discover it's zoned to a different district than they expected. Always verify with the district directly or by entering the address on the district's campus locator.

Kristen's Take

All four districts in this table are genuinely excellent — Prosper ISD in particular is one of the fastest-rising districts in Texas. If the specific campus your child attends matters more than the district name, Frisco's ISD split is less of an issue than it seems. But if you're committed to Frisco ISD by name, stick to central and south Frisco neighborhoods and verify every address before you get attached.

What Does It Actually Cost to Live in Frisco?

Frisco is not cheap — and anyone who suggests otherwise hasn't priced it recently. At a median SFR price of $681,000, it sits at the upper end of the DFW market. Niche gives it a B- for cost of living, which, in DFW context, means it's materially more expensive than most comparable suburbs. If you're coming from Dallas proper, Frisco's price premium is real.

If you're coming from the Bay Area, Los Angeles, New York, or Chicago, though, the math looks entirely different. Homes are roughly half the price of equivalent Bay Area properties. And Texas has no state income tax — which means a household earning $250,000 that moves from California is looking at $23,000+ in annual income tax savings before touching a single housing cost.

The California comparison often surprises my clients. Yes, Frisco's property taxes are higher than what California homeowners pay on their assessed value under Prop 13. For a family earning $250K/year and buying a $681K home: income tax savings alone typically run $20,000–$30,000 annually, which more than covers the annual property tax bill.

Commuting from Frisco — The DNT/121 Advantage

Frisco's location on the Dallas North Tollway (DNT) and SH-121 axis is one of its most underrated features. This is the spine of North Texas corporate activity — Toyota, JPMorgan, Liberty Mutual, Oracle, and Charles Schwab all have major facilities within 10–12 miles of central Frisco. Most of my Frisco clients don't commute to Downtown Dallas — they commute south to Plano and Legacy West, which on the DNT runs 20–25 minutes most mornings.

Budget for $100–$180/month in toll costs if you're on the DNT daily. But compared to I-405 in Los Angeles or I-95 in New Jersey, Frisco commutes are still — genuinely — manageable.

What Makes Frisco Different — The Star, PGA, and Pro Sports Culture

Every suburb in North Texas has good schools and well-maintained parks. What Frisco has that virtually no other suburb in the country can match is this: world-class professional sports infrastructure that's genuinely integrated into family life.

The Star in Frisco is the Dallas Cowboys' world headquarters — a 91-acre campus housing the team's training facility, the Ford Center indoor arena, a hotel, restaurants, and entertainment venues. The campus includes the Tostitos Championship Plaza, a 50-yard replica turf field that's open to the public for youth sports, community events, and pickup games. The Cowboys' own practice field isn't available for public play, but having that level of infrastructure in your backyard shapes community culture here in a way that's genuinely hard to find anywhere else.

PGA Frisco opened in 2023 and immediately became one of the premier public golf destinations in the country — two championship courses (Fields Ranch East and Fields Ranch West), a par-3 short course, putting course, and the PGA Works training facility. It's open to the public. Junior golf programming starts young and is run by PGA professionals. For families who golf, this alone is a deciding factor.

Toyota Stadium is home to FC Dallas (MLS) and frequent collegiate events. Riders Field hosts the RoughRiders, the Rangers' AA affiliate. Stonebriar Centre handles the major retail anchors; the dining scene along Preston Road and near Legacy West has grown dramatically with the corporate population. Frisco also has a growing cultural presence — the National Videogame Museum, TrainTopia, the Frisco Heritage Museum, and the Museum of the American Railroad are all here.

For families moving from the Bay Area who are accustomed to world-class infrastructure, Frisco is the North Texas suburb most likely to match that expectation for family amenities — even if it's a very different kind of world-class.

The Honest Trade-offs — What Frisco Doesn't Do Well

I've walked through what makes Frisco exceptional. Now the part that doesn't make the city's brochure.

It's car-dependent. Frisco has no light rail. Every errand, every school pickup, every dinner out requires a car. The city has launched GoZone, an on-demand rideshare service covering portions of Frisco — it's a genuine improvement over nothing, but it's not a substitute for a car. Day-to-day life here is built around driving. For families coming from walkable urban environments, this is a real adjustment to plan for.

It's expensive — and not just the houses. The grocery stores are nicer but priced accordingly. Youth sports registration fees are higher than most of DFW. The restaurant scene skews upscale. Frisco has a "premium suburb" price floor that extends beyond the mortgage payment. Niche gives it a B- for cost of living, and that rating is earned.

DNT traffic is real. The Tollway widening project has been ongoing for years, and while progress continues, peak-hour southbound traffic between Frisco and Legacy West is a daily reality. If your commute destination is anywhere south of Plano, budget honest time — not Google Maps's optimistic estimate.

The ISD split can bite you. Covered in detail above, but worth repeating: if you're buying in north or northwest Frisco specifically for Frisco ISD and you haven't verified the zoning by property address, you may be disappointed. Prosper ISD and McKinney ISD are excellent — but they're not what you came for if you had Frisco ISD on your shortlist.

Outdoor activities are limited. Niche gives Frisco a B- for outdoor activities. There are no lakes within the city limits, minimal natural terrain, and the trail network is well-maintained but urban. For families who prioritize hiking, water access, or natural green space, Frisco isn't the answer. (Rockwall or Flower Mound are better fits.)

Frisco is likely the right fit if...
  • School district rating is your top priority and your budget supports $550K+
  • Your employer is along the DNT/121 corridor — Legacy West, Plano, Allen, or in Frisco itself
  • You want master-planned community amenities (pools, trails, organized events) built in
  • You're coming from California, New York, or Illinois and want the closest DFW equivalent to a premium suburb
  • Pro sports culture and world-class facilities within your neighborhood matter to your family
Frisco may not be the right fit if...
  • Your budget peaks around $450K–$500K — the entry tier here is thin and competitive
  • You need lake access, hiking trails, or genuine outdoor recreation nearby
  • Your commute destination is Downtown Dallas, Fort Worth, or Las Colinas daily
  • You're a first-time buyer in Texas who hasn't run the numbers on property taxes + tollway costs
  • You want a quieter, less manicured suburb with a slower pace and a smaller-town feel

Real Estate Market — Is Now a Good Time to Buy in Frisco?

Frisco's market sits in balanced territory right now — 4.5 months of supply and 37.7 average days on market. That's a meaningful shift from the seller's market frenzy of 2021–2022 when homes were going 5–10% over list price in days. What it means in practice: you can negotiate. Contingencies are available. You can do a thorough inspection without waiving everything. For families who want to buy thoughtfully rather than frantically, the current market is the most favorable Frisco has been in years.

One thing I tell every out-of-state buyer considering Frisco: at 2,151 closed sales in the last 12 months, this is an extremely liquid market. There is real selection. For families considering whether to rent first or buy immediately: Frisco has a strong luxury rental market. If you have school-age kids and want to be certain about ISD zoning before committing to a purchase, a 6–12 month rental in the right part of the city is a legitimate strategy. The cost is modest compared to making the wrong purchase decision.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Frisco Texas a good place to live for families?
Frisco consistently ranks among the top family cities in the U.S. — A+-rated Frisco ISD, violent crime under 1 per 1,000 residents, 227+ parks, world-class sports venues, and master-planned communities built around family life. For families relocating from California or New York, it delivers publicly excellent schools without private school costs. The trade-off is price and car-dependence.
What school district is Frisco Texas in?
Most of Frisco is served by Frisco ISD — A+ from Niche, TEA A (90/100). But Frisco is a split-ISD city: north and northwest Frisco may fall in Prosper ISD, northeast areas near the McKinney border in McKinney ISD, and the far southeast in Allen ISD. All four districts are strong, but always verify zoning by exact address before making an offer.
How much does a house cost in Frisco Texas?
The current median single-family home is approximately $681,000. Entry-level communities like Phillips Creek Ranch and Lone Star Ranch start around $480K–$550K. Luxury gated communities like Starwood and Newman Village run $900K–$2M+. The Fields, adjacent to PGA Frisco, offers new construction from $700K to $1.5M+.
What are property taxes in Frisco Texas?
The combined base rate is approximately $1.68 per $100 of assessed value (Collin County side, FY26) — Frisco ISD ~$1.019, City of Frisco ~$0.426, Collin County ~$0.149, Collin College ~$0.081. On a $681K home, roughly $11,400/year. Denton County-side addresses differ slightly; newer communities may add MUD or PID fees. Higher than California's Prop 13 rate, but largely offset by Texas's zero state income tax.
How far is Frisco from Dallas?
About 30 miles north of Downtown Dallas via the DNT — 35–45 minutes without traffic, 65–80 minutes in heavy morning rush. But most Frisco residents don't commute to Downtown Dallas. Their daily destinations are the Legacy West/Plano corridor (~10 miles, 15–20 min) or DFW Airport (~24 miles, 30–40 min) — both substantially shorter.
Is Frisco Texas safe?
Yes — Frisco has a violent crime rate of less than 1 per 1,000 residents, versus the U.S. average of about 4 per 1,000. WalletHub has consistently ranked Frisco among the safest large cities in America across multiple years. Property crime exists near commercial corridors around Stonebriar, but residential neighborhoods consistently report low rates. Safety is one of Frisco's strongest selling points.

Frisco is the suburb I recommend most often to families relocating from high-cost-of-living states who want a complete, premium family environment and can absorb the price point. It delivers on what it promises — consistently, repeatably, year after year. The families I've worked with who bought in Frisco and stayed are, almost uniformly, glad they did. The ones who regret it are usually ones who stretched their budget too far, didn't verify their ISD zone, or were expecting a commute to Downtown Dallas to be shorter than it is.

Go in with clear eyes on those three things, and Frisco is genuinely one of the best suburbs in the country for raising school-age kids.
