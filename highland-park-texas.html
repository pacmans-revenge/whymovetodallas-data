Getting Your Bearings

Where Is Highland Park, Texas?

Highland Park is a small, independent town of just 2.2 square miles completely surrounded by the city of Dallas — 4 miles north of downtown. Together with neighboring University Park to its north, it forms the Park Cities, an enclave within Dallas County that operates its own city government, its own school district, and its own police department entirely separate from the City of Dallas. US-75 (North Central Expressway) runs along its eastern edge, Preston Road bisects it north–south, and Mockingbird Lane marks its southern boundary. The Katy Trail — one of Dallas's most beloved urban greenways — follows the Missouri-Kansas-Texas railroad bed along the eastern border.

Location is Highland Park's most underrated asset. In under 15 minutes you can be at work in Uptown, on the elevator in a downtown office tower, or at a gate at Love Field. The Dallas North Tollway is a quick hop north and west, putting Addison and the Galleria Corridor inside 20 minutes. That proximity to the entire Dallas employment core is something you simply can't replicate in North Dallas or any of the northern suburbs — and it's a legitimate reason families in demanding careers choose to pay the premium here rather than add a 40-minute highway commute to every work day.

Transit reality: Highland Park has no DART rail access and limited bus service — a car is essential for daily life. However, the Katy Trail along the eastern edge gives walkers, runners, and cyclists a direct 3.5-mile paved connection south into Uptown and the Dallas Arts District. Many Highland Park residents bike to Uptown restaurants or use the trail for a car-free commute to Uptown offices. For a suburb with a Walk Score of 43, the Katy Trail connection adds real lifestyle value that the raw number doesn't capture.
Education

Highland Park ISD — Schools Deep Dive

If school quality is your top priority and budget is not the binding constraint, Highland Park ISD is in a category of its own among Texas public school districts. In 2025, HPISD became the first district of its size — 5A/6A enrollment — to earn the top TEA score among all comparable districts statewide, finishing with a 96 out of 100 and an "A" rating. Niche gives the district an A+ across the board: overall, academics, college prep, and families. This isn't marketing language — it's a consistent pattern across multiple independent rating systems, year after year. Highland Park HS ranked #1 among Texas open-enrollment public high schools. For families where the school district is the purchase driver, Highland Park ISD is the answer.

Where to Live

Best Neighborhoods in Highland Park

Highland Park is fully built out — there's no new construction here, no master-planned communities breaking ground, no future phases. What exists is what you're buying into: roughly 2,500 single-family homes on 2.2 square miles of tree-canopied streets between the Katy Trail and Preston Road. The architectural diversity is genuine — Tudors, colonials, Spanish Mediterraneans, Georgian estates, and a handful of modern builds sit side by side on streets lined with 60-year-old oaks. Price ranges are wide within the town, but the floor is high. Here are the neighborhoods families most often ask about.

What You're Trading

Cost of Living: How Highland Park Compares

Highland Park is not a cost-of-living value play — the Affordability Score of 44 is the honest signal. This is one of the most expensive residential communities in Texas. The tradeoff families accept is explicit: the highest-rated public school district in the state, 4 miles from downtown Dallas, on streets that have barely changed in 50 years. If the calculus works for your family, it works. If you need it to pencil on an affordability basis, look at Allen, Plano, or Prosper — you'll get A-tier schools at a fraction of the price.

Work

Where Highland Park Residents Work

Highland Park itself has almost no large employers — it's a residential enclave, not a commercial district. The commercial centers that residents commute to are overwhelmingly in Dallas proper, and the proximity to the downtown and Uptown employment cores is one of the strongest practical arguments for the premium. Families tied to the Dallas financial, legal, medical, or technology corridors can reduce commute time dramatically compared to living anywhere north of the Park Cities.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Highland Park

Highland Park is one of those places where the lifestyle matches the price — but it's a very specific lifestyle. It's not resort-style amenities and master-planned conveniences. It's quiet streets shaded by 70-year-old trees, kids walking to school or biking to a park, Saturday mornings on the Katy Trail, and the kind of neighborhood where people know each other's names. The community is tight-knit, the schools are genuinely excellent, and the proximity to the city means you never feel trapped in a suburb. What you give up is space, diversity, and the lower price points that most of DFW's family suburbs offer.

Youth Sports & Activities

HPISD athletics are competitive at the 5A level — the Scots (HP High) field strong programs across football, basketball, track, soccer, and lacrosse. Highland Park Pool offers summer swim team programs. Youth leagues through the Park Cities Soccer Club and YMCA serve younger kids before high school varsity eligibility.

Outdoor Recreation & Trails

The Katy Trail — 3.5 miles of paved greenway along the former rail corridor — runs along HP's eastern border and connects south into Uptown and the Dallas Arts District. Turtle Creek Boulevard's azalea-lined walking path is one of Dallas's most beautiful urban spaces. Lakeside Park's 14 acres along the creek is the neighborhood anchor for morning runs and weekend relaxation.

Dining & Shopping

Snider Plaza (HP's original retail district) has neighborhood restaurants, boutiques, and coffee shops that residents walk to. Knox-Henderson — immediately to the south along the Katy Trail — is one of Dallas's best dining corridors, walkable or a quick bike ride from the eastern side of HP. NorthPark Center, one of the top malls in Texas, is 10 minutes north.

Community Events & Culture

The Turtle Creek Azalea Trail blooms every March — families from across Dallas visit but residents get it all spring, practically in their front yards. HPISD arts performances at the Auditorium are a genuine community anchor. The Park Cities social calendar is active — charity galas, school fundraisers, and neighborhood gatherings give the town a cohesive, in-it-together feel that few DFW suburbs match.

City Access Without the City

This is the defining Highland Park experience: the cultural institutions, restaurants, and professional opportunities of one of the 10 largest cities in America are 10 minutes away — but you come home to quiet residential streets, a town with its own government, and a school district that operates independently from Dallas ISD. You get urban proximity with a genuinely suburban and community-oriented home life.

Stability & Legacy

Highland Park has looked essentially the same since the 1960s. No new construction, no rezoning debates, no apartment complexes going in next door. For families who've lived through California's rapid suburban densification, there's something deeply appealing about a community that simply doesn't change. Ownership here is generational — turnover is low (75 SFR sales in 12 months), which tells you about the commitment residents have to staying.

The honest tradeoff: Highland Park's Niche Diversity grade is a C — the community skews toward a homogeneous demographic profile in a way that's different from Frisco, McKinney, or Plano. Families coming from diverse California metros or NYC boroughs sometimes find the social landscape narrower than what they're used to. There is also no DART rail, traffic on US-75 and the neighboring Dallas arteries can be heavy during morning rush, and the complete absence of new construction means you're buying into a renovation or paying for someone else's. You're also paying a lot — the entry price is real, and families who stretch to afford it sometimes feel it financially in ways that affect quality of life. This is a community worth buying into deliberately, not just because it's prestigious.
Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & City Amenities

Highland Park is car-dependent by standard metrics — a Walk Score of 43 reflects the residential-only character of most of the town. But the Katy Trail and Turtle Creek corridors give it more on-foot access than that number suggests, particularly for residents in the eastern half of the town. The caveat: Highland Park is 2.2 square miles with no big-box retail, no grocery superstores, and no dense commercial strip. You drive for almost everything practical. What you get in return is access to every Dallas dining, arts, and recreational amenity within 15 minutes, and some of the best-maintained park land per capita in DFW.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

For a 2.2-square-mile town, Highland Park's park system is exceptional. The town consistently ranks #1 in DFW for parks per resident — a function of the Turtle Creek greenbelt and the town's deliberate investment in maintained public space. The azalea-lined stretch of Turtle Creek Boulevard alone draws visitors from across Dallas each spring.

Lakeside Park

14 acres along Turtle Creek between Beverly Drive and Armstrong Parkway. Exceptionally maintained landscaping, walking paths, and open lawn along the creek. A neighborhood anchor for morning runs and weekend family outings. One of the nicest urban parks in Dallas County.

Flippen Park

Located at 4421 N. Versailles Avenue, Flippen Park is a romantic neighborhood green with a gazebo, fountain, and reflecting pool, surrounded by residential streets. A favorite for evening walks and weekend morning coffee routes.

Turtle Creek Park & Boulevard

The Turtle Creek corridor runs through the heart of Highland Park — part water, part greenway, part civic showpiece. The famous azalea planting along the boulevard blooms spectacularly each March. The creek-side paths are used by runners and dog walkers year-round.

The Katy Trail (Eastern Border)

Highland Park's eastern edge runs along the Katy Trail — a 3.5-mile paved greenway on the former MKT railroad right-of-way. One of Dallas's most beloved urban recreational amenities, with a car-free route south all the way to American Airlines Center. Connects HP directly to Knox-Henderson, Uptown, and the Arts District.

Davis Park & Prather Park

Neighborhood pocket parks scattered throughout the residential grid — maintained green space within walking distance of most HP streets. Not large parks, but well-kept and regular anchors for young kids and dog owners.

Bachman Lake & White Rock Lake (nearby)

Just outside HP's boundaries, Bachman Lake (minutes west) and White Rock Lake (15 min east) expand recreation options significantly — hiking, cycling, kayaking, and shoreline access that the Town itself doesn't have. Both are commonly used by HP families on weekends.

Safety

Crime & Safety in Highland Park

Highland Park's violent crime rate is among the lowest in the Dallas area — a function of both the town's independent police department and the socioeconomic profile of the community. NeighborhoodScout reports a violent crime rate of approximately 0.57 per 1,000 residents — meaning the chance of being a victim of violent crime in Highland Park in a year is roughly 1 in 1,752. The property crime picture is more nuanced: rates are influenced by Highland Park's adjacency to busy Dallas corridors, and property crimes (vehicle break-ins, opportunistic thefts) do occur, particularly near commercial areas. Niche gives the town a "B" for crime — one of the stronger grades in Dallas County.

The Market

Highland Park Real Estate Market

Highland Park is one of the thinnest real estate markets in DFW — only 75 single-family homes changed hands in the past 12 months across the entire town. That's a function of both the price level and the ownership culture: people who buy here tend to stay for decades. The market is balanced (3.7 months supply), which means neither buyers nor sellers hold extreme leverage. When a well-priced home in the right neighborhood hits the market, it moves. Overpriced listings sit — even here. Median DOM is about 50 days, which reflects the selectivity of the buyer pool at $3.7M+. Condos also trade, with 10 units sold in the past 12 months at a median of ~$1.2M — primarily in the high-rise buildings along the southern edge near Mockingbird.

The Honest Summary

Highland Park Pros & Cons for Families

✓ Why Families Choose Highland Park

  • Best public school district in Texas (5A/6A). HPISD TEA score 96/100, Niche A+, nationally ranked. This isn't marketing — it's a consistent multi-source result. If the school district is the primary driver of your purchase, HPISD is the answer.
  • Four miles from downtown Dallas. No suburb in DFW offers this combination of school quality and proximity to the downtown employment core. The commute advantage for families working in financial services, law, medicine, or consulting downtown is real and daily.
  • The Katy Trail and Turtle Creek access. One of the best urban recreational amenities in Dallas runs along Highland Park's eastern border — 3.5 miles of paved trail connecting to Uptown, Knox-Henderson, and the Arts District. Families use it daily, not just on weekends.
  • Town independence — own government, own police. Highland Park operates entirely separately from Dallas. The town's independent DPS provides fast response times, and residents feel the direct accountability of a small-town government that serves 8,774 people.
  • Stable, fully built-out community. No new construction risk, no rezoning surprises, no apartment complexes going in. Generational ownership, consistent streetscapes, and a community character that has been consistent for 50+ years.
  • #1 parks per capita in DFW. Lakeside Park, Turtle Creek, Flippen Park, and Dallas Country Club's private grounds give the town exceptional green space for its footprint. Morning runs and after-school play don't require driving.

— What Families Trade

  • $3.7M median — one of the most expensive markets in Texas. The Affordability Score is 44 for a reason. Families who stretch to afford HP sometimes find the financial pressure affects quality of life in ways that weren't expected. The honest conversation is whether the budget works comfortably, not just barely.
  • 75 homes sold per year across the entire town. Inventory is thin. The right house doesn't come up every month. Buyers need patience, financial readiness, and ideally access to off-market opportunities.
  • Car-dependent for almost everything practical (Walk Score: 43). Despite proximity to Dallas, Highland Park has no grocery stores, no pharmacy, no Target — you drive. The Katy Trail mitigates this for residents in the eastern half, but daily errands require a car.
  • No racial or socioeconomic diversity (Niche Diversity: C). Families relocating from diverse California metros or major northeastern cities often notice this. It's a real tradeoff for families who value their kids growing up in a diverse environment.
  • No new construction — renovation risk. Every home has history. Buyers should expect to budget for updates, deferred maintenance, or full renovations, even on recently updated properties. The "as-is" risk is higher than in new-build suburban markets.
  • Property tax bills are substantial in absolute dollars. At 1.09% effective on $3.7M, annual taxes run ~$40,000. The rate is lower than many Texas suburbs in percentage terms, but the dollar amount is among the highest in the state.
Common Questions

Highland Park, TX — Frequently Asked Questions

Is Highland Park, Texas its own city separate from Dallas?
Yes — Highland Park is an independent incorporated town in Dallas County with its own city government, police department (Highland Park DPS), and school district (HPISD). It is completely surrounded by the city of Dallas but operates entirely separately. It shares no services with Dallas ISD, Dallas PD, or the City of Dallas.
What school district is Highland Park, Texas?
Highland Park ISD (HPISD) serves students in both the Town of Highland Park and the Town of University Park. HPISD earned a TEA score of 96/100 and Niche A+ overall grade, ranking #1 among all 5A/6A districts in Texas in 2025. You must physically reside within the incorporated town of Highland Park or University Park to enroll — verify your address at hpisd.org before making an offer.
How much does it cost to live in Highland Park, Texas?
The median single-family home price in Highland Park is approximately $3.7M as of the most recent NTREIS data — making it one of the most expensive communities in Texas. Entry-level homes in the Old Highland Park area start around $2.5M; estate homes along Armstrong Parkway and Volk Estates range from $5M to $20M+. There is no new construction; every home has existing history.
What is the property tax rate in Highland Park, Texas?
The combined tax rate is approximately $1.34 per $100 of assessed value — comprised of HPISD ($0.8347), the Town of Highland Park ($0.1993), Dallas County (~$0.1817), and DCCCD (~$0.1220). The effective rate after exemptions is approximately 1.09%. On a $3.7M home, annual property taxes run roughly $40,000. Homestead exemptions and value protests can reduce this; consult a tax professional.
Is Highland Park Texas safe?
Yes — Highland Park has very low violent crime, with NeighborhoodScout reporting approximately 0.57 violent crimes per 1,000 residents. The town has its own independent police department with fast response times across a 2.2-square-mile footprint. Property crime (vehicle break-ins, opportunistic theft) occurs at a moderate rate, primarily near commercial areas and US-75. Niche gives the town a "B" for crime and safety overall.
How far is Highland Park from downtown Dallas?
Highland Park is approximately 4 miles from downtown Dallas via US-75 South — a 12–15 minute drive in normal traffic. Uptown and the Design District are closer still, under 10 minutes. This proximity to the Dallas employment core is one of Highland Park's primary selling points for families in finance, law, or medicine with downtown offices.
What are the best neighborhoods in Highland Park, Texas?
The town's most notable neighborhoods include Old Highland Park (the original area, with the widest price range from $2.5M up), Volk Estates (the most exclusive area, backing to Turtle Creek), the Armstrong Parkway corridor (largest lots, grandest estates), and central Highland Park around Westchester Drive (most active market, widest variety). All sit within the same 2.2-square-mile town and feed the same schools.
Does Highland Park have new construction homes?
No. Highland Park is fully built out — there is no undeveloped land and no new construction. Every home in the town has existing history, ranging from original 1920s–1940s builds to fully renovated custom properties. Buyers should budget for updates or renovations even on recently refreshed homes, and should have an inspection strategy appropriate for older construction.
Is there a Katy Trail in Highland Park?
The Katy Trail runs along Highland Park's eastern border — the former MKT railroad right-of-way. It's a 3.5-mile paved recreational trail connecting south through Knox-Henderson to Uptown and American Airlines Center. Many Highland Park residents use it daily for running, cycling, and even bike commuting to Uptown offices. It's one of the most valuable lifestyle amenities in the community.
How does Highland Park compare to Frisco or Allen for families?
The tradeoff is location vs. affordability. Frisco and Allen offer A-tier school districts (Frisco ISD A+, Allen ISD A+), brand-new construction, and median home prices in the $600K–$700K range — roughly one-fifth the Highland Park price. HP offers the #1-rated district in Texas and a 12-minute drive to downtown Dallas. Families who work downtown and can afford the premium often choose HP; families prioritizing new homes, more space, or newer suburban amenities often choose Frisco or Allen.
