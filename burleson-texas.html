Getting Your Bearings

Where Is Burleson, Texas?

Burleson sits in Johnson County about 16 miles south of downtown Fort Worth, straddling the I-35W corridor that connects it to one of the most concentrated employment zones in North Texas. That single highway is Burleson's geographic advantage — most residents are at their desk in Fort Worth in under 25 minutes on a normal morning, which is rare for a suburb with homes in the $290K–$500K range. The city proper covers roughly 30 square miles, with older neighborhoods near the historic town center and newer master-planned subdivisions growing south and east.

For families relocating from California or the Northeast, Burleson often registers as an afterthought next to names like Frisco or Southlake — but that's the point. It offers legitimate community character, Burleson ISD schools, 348 acres of parks, and proximity to Fort Worth's hospitals, defense contractors, and aviation industry without the premium price tags of suburbs closer to Dallas. If your job is on the I-35W corridor and your budget is under $450K, Burleson deserves a serious look.

Transit reality: Burleson has no DART rail or commuter rail service. The city operates a small local bus system (The T — Fort Worth), but it's not a practical commute option. A personal vehicle is required for daily life. The silver lining is that I-35W southbound out of Fort Worth clears quickly compared to DFW's eastern corridors, and most residents find the 20-minute Fort Worth run genuinely stress-free outside of peak hours.
Education

Burleson ISD — Schools Deep Dive

Burleson ISD has quietly built a reputation as a solid, community-oriented district — not the flashiest name in DFW, but genuinely above-average and improving. The district earned a TEA B rating in 2024, with standout scores in Student Achievement and College Preparation. For families relocating from states with inconsistent public school quality, the consistency across Burleson ISD campuses — especially at the elementary level — is one of its most underrated selling points. The district's crown jewel is Burleson Collegiate High School, ranked #1 public high school in Johnson County by Niche and earning a TEA score of 99 — one of the highest in the region.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Burleson

Youth Sports Are a Big Deal Here

Chisenhall Fields is the centerpiece — 10 baseball diamonds and 5 softball fields used year-round by Burleson Youth Sports Association and travel teams across South DFW. Soccer, flag football, basketball leagues, and gymnastics round out the calendar. If your kids are in sports, Burleson keeps them busy from January through December.

Parks, Trails, and Bailey Lake

Burleson's 348 acres of parkland and 22 miles of trails are well-distributed across the city. The Oak Valley Trail connects multiple neighborhoods and parks for cyclists and walkers. Bailey Lake Park anchors the east side with splash pads, playgrounds, and fishing. Families here genuinely use the outdoor infrastructure — it's not just on paper.

Dining and Shopping That Works

Burleson's commercial corridor along I-35W and Wilshire Boulevard has every major chain and enough independent dining to keep weeknight meals interesting. The Texas Roadhouse, Chick-fil-A, and HEB anchors cover daily needs. For a wider selection, Fort Worth is 20 minutes north — close enough that the limited local restaurant scene rarely feels like a real trade-off.

Small-Town Events, Real Community

Old Town Burleson hosts seasonal festivals, farmers markets, and community events throughout the year. The city organizes Fourth of July celebrations at Chisenhall, holiday light displays, and community rec center programming that keeps families engaged. It has the bones of a small Texas town with the resources of a 50,000-person city — an increasingly rare combination in DFW.

Fort Worth Access Without Fort Worth Prices

This is Burleson's defining advantage — the Fort Worth cultural district, Sundance Square, the Fort Worth Zoo, the Botanic Garden, Kimbell Art Museum, and world-class live music venues are all under 25 minutes away. Burleson families get the full Fort Worth experience without paying Fort Worth property taxes or enduring urban density. It's the best of both at a price point most Texas metros can't match.

Neighbors Who Stick Around

Residents consistently describe Burleson as a city where people stay once they arrive. Owner-occupancy is 71% — high even by DFW standards — and neighborhood Facebook groups are active and genuinely helpful for newcomers. Military families in particular have noted how welcoming the community is to relocators, which reflects both the area's proximity to NAS JRB and the small-town culture that comes with roots.

Honest tradeoff: Burleson has no meaningful nightlife, no walkable entertainment district, and no craft brewery scene worth noting. The downtown area has charm, but it closes early. If you're relocating from Austin or a large city expecting that energy in your suburb, you won't find it here. Fort Worth fills that gap — but it requires getting in the car. For families at the stage of life where Saturday mornings mean baseball games and Sunday evenings mean backyard grilling, this isn't actually a trade-off at all.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Burleson maintains 348 acres of parkland and 22 miles of trails — a genuine strength for a city of 51,000. Green space is distributed across the city's neighborhoods, and the Oak Valley Trail connects multiple parks in a continuous loop that cyclists and walkers use year-round.

Chisenhall Fields Sports Complex

10 baseball and 5 softball diamonds with stadium lighting, concession stands, and tournament-grade facilities. Home to Burleson Youth Sports Association and travel tournaments that draw teams from across DFW. One of the best youth baseball complexes in Tarrant/Johnson County.

Bailey Lake Park

Burleson's most beloved family park — a lakefront green space with fishing piers, splash pads, playgrounds, covered pavilions, and a 1-mile loop trail. Weekend mornings draw hundreds of families, and the splash pad runs from late spring through Labor Day weekend.

Oak Valley Trail

A 5-mile multi-use trail that connects parks and neighborhoods across central Burleson. The trail is paved, well-lit in key sections, and popular with cyclists, joggers, and dog walkers. Connects to Elk Ridge Park, providing a natural east-west spine through the city.

Elk Ridge Park

A neighborhood anchor park in the Elk Ridge area featuring soccer fields, a playground, shaded pavilions, and open green space. Regularly used for community soccer leagues and impromptu youth games. One of the more active community parks in the city on weekends.

Hidden Creek Sports Complex

Adjacent to Hidden Creek neighborhood and offering multi-use sports fields, a walking trail loop, and open lawn space. Regularly scheduled for youth soccer and community flag football leagues. A good indicator of Burleson's investment in youth athletic infrastructure.

Burleson Golf Course at Summercrest

An 18-hole public golf course in the heart of Burleson — one of the more affordable public courses in the Fort Worth metro, making it accessible for families who want to introduce kids to the game without the country club price tag.

Crime & Safety

How Safe Is Burleson?

Burleson's overall crime rate sits below both the Texas state average and the national average — a meaningful data point for families relocating from higher-crime metro areas. Violent crime in particular is well below the U.S. norm at approximately 1.7 incidents per 1,000 residents, compared to a national average of roughly 4.0. The Niche crime grade of C+ reflects that property crime is more prevalent than violent crime — a typical suburban Texas pattern — but Burleson PD actively publishes crime reports and the trend over the past several years has been declining. Reddit and community forums consistently describe Burleson as a "feels safe" suburb where the biggest concerns tend to be car break-ins in unlocked vehicles, not violent incidents.

New Construction

New Homes in Burleson

Burleson is a fully established suburb — the kind of place where the neighborhoods are set, the trees are mature, and the community feel is already there when you move in. There's little vacant land left for large-scale new residential development, so most families buying here are focused on the resale market. That's not a disadvantage: Burleson has strong inventory across a range of price points, lot sizes, and neighborhood styles, and resale homes often come with finished landscaping, custom touches, and HOA communities that are already thriving. If you're weighing Burleson against a suburb with active new construction, the honest trade-off is this — you're giving up the ability to choose your finishes and get a builder warranty, and gaining a suburb where the character and school patterns are well-established and predictable. For many relocating families, that's exactly what they're looking for. Pros & Cons of Living in Burleson

I won't sell you on Burleson. I'll give you the real picture so you can decide if it's right for your family.

✓ What Burleson Does Well

  • Fort Worth access without Fort Worth prices — 16 miles from downtown Fort Worth via I-35W. You live 20 minutes from one of DFW's most underrated cities — and pay Johnson County property taxes to get there.
  • Genuine affordability in DFW — A $351K median puts Burleson among the most affordable cities within striking distance of a major employment hub in the entire Metroplex. You get real square footage on a real lot at a price families from California or New York genuinely find hard to believe.
  • Youth sports infrastructure — Chisenhall Fields is exceptional. If your family lives inside baseball or softball season, Burleson has facilities that rival cities three times its size.
  • Community feel that persists — 71% owner-occupancy, active neighborhood groups, and a small-town culture that's rare to find in a growing DFW suburb. Residents who move here tend to stay.
  • Burleson Collegiate High School — The #1 public high school in Johnson County is right here, earning a TEA score of 99. Families with high-achieving students have a real option without moving to a premium suburb.
  • Balanced market right now — 3.9 months supply means you're buying in a market with negotiation room. Not a buyer's market, but not a frantic seller's market either.
  • No state income tax — Meaningful savings, especially for high earners relocating from California, New York, or Illinois.

— What to Weigh Carefully

  • Property taxes are real — The $2.41/$100 combined rate means roughly $8,400/yr on a $350K home before the ISD homestead exemption. The income tax savings offset this substantially for most California or New York families, but don't expect Texas to be cheap on property taxes.
  • I-35W traffic is growing — The Burleson-to-Fort Worth morning commute is manageable today, but I-35W southbound out of Fort Worth has gotten noticeably worse as South Tarrant County has grown. Budget 30–40 minutes on heavy days, not 15.
  • Schools are solid, not elite — Burleson ISD is above average and improving, but it's not Frisco ISD or Carroll ISD. Families who rank schools as their absolute top priority and have a flexible budget will find stronger districts in Southlake or Flower Mound.
  • Limited nightlife and entertainment — Old Town Burleson is charming but closes early. For live music, craft cocktail bars, or a restaurant scene with depth, you're driving 20 minutes to Fort Worth. That's fine for most families — just be honest with yourself about whether it matters.
  • Car-dependent — No meaningful public transit. A personal vehicle is essential. Not ideal if you're used to public transportation in your current city.

Burleson's combined base tax rate is approximately $2.4063 per $100 of assessed value (2025), comprising Burleson ISD ($1.2552), City of Burleson ($0.7218), Johnson County ($0.3293), Johnson County Lateral Road ($0.0500), and Hill College ($0.0500). On Burleson's median-priced home of $350,919, that's roughly $8,422 per year before the mandatory ISD homestead exemption — which reduces the school district's taxable value by $100K, dropping annual taxes to approximately $7,150 for a primary residence.

Texas has no state income tax, so families relocating from California or New York typically find the net tax burden is significantly lower in Burleson even with higher property tax rates. Always verify the exact combined rate for a specific address before closing — some new-construction communities have additional MUD or PID assessments. Source: Texas Comptroller.

The headline numbers are stark: Burleson's median home price is $351K versus $1.3M in the Bay Area or $750K–$1.1M in New York metro. Texas has zero state income tax — a family earning $250,000 in California saves $23,000+ annually just by crossing the state line. Even accounting for Burleson's higher property tax rate, most California and New York families come out $15,000–$30,000 ahead per year on a combined tax basis.

The lifestyle trade-offs are real: Burleson doesn't have the walkability, the urban density, or the cultural scene of a coastal city. But it offers a 20-minute commute to Fort Worth, a genuine community feel, good public schools, and no ice or sustained cold. For families with school-age kids who've been renting in San Jose or New York and finally want a backyard, the math is hard to argue with.

The top family neighborhoods in Burleson are: Hidden Vistas ($480K–$560K) for upscale established living with greenbelt lots; Shannon Creek ($370K–$420K) for new-construction D.R. Horton homes near Chisenhall Fields; Prairie Timber Estates ($390K–$460K) for half-acre semi-rural lots; Elk Ridge ($290K–$340K) for established value at an accessible entry point; Mountain Valley Lake ($410K–$480K) for lakefront access; and Bent Oaks ($320K–$375K) for a classic quiet suburban feel. The most critical variable is which high school your address feeds — verify feeder patterns at burlesonisd.net before selecting a neighborhood.

It depends on your timeline and confidence in the suburb. For families relocating from out of state who've never spent time in the South Fort Worth corridor, a 6–12 month rental period is worth considering — it gives you time to identify which neighborhoods feel right, understand the school feeder patterns from the inside, and buy deliberately rather than under relocation pressure. Burleson is a balanced market right now with 3.9 months supply, so a brief rental period won't cost you a dramatically missed opportunity. If you've visited, done your research, and know which school zone you want, buying directly is entirely reasonable.

Burleson's violent crime rate is approximately 1.7 per 1,000 residents — well below the U.S. average of 4.0 and the Texas average. Property crime runs at about 10 per 1,000, also below state norms. Niche rates crime and safety C+, reflecting that property crime (primarily car break-ins and package theft) is more common than in Burleson's top-ranked neighboring suburbs like Mansfield. Overall crime trended down 14% in 2024 compared to 2023. The community forums consistently describe Burleson as a "feels safe" suburb where violent incidents are rare and the biggest concern is leaving a car unlocked overnight.

No meaningful public transit. The T (Fort Worth's transit authority) operates some limited bus service in the northern Burleson area, but it's not a practical commute option for most residents. There is no DART rail service, no commuter rail, and no park-and-ride infrastructure worth noting. A personal vehicle is not optional — it is essential for daily life in Burleson. If you're relocating from a city where you relied on public transit, factor two-vehicle costs into your budget. For most Texas suburban families, this is simply the expected reality.

Within Burleson: Texas Health Huguley Hospital, Burleson ISD, Amazon Fulfillment Center, and Walmart Distribution Center are the largest employers. The real opportunity is 15–30 minutes north in the Fort Worth corridor: Lockheed Martin (14,000+ employees in aerospace/defense), Bell Textron/Bell Helicopter, American Airlines (HQ), BNSF Railway, Cook Children's Medical Center, JPS Health Network, and NAS Fort Worth JRB (military). For Las Colinas/Irving roles (ExxonMobil, Celanese, Fluor), Burleson is 35–45 minutes via I-20 East — manageable, though most Burleson residents work the I-35W corridor rather than the DNT/I-635 corridor.

I-35W is the primary artery — northbound into Fort Worth in the morning, southbound back in the evening. The commute has gotten noticeably heavier as the South Tarrant/Johnson County area has grown, and some residents report 30–40 minutes northbound during peak hours compared to the 20-minute off-peak drive. The I-35W widening project in the Burleson area has helped, but construction phases periodically cause delays. Within Burleson itself, Wilshire Boulevard and Alsbury Boulevard carry most local traffic and can back up near the I-35W interchange during school drop-off hours. For most residents, the I-35W reality is the key variable to experience firsthand before buying.
