Everything relocating families need to know about Garland ISD — TEA ratings, campus highlights, magnet and dual language programs, enrollment steps, and boundary verification before you buy in Garland, Rowlett, or Sachse.

Garland ISD at a Glance

72 Campuses

A‑ Niche Grade (Third-Party)
92.9% Graduation Rate
$1.17 Tax Rate per $100 (2025)

Garland ISD is the second-largest school district in Dallas County and one of the 100 largest in the United States, serving approximately 55,000 students across the cities of Garland, Rowlett, and portions of Sachse. For relocating families, the key thing to understand about this district is its trajectory: Garland ISD earned a TEA score of 84 in 2025 — up from 81 the prior year — and led Dallas County in TEA gains, with the district posting a 96% College, Career, and Military Readiness (CCMR) rate that ranks among the highest in the state for a district of its size. That combination of scale, improving academic ratings, and a robust magnet program network makes it a legitimate shortlist option for many east-DFW families.

The honest tradeoff is that with 72 campuses serving a large and economically diverse community, individual campus quality varies considerably. The district's overall TEA score of 84 reflects real district-level improvement, but your child's experience will be shaped by their assigned campus — not the district average. More than one-third of campuses earned an A rating in 2025, and about two-thirds earned an A or B, so there is genuine strength across the system. But families should research their specific campus, not just the district headline, before committing to a particular neighborhood. The Sachse boundary situation also requires attention: parts of Sachse fall within Wylie ISD, not Garland ISD, so a Sachse address is not a guarantee of Garland ISD enrollment.

What sets Garland ISD apart for relocation families is the program depth — selective magnet schools at multiple levels, an Early College High School magnet that lets students earn up to 60+ college credits or an associate's degree alongside their diploma, a Dual Language pathway, and one of DFW's stronger CTE networks. For families who want program variety inside an affordable housing market, Garland ISD offers options that many smaller A-rated districts simply can't match at scale.

Best Fit For Families Who…

  • Want affordable housing in east DFW with access to a large district's program variety
  • Are interested in magnet programs, dual language pathways, or an early college track
  • Are buying in established Rowlett or Garland neighborhoods and value campus proximity
  • Prioritize CCMR and post-secondary readiness metrics alongside traditional test scores
  • Want a improving district on an upward trajectory, not one that peaked years ago

May Not Be the Best Fit If…

  • You need a consistently A-rated district and campus quality is non-negotiable (Wylie ISD serves parts of the same Sachse geography)
  • You are moving from a smaller, tight-knit district and want a similar community feel — 55,000 students means significant scale
  • You want the simplest possible boundary picture — parts of Sachse span two ISDs and require address-level verification
  • You are targeting a specific high school campus and need to confirm your address feeds that campus before making an offer
Boundary Verification

Not every home with a Garland, Rowlett, or Sachse address falls within Garland ISD boundaries. Parts of Sachse are served by Wylie ISD instead. Always verify your specific address using the district's campus locator at garlandisd.net or the TEA school locator before making an offer.

This is especially relevant for buyers targeting new construction in the Sachse area — the ISD boundary does not follow city limits.

TEA Accountability Rating

In 2025, Garland ISD earned an overall TEA score of 84 out of 100 (Grade: B) — a three-point gain from its 82 score in 2024, and a four-point gain from 80 the year before. The district led Dallas County in TEA gains and was described by TEA as "on track to become an A-rated district." More than one-third of Garland ISD campuses earned an A in 2025, and approximately two-thirds received an A or B rating.

TEA rates districts on three domains:

  • Student Achievement — how much students know and can demonstrate at year's end (STAAR proficiency rates, graduation rate, CCMR). Garland ISD earned a B in Student Achievement in 2025 with an improved score.
  • School Progress — academic growth over time, and how growth compares to similar schools statewide. The district showed consistent improvement in this domain.
  • Closing the Gaps — how well different student subgroups are progressing. Garland ISD gained five points in this domain in 2025, reflecting meaningful improvement in equity across student populations.

What this means for a relocating family: A district TEA score of 84 with a clear upward trajectory tells you the system is improving, not stagnating. The 96% CCMR rate — one of the highest in the state at this district size — is a particularly strong signal that students are leaving Garland ISD prepared for college and career, not just passing state tests. That said, the district average still masks real campus-to-campus variation. A B-rated district can contain a handful of A-rated campuses that rival any district in the region and a handful of lower-performing campuses. Research your assigned campus, not just the district scorecard.

TEA vs. Niche: Why They Differ

Garland ISD holds a Niche grade of A- (third-party) alongside its TEA grade of B. Niche weighs factors like student-to-teacher ratios, college readiness data, and community reviews differently than TEA's state accountability formula. Neither is "wrong" — they measure different things. Use both as directional signals, and verify at the campus level before deciding.

Academic Performance

Garland ISD's 2024–25 academic results reflect a district in consistent upward motion. The headline metric is a 96% College, Career, and Military Readiness (CCMR) rate — a figure that ranks Garland ISD among the highest in Dallas County, the region, and the state for a district of its scale. CCMR measures whether graduates leave high school with a concrete next step: passing an AP or IB exam, earning dual credit hours, obtaining an industry certification, or meeting college-readiness benchmarks on standardized assessments.

Graduation rate (2024): 92.9% — above the national average of 86.5%. For a large, diverse urban district, sustaining a graduation rate above 92% reflects meaningful institutional effort on student persistence, not just selective admissions.

AP, Dual Credit, and Early College access: Garland ISD students can earn at least 12 hours of college credit or an industry certification alongside their high school diplomas. In 2024–25, the district reported saving families $5.7 million in tuition through dual credit and early college pathways. At the high school level, each campus offers AP courses, with North Garland High School hosting a dedicated MST (Math, Science, Technology) magnet and Garland High School hosting an Early College HS Magnet.

What this means practically: A 96% CCMR rate and 92.9% graduation rate are genuinely strong numbers — not just for a B-rated district but for any district. The practical implication for families is that Garland ISD does a notably good job of ensuring students have a post-secondary pathway when they leave. If your priority is that your child graduates with real college credit or an industry certification in hand, the district delivers on that consistently.

Notable Campuses

Garland ISD operates six comprehensive high schools serving students across Garland, Rowlett, and Sachse. Each campus has its own culture, extracurricular strengths, and — for some — a specialized magnet program. Your child's assigned high school will depend on your home address, not your preference, so boundary verification matters here.

Garland High School

High School ~2,450 students Early College Magnet

Home to the Early College High School Magnet, where students can earn an associate's degree (or 60+ college credit hours) alongside their diploma in partnership with Richland College. Also hosts the Academy of Fine Arts magnet for theatre studies. One of the most program-rich campuses in the district.

North Garland High School

High School MST Magnet

Hosts the Math, Science, and Technology (MST) magnet program — a selective pathway for students who want a rigorous STEM-focused curriculum. The MST program requires application and meeting program qualifications. North Garland earned a TEA A rating in 2025, with scores of 92 in Student Achievement, 94 in School Progress, and 94 in Closing the Gaps.

Naaman Forest High School

High School ~1,950 students

Located in the eastern part of the district serving parts of Garland and Sachse. Naaman Forest is one of the district's more recently prominent campuses for academic performance and extracurricular activity. Families buying in eastern Garland or western Sachse should confirm whether their address feeds Naaman Forest or a different campus.

Rowlett High School

High School Rowlett / Lake Ray Hubbard area

Serves the Rowlett community. Rowlett is one of the district's more geographically distinct campuses, with a strong booster culture and community identity tied to the lakeside character of the city. Buyers in Rowlett should confirm their specific address feeds Rowlett High School, as the district-Rowlett boundary can vary by neighborhood.

Lakeview Centennial High School

High School

Serves the southern Garland and Rowlett corridor. Lakeview Centennial is known for a strong fine arts and athletic tradition. Part of the district's commitment to distinguishing each campus through extracurricular strengths rather than a single districtwide identity.

South Garland High School

High School

Serves the southern Garland area. South Garland's proximity to the Dallas city limit and the Richardson ISD boundary means a small number of addresses along the southern edge of Garland could potentially feed different attendance zones. Verify before buying.

Beyond high schools, Garland ISD received a 2025 bond authorization to break ground on new campuses and Multi-Activity Centers (MACs), reflecting the district's investment in facilities across its growing student population. For standout elementary campuses, families should check GreatSchools ratings filtered by their specific neighborhood at the time of their home search — campus ratings shift with enrollment changes and are best verified close to your move date.

Specialty & Magnet Programs

Garland ISD operates one of the more robust magnet and specialty program networks among large DFW districts. All magnet programs require application and qualifying criteria — placement is not automatic based on address. Transportation arrangements vary by program; confirm directly with the magnet office at gisdmagnetprograms@garlandisd.net.

Early College HS Magnet

Campus: Garland High School. Access: Application required, selective. Students can earn an associate's degree or 60+ college credit hours (saving families significant tuition costs) in partnership with Richland College. Home-search implication: This program is campus-limited to Garland High School — families specifically targeting Early College should confirm their address feeds GISD (not Richardson ISD or Dallas ISD) and that they are in Garland HS's attendance zone, or are prepared to apply as a transfer.

Math, Science & Technology (MST) Magnet

Campus: North Garland High School. Access: Application required, meets qualification criteria (grades, assessments). What it offers: A rigorous STEM-focused curriculum pathway across four years of high school. North Garland earned a TEA A in 2025 — the MST program is a significant contributor to that campus's academic profile.

Academy of Fine Arts

Campus: Garland High School. Access: Application, audition required for theatre studies diploma pathway. What it offers: A structured fine arts diploma track with a focus on performance and theatre. Families interested in the arts pathway at the high school level should apply during the annual magnet application window.

Dual Language Magnet

Campus: Classical Center at Vial Elementary (and expanding to other campuses). Access: Application through the Magnet office; students receive 50% instruction in English and 50% in Spanish. Important: Magnet choices are binding for the school year. If a student withdraws from GISD and returns, they lose their magnet placement and must re-apply. Home-search implication: Families targeting the Dual Language pathway ideally want to be living within the district before the application window opens (typically in early spring for the following school year).

Gifted & Talented (GT)

Districtwide: Yes, available across multiple campuses. Access: Assessment and identification process; contact your assigned campus for testing windows. GT services in Garland ISD include differentiated instruction within the base school, dedicated GT classes, and advanced coursework pathways into AP and dual credit at the high school level. Families transferring from another Texas district with a GT designation should contact the district's Special Services to expedite recognition of prior identification.

Career & Technical Education (CTE)

Districtwide: Available at all comprehensive high schools. What it offers: Industry certifications in healthcare, technology, business, and skilled trades — many of which count toward CCMR. The district's CTE network is a key driver of its 96% CCMR rate. Students can graduate with a certified skill credential alongside their diploma, often without additional cost. Home-search implication: CTE programs are not magnet — they are available based on course selection at your assigned campus.

Enrollment & Transfer Guide

Garland ISD uses an online enrollment system for new students. Here is what families relocating to the district need to know for the 2025–26 school year (verify current timelines at garlandisd.net as windows shift year to year):

Steps for New Students

  1. Confirm your boundary assignment: Before enrolling, verify that your specific address falls within Garland ISD — not a neighboring district. Use the school directory at garlandisd.net/choose-your-school/school-directory.
  2. Gather required documents: Proof of residency (lease agreement, utility bill, or closing documents), immunization records, withdrawal documents from previous school, and birth certificate or government ID. Requirements for international families may include additional documentation.
  3. Register online: Garland ISD uses an online registration portal accessible from garlandisd.net. New families enroll at their assigned campus or the district enrollment center.
  4. Enrollment window (2025–26): Garland ISD typically opens enrollment for the following school year in late spring. Confirm the exact window at garlandisd.net closer to your move date, as dates shift annually.

Transfer Policies (2025–26)

Intra-district transfers (changing campuses within Garland ISD) are available through the district's Open Enrollment process. Applications are reviewed on a space-available basis; approval is not guaranteed. The application typically opens in late winter/spring for the following school year.

Inter-district transfers (from another Texas ISD into Garland ISD) are handled through the Open Enrollment Application Process at garlandisd.net/choose-your-school/open-enrollment. Approval depends on campus capacity and is not guaranteed.

IEP / 504 / Special Education Transfers

Texas law requires Garland ISD to convene an ARD (Admissions, Review, and Dismissal) meeting within 30 days of a student with an active IEP enrolling in the district. Bring all prior IEP documentation, evaluation records, and any service agreements from your previous district. Contact Garland ISD's Special Services office directly to discuss your child's needs before or immediately after enrollment. Parent experience with special education services varies by campus and by student need — direct communication with the Special Services team is the best way to assess fit for your specific situation.

Boundary & Address Verification

Garland ISD's attendance boundary is one of the most important things to verify before buying a home in this area — and it is more complex than most families expect. Here is what you need to know.

Cities and Areas Served

Garland ISD serves most of the city of Garland, most of Rowlett, and the western portions of Sachse. It does not serve all of these cities — specific neighborhoods within each city may fall in a different district.

The Sachse Split: Garland ISD vs. Wylie ISD

This is the most critical boundary edge case in this market. Sachse is served by two ISDs: Garland ISD (generally the older, western sections of Sachse) and Wylie ISD (generally the newer, eastern sections, including much of Wylie's growth corridor along Highway 78). A Sachse address does not tell you which district you're in. Families who move to Sachse expecting Garland ISD enrollment — or Wylie ISD enrollment — and skip the address check have been unpleasantly surprised at closing.

Rowlett and the Dallas ISD Edge

A small number of addresses along the southern edge of Rowlett, near the Dallas city limit, may fall within Dallas ISD rather than Garland ISD. This is uncommon but worth checking if you are looking at homes in southern Rowlett ZIP codes.

New Construction Communities

New construction in the Sachse/Wylie/Rowlett corridor is particularly prone to ISD boundary confusion. Development phases in fast-growing communities sometimes open before official boundary assignments are finalized. If you are buying new construction in this area, verify the ISD assignment with the builder AND confirm it independently using the official district locator — builder sales teams do not always have the most current boundary information.

High School Attendance Zones Within Garland ISD

Even within confirmed Garland ISD territory, different neighborhoods feed different high schools. Garland ISD has six comprehensive high schools. If a specific campus matters to your family — the Early College Magnet at Garland HS, the MST Magnet at North Garland, or proximity to Naaman Forest — verify your address feeds that campus's attendance zone, not just that it falls within Garland ISD.

How to Verify

  • Garland ISD school locator: garlandisd.net/choose-your-school/school-directory
  • TEA school locator: tea.texas.gov/apps/school-directory

Making an offer without verifying your specific address can mean your child attends a different school than you planned — and boundary maps do not always match common-sense geography in this area. A quick address lookup before you submit an offer is the simplest insurance against this problem.

Tax Rate & District Finances

Garland ISD's 2025 adopted total tax rate is $1.1709 per $100 of assessed value — an increase of $0.12 from the 2024 rate of $1.0509. This increase reflects the district's investment in new facilities, bond-funded construction, and operational needs.

What the Tax Rate Means for Your Mortgage

On a $400,000 home (after the Texas $100,000 homestead exemption reducing taxable value to $300,000), the 2025 Garland ISD rate produces an estimated annual school tax of approximately $3,513, or roughly $293 per month. On a $550,000 home (taxable value $450,000), that becomes approximately $5,269/year ($439/month). This is a mid-range rate for DFW school districts — lower than some suburban districts like Wylie ISD ($1.21) and in line with Richardson ISD ($1.17) and Mesquite ISD ($1.17).

Texas Homestead Exemption: Texas law grants a $100,000 homestead exemption on school district taxes for your primary residence, reducing the taxable assessed value by $100,000 before applying the rate. File for your exemption with the Dallas County Appraisal District in the year you take ownership.

Per-Pupil Expenditure

Garland ISD's per-pupil expenditure is approximately $11,836 per student — below the national average for a US public school district of comparable size. This reflects the district's scale efficiencies rather than a lack of investment; the district's spending on dual credit and early college programs has saved families an estimated $5.7 million in tuition in a single year.

Bond Programs and Capital Investment

In 2025, Garland ISD broke ground on new campuses and Multi-Activity Centers (MACs), funded by a voter-approved bond program. Capital investment at this level typically precedes, not follows, a district's climb toward A-rated status — a pattern worth noting for families thinking about long-term neighborhood investment value.

How Garland ISD Compares

Families researching homes in east DFW most commonly cross-shop Garland ISD against Richardson ISD (to the north and west), Wylie ISD (which serves eastern Sachse and the Wylie/Murphy corridor), and sometimes Mesquite ISD (to the south). Here is how they stack up on key objective factors:

District Enrollment TEA Grade (2025) Tax Rate (2025) Best For
Garland ISD ~55,000 B (84) — improving $1.17 Program variety, magnet access, affordable east DFW housing, high CCMR
Richardson ISD ~38,000 B (85) $1.17 Lake Highlands and Richardson buyers wanting a mid-size district with strong academic reputation
Wylie ISD ~17,400 A (92) $1.21 Families prioritizing the highest TEA scores in a smaller, fast-growth district; parts of Sachse
Mesquite ISD ~38,000 C (76) $1.17 Mesquite and Sunnyvale area buyers; lower home prices

Families who most often choose Garland ISD over Richardson ISD do so for geography (proximity to the Lake Ray Hubbard corridor, Rowlett, eastern Garland) or for specific magnet program access, particularly the Early College track. Families who choose Wylie ISD over Garland ISD — when both are available via Sachse address — typically prioritize the raw TEA score and the smaller-district feel. The gap is meaningful: Wylie ISD's 92 (A) vs. Garland ISD's 84 (B) is measurable, though Garland ISD's 96% CCMR rate actually outpaces many A-rated districts on that specific measure. Mesquite ISD serves a different geography and is less commonly a direct comparison unless the family is specifically targeting the Mesquite/Sunnyvale area.

Community & District Culture

Garland ISD reflects the communities it serves — ethnically and economically diverse, with a strong emphasis on practical post-secondary outcomes over purely academic prestige. Families moving in from smaller suburban districts sometimes note the adjustment to a larger, more urban district feel, particularly at the high school level where campuses of 1,900–2,450 students are the norm. But families who engage with campus-level extracurriculars — athletics, fine arts, academic competitions — consistently describe high levels of parent involvement and school pride specific to each campus.

The Rowlett portion of the district has a notably strong community identity. Rowlett is a lakeside city with a distinct character, and Rowlett High School reflects that — athletic traditions, active booster culture, and a campus feel that reads more like a mid-size suburban district than a piece of a 55,000-student system. Families buying in Rowlett specifically for the community feel, not just the price point, often find the campus experience more cohesive than the district's size would suggest.

One detail that sets Garland ISD apart in parent conversations is the district's explicit focus on saving families money through dual credit and early college pathways. The $5.7 million in saved tuition costs in a single year is not a marketing talking point — it reflects a real structural commitment to getting students into college credit programs while still in high school. For families who value tangible outcomes over prestige rankings, this is a differentiating quality that does not always show up in standard district ratings.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does my Sachse address guarantee enrollment in Garland ISD? ▾
No. Sachse is served by both Garland ISD and Wylie ISD, depending on your specific address. The split generally follows an east-west divide — older, western Sachse neighborhoods tend to fall in Garland ISD, while newer eastern sections (particularly near the Highway 78 corridor) fall in Wylie ISD. City limits and ISD boundaries do not match. You must verify your specific street address using the Garland ISD school locator at garlandisd.net or the TEA school locator before purchasing a home. This is not a step to defer to closing — confirm before you make an offer.
How do Garland ISD's six high schools differ from each other? ▾
Garland ISD operates six comprehensive high schools: Garland, North Garland, South Garland, Naaman Forest, Lakeview Centennial, and Rowlett. The key academic differentiators are the magnet programs: Garland High School hosts the Early College HS Magnet (associate's degree or 60+ credit hours) and the Academy of Fine Arts. North Garland hosts the MST (Math, Science, Technology) Magnet and earned a TEA A rating in 2025. All six campuses offer AP courses and CTE pathways. If a specific campus or magnet program matters to your family, verify that your home address falls in that campus's attendance zone before buying — or factor in the magnet application process, which is open to students across the district.
How does Garland ISD handle IEP and special education transfers from another district? ▾
Texas law requires Garland ISD to convene an ARD (Admissions, Review, and Dismissal) meeting within 30 calendar days of a student with an active IEP enrolling in the district. The district is required to implement the previous IEP until the ARD meeting is held. Bring all prior evaluation reports, IEP documents, and any relevant service plans from your previous district. Contact Garland ISD's Special Services office as early as possible — ideally before your move — to discuss the process and timeline. Parent experience with special education services varies significantly by campus and by individual student needs; a direct conversation with the Special Services team is the most reliable way to assess whether the district can meet your child's specific requirements.
Why does Garland ISD have an A- from Niche but a B from TEA? ▾
Niche and TEA measure different things. TEA's A-F system is based primarily on STAAR test scores (student proficiency and growth), graduation rates, and CCMR — state-defined metrics with specific cutoff scores. Garland ISD's 2025 TEA score of 84 puts it solidly in the B range. Niche, as a third-party platform, weights additional factors including student-to-teacher ratios, college readiness signals, survey-based community reviews, and other variables. Garland ISD's strong CCMR rate (96%), its graduation rate (92.9%), and parent satisfaction signals likely boost its Niche grade above what the raw TEA score alone would produce. Use both as directional signals — neither is the complete picture.
What is Garland ISD's transfer policy for families moving in from another district? ▾
Garland ISD's inter-district Open Enrollment process allows students from outside the district to apply for enrollment at a Garland ISD campus. Applications are reviewed on a space-available basis — approval is not guaranteed, and the most popular campuses (including those with magnet programs) may have limited availability. The application window typically opens in late winter/early spring for the following school year. Once enrolled, intra-district transfers (changing campuses within Garland ISD) are also available through a separate Open Enrollment application process. For magnet programs specifically, a separate magnet application is required regardless of whether the student lives within district boundaries.
How does Garland ISD's 2025 tax rate compare to neighboring districts? ▾
Garland ISD's 2025 adopted total tax rate is $1.1709 per $100 of assessed value — the same as Richardson ISD ($1.17) and Mesquite ISD ($1.17), and slightly lower than Wylie ISD ($1.21). On a $450,000 home with Texas's $100,000 homestead exemption applied (taxable value $350,000), the estimated annual school tax under each rate is: Garland ISD ~$4,098, Richardson ISD ~$4,095, Wylie ISD ~$4,235. The differences are relatively modest at comparable home price points. Note: always verify the current certified rate with the Texas Comptroller's office at comptroller.texas.gov, as rates are set annually.
What makes Garland ISD's CCMR rate significant? ▾
Garland ISD's 96% College, Career, and Military Readiness (CCMR) rate is one of the highest in Dallas County, the greater DFW region, and the state — particularly notable because it comes from a district of 55,000 students, not a small selective program. CCMR measures whether graduating students leave high school with a concrete next step: a passing AP or IB exam score, dual credit completion, an industry certification, or demonstrated college readiness on a recognized assessment. A 96% rate means nearly all graduates exit with something tangible — not just a diploma. For families who value practical post-secondary outcomes (job-ready certifications, college credit, reduced tuition cost) alongside traditional academic metrics, this is a standout figure that the district's B rating does not fully capture.
