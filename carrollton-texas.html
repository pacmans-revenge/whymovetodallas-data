Location & Commute

Where Is Carrollton? Commutes & Getting Around

Carrollton sits roughly 20 miles north of Downtown Dallas and about 35 miles northeast of Fort Worth, straddling Dallas and Denton County (with a small slice of Collin County in the northeast corner). The city is threaded by I-35E running north-south through its heart, President George Bush Turnpike (SH-190) cutting east-west across the north side, and Sam Rayburn Tollway (SH-121) giving fast access west toward DFW Airport. It borders Farmers Branch and Addison to the south, Coppell to the west, and Lewisville and The Colony to the north.

What makes Carrollton genuinely different from almost every other DFW suburb: it's a DART member city with real rail access. The DART Green Line runs through Downtown Carrollton and North Carrollton/Frankford stations, with direct service to downtown Dallas in about 30–35 minutes with no parking headaches. The newer DART Silver Line (opened late 2025) extends through North Carrollton/Frankford all the way to DFW International Airport — a huge advantage for corporate transferees and frequent flyers. For families used to transit-accessible cities in California or the Northeast, this is a meaningful quality-of-life differentiator.

Rush-hour traffic on I-35E southbound and Belt Line Road can add 15–25 minutes to typical commute times. Budget for tolls on PGBT and SH-121 if you work west or north.
Education

Schools in Carrollton — A Three-ISD City

Carrollton is served by three school districts depending on which part of the city you live in — this is one of the most important things to understand before buying. Carrollton-Farmers Branch ISD (CFBISD) covers most of the city (zip codes 75006 and 75007). Lewisville ISD serves the northwest portion of Carrollton (primarily zip code 75010, including the Hebron High School feeder zone). A very small slice of southern Carrollton touches Dallas ISD. Your specific street address determines your ISD — always verify at cfbisd.edu or lisd.net before making any real estate decision.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Carrollton

Carrollton is a working suburb in the truest sense — not a resort-style master-planned community, but a real city where families have lived for decades. That means mature trees, established schools, a genuinely diverse population, and the kind of cultural depth you don't find in suburbs that have only existed since 2010.

Youth Sports & Athletics

CFBISD and LISD both run competitive UIL programs across football, soccer, baseball, basketball, and swimming. The Carrollton Recreation Division adds community leagues for kids 4–18, and select-level clubs in soccer, baseball, and gymnastics operate within easy driving distance.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

The Cottonwood Creek Trail connects several parks through the city, giving families genuine trail access without leaving town. Josey Ranch Lake Park is the standout — 90+ acres with a fishing lake, splash pad, and sports courts. Over 60 parks total spread across established neighborhoods.

Belt Line Road Food Culture

Belt Line Road through Carrollton is one of DFW's most authentic international dining corridors — Vietnamese pho, Korean BBQ, Indian restaurants, Middle Eastern grocers, Ethiopian spots, and more. It's a genuine local treasure and a significant lifestyle draw for families who care about food diversity.

Old Town Events & Community

Old Town Carrollton is a genuine walkable neighborhood hub with local restaurants, boutiques, and a regular calendar of community events — festivals, a farmers market, and seasonal gatherings. It gives Carrollton a small-town square feel despite being a city of 136,000.

DART Rail Access

Carrollton is one of a small number of DFW suburbs served by DART light rail — the Green Line and Silver Line both run through the city. For families where one spouse works Downtown Dallas, car-free commuting is genuinely viable here in a way it simply isn't from Frisco, Prosper, or Allen.

Diverse, Established Community

Carrollton's decades-long history as a working suburb means a genuinely diverse, rooted community. You're likely to have neighbors who've lived on the same street for 15–20 years. The city's cultural diversity shows up in its schools, restaurants, religious institutions, and community organizations.

Honest trade-off: Carrollton doesn't have the master-planned resort feel of newer Frisco or Prosper neighborhoods. There's no HOA-maintained landscape at every corner, no community lazy rivers, and new construction is very limited. What you get instead is an established city with mature trees, real neighborhood character, and a price point that leaves meaningful money in your budget — often $100K–$150K less than equivalent square footage 15 miles north.
Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & City Amenities

Like most of the DFW metro, Carrollton is a car-dependent city for most daily needs. The DART Green and Silver lines change the equation for Downtown Dallas commuters, but weekend errands and school runs still require a car. The trade-off: Carrollton's park system, trail network, and recreation programming are more developed than most families expect for an established city its size. Josey Ranch Lake Park and the Cottonwood Creek Trail system are genuine assets, not afterthoughts.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Carrollton maintains over 60 parks covering more than 900 acres, plus the Cottonwood Creek Trail system connecting key park nodes across the city. For an established suburban city, it's a well-developed outdoor network.

Josey Ranch Lake Park

Carrollton's flagship park — 90+ acres with a fishing lake, splash pad, sports courts, walking trails, and open lawn space. A genuine community gathering point, especially on weekends and summer evenings.

Cottonwood Creek Trail

5+ miles of connected paved trail running through the heart of Carrollton, linking multiple parks and green corridors. Popular with families for biking, jogging, and stroller walks — one of the best recreational assets in the city.

Indian Creek Park

Located on the northwest side, this park features sports fields, recreation facilities, and access to the greenways surrounding Indian Creek Golf Club. Well-used by families living in the NW Carrollton corridor.

Rosemeade Park & Area Parks

Rosemeade Park anchors a cluster of community parks in central-south Carrollton with fields, playgrounds, and picnic areas. The city's park distribution means most neighborhoods have a community park within a 5–10 minute walk.

Twin Creeks & Neighborhood Parks

Twin Creeks Park and numerous smaller neighborhood parks fill out Carrollton's park grid — sports fields, basketball courts, playgrounds, and open green space. Most established neighborhoods have a park within easy walking distance.

Indian Creek Golf Club

A 27-hole public golf course on the northwest side of Carrollton — open to the public and family-friendly, adding a significant tract of green space to that part of the city. Junior golf programs are available seasonally.

Safety & Crime

Is Carrollton Safe?

Carrollton's violent crime rate is meaningfully below the U.S. national average, making it a safe option for families from that perspective. Property crime — vehicle break-ins, package theft, and retail theft concentrated along commercial corridors like Belt Line and I-35E frontage roads — runs above state and national averages, which is typical for larger suburban cities with extensive retail development. Carrollton is not unsafe; it simply isn't as quiet as a newer, smaller master-planned suburb with limited commercial exposure. Residential neighborhoods are generally calm and well-maintained.

Violent Crime Rate

~1.4 per 1,000 residents — well below the U.S. national average of ~4.0 per 1,000. Carrollton consistently ranks among the safer large suburbs in DFW for violent crime.

Property Crime

Above state and national averages — consistent with larger suburban cities with major commercial corridors. Residential neighborhoods are calm; commercial and retail zones see higher activity.

Carrollton Police Dept.

Full-service accredited department with active community policing programs, neighborhood watch coordination, and consistent commercial corridor patrols. More at cityofcarrollton.com/police.

New Construction

New Homes in Carrollton

Carrollton is a fully established suburb — the kind of place where the neighborhoods are set, the trees are mature, and the community feel is already there when you move in. There's little vacant land left for large-scale new residential development, so most families buying here are focused on the resale market. That's not a disadvantage: Carrollton has strong inventory across a range of price points, lot sizes, and neighborhood styles, and resale homes often come with finished landscaping, custom touches, and HOA communities that are already thriving. If you're weighing Carrollton against a suburb with active new construction, the honest trade-off is this — you're giving up the ability to choose your finishes and get a builder warranty, and gaining a suburb where the character and school patterns are well-established and predictable. For many relocating families, that's exactly what they're looking for.   • DART rail access — Green Line + Silver Line stops make car-free Downtown Dallas commuting genuinely viable; a rare asset in DFW suburbs

— What to Know

  • CFBISD rated B/B+ — solid but trails Frisco ISD (A+), Plano ISD (A), and neighboring Coppell ISD (A); families prioritizing elite ISD ratings should weigh this carefully

  • Property crime above average — consistent with larger suburban cities; residential neighborhoods are calm, but commercial areas see more vehicle break-ins and retail theft

Carrollton is a solid choice for families who prioritize central DFW location, transit access, and established neighborhoods at a price point meaningfully below the leading north suburbs. With a population of 136,000+, it offers genuine community depth — active youth sports leagues, a park system covering 900+ acres, multicultural dining along Belt Line Road, and an Old Town district with regular events. CFBISD earns a B/B+ from Niche, which is respectable but not the top-ranked school district in DFW. Families who value affordability, accessibility, and genuine neighborhood character over brand-new construction often find Carrollton fits their criteria well.

Top CFBISD campuses include Creekview High School and R.L. Turner High School.
