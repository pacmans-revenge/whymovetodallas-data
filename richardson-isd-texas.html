Richardson ISD at a Glance

~38,000 Students
55 Campuses
B (85) TEA Grade (2023)
A− Niche Grade (3rd party)
~97% Graduation Rate
$1.17 Tax Rate / $100 (2024)

Richardson ISD is a large, well-established district serving approximately 38,000 students across 55 campuses in one of DFW's most geographically complex service territories. Unlike districts that align cleanly with a single suburb, RISD spans four distinct city governments — the city of Richardson, the Lake Highlands neighborhood of northeast Dallas, slices of Plano ZIP codes, and sections of Garland. That geographic spread is the most important thing a relocating family needs to understand up front: your address, not your city name, determines your campus assignment.

For families who do their homework on boundaries, RISD offers real academic depth. The International Baccalaureate (IB) diploma programme at Lake Highlands High School, strong GT pathways, dual language programs at select elementary campuses, and a nationally recognized fine arts tradition at Richardson High give the district breadth that many same-size peers lack. The district's 2023 TEA score of 85 (B) places it above comparable-size urban-adjacent districts, and Niche rates it A− among Texas districts. The honest tradeoff: this is not a homogenous suburban district where every campus looks the same — the experience varies meaningfully by school and part of the district.

Families often shortlist RISD when they want IB access, appreciate the walkable, established feel of Richardson and Lake Highlands, or are buying within reach of UT Dallas — the district sits adjacent to UTD's campus, creating dual-credit and enrichment pathways that outlying suburban districts simply can't replicate. If you're weighing RISD against Plano ISD (which also serves parts of this area), the decision typically comes down to specific campus preferences and which street your target home sits on — boundary lines here do not always follow intuitive geography.

Best Fit For Families Who…

  • Want IB diploma programme access at the high school level
  • Value an established, walkable urban-suburban community feel
  • Are drawn to strong fine arts, theater, or performing arts programs
  • Want GT pathways from elementary through high school
  • Appreciate proximity to UT Dallas for dual credit and enrichment
  • Are comparing options between Richardson and nearby Plano neighborhoods

May Not Be the Best Fit If…

  • You need an A-rated TEA district and won't consider anything below
  • You're looking for a small, tight-knit district where most students move through together K–12
  • You want a single-city district with straightforward, predictable boundaries
  • Your primary priority is the lowest possible tax rate in the area
⚠ Boundary Verification — Essential

Not every home with a Richardson, Dallas (Lake Highlands), Plano, or Garland mailing address falls within Richardson ISD boundaries. The district's territory crosses four city limits, and several streets within those cities are zoned to neighboring ISDs. Always verify your specific address at risd.org or by contacting the RISD enrollment office before making an offer.

This is especially important in new construction communities near the Plano/Richardson boundary and in the Garland-adjacent portions of RISD territory, where a single block can cross into a different district.

TEA Accountability Rating

Richardson ISD earned a B (85 out of 100) on the Texas Education Agency's 2023 A-F accountability system. TEA accountability ratings evaluate school performance across three primary domains:

  • Student Achievement — STAAR test proficiency rates in reading, math, and other subjects, plus graduation rates and CCMR performance
  • School Progress — Growth scores measuring how much students improve year-over-year, and district performance relative to similar campuses statewide
  • Closing the Gaps — Performance equity across student groups, including English learners, students with disabilities, and economically disadvantaged students

A score of 85 — comfortably in B territory — means RISD is outperforming many urban-adjacent large districts in Texas. To put the number in practical terms: a B (85) district is typically one that performs well on proficiency and growth measures but may have specific campuses or student-group gaps that pull the composite score below A range. For a district serving a geographically diverse, multi-city territory, this score reflects genuine academic strength. Importantly, RISD's 85 score is higher than both Plano ISD (82) and Garland ISD (82) based on 2023 TEA data — a fact that often surprises families who assume the comparably-priced Plano district must outperform RISD on all metrics.

Note on TEA Data

TEA A-F ratings are published annually in the fall and may lag the most recent school year by 12–18 months. Campus-level ratings can differ significantly from the district composite — always check individual campus scorecards at txschools.gov for the campuses most relevant to your family.

Academic Performance

Richardson ISD's academic profile reflects its geographic complexity: strong district-level metrics with meaningful campus-to-campus variation. When evaluating RISD, the district composite is a reasonable starting point — but individual campus performance matters more for most families.

STAAR proficiency (2022–23): District-wide reading and math proficiency rates for RISD generally align with statewide averages for districts of its size and demographic profile. Campuses in the Richardson and northwestern portions of the district tend to post higher proficiency rates than campuses in the eastern and Garland-adjacent zones, reflecting variation in student population mix. Verify campus-level STAAR data at txschools.gov for the specific schools your family would attend.

Graduation rate: RISD consistently reports a four-year graduation rate of approximately 97%, placing it among the stronger performers for a district of its size in the Dallas County footprint.

AP and dual credit: All four RISD high schools offer a range of AP courses. Lake Highlands High School additionally offers the full IB Diploma Programme for students who qualify and apply. Dual credit coursework through Collin College and other partnering institutions is available at multiple campuses — verify current offerings with the district, as partnerships evolve year to year.

CCMR (College, Career, and Military Readiness): RISD reports strong CCMR rates, meaning a high percentage of graduates exit with at least one qualifying indicator — an AP exam score, dual credit hours, an industry certification, or military enlistment. The district's breadth of CTE programs at all four high schools contributes meaningfully to this number.

What the numbers mean for a relocating family: RISD is a district where the aggregate metrics are solid, but where the campus your child attends matters considerably. A family whose children would attend J.J. Pearce High School or Lake Highlands High School is in a different academic environment than one whose children attend a campus in the district's more eastern territory. Do the campus-level research before assuming the district-wide grade applies uniformly.

Key Campuses

Richardson ISD operates four comprehensive high schools, along with numerous middle and elementary campuses across its service area. All four high schools offer competitive academic programs; they differ primarily in geographic location, campus culture, and specialty program access.

Richardson High School

High School 9–12 Fine Arts Tradition

The oldest high school in the district, serving students primarily in the western Richardson area. Richardson High is particularly known for its performing arts program — theater, orchestra, and choir have won state recognition — making it a strong choice for families prioritizing fine arts alongside standard academics.

Lake Highlands High School

High School 9–12 IB Diploma Programme

The home of RISD's International Baccalaureate (IB) Diploma Programme — the only IB campus in the district. Serves the Lake Highlands (northeast Dallas) portion of the district. Families specifically seeking IB need to confirm their address feeds to Lake Highlands High or understand the application process if a transfer is required.

J.J. Pearce High School

High School 9–12 Strong AP Program

Located in central Richardson, Pearce is often cited by RISD parents as among the district's most academically competitive high school environments. Strong AP course catalog and high college-going culture. A common destination for families buying in the Pearce Cluster neighborhoods of Richardson.

H.H. Berkner High School

High School 9–12 East RISD Territory

Serves the eastern portion of RISD's territory near the Garland border. Berkner offers the same core RISD curriculum and CTE programming as the other high schools. Families in this part of the district should verify whether specific magnet or advanced program pathways are available on-campus or require inter-campus access.

West Junior High School

Middle School 7–9 GS: Above Average

One of RISD's higher-rated middle campuses per GreatSchools, serving students who will feed into Pearce and Richardson High clusters. Families buying in the central Richardson area typically pass through West JH or its peers; verify your specific campus assignment with RISD's address lookup.

Select Elementary GT Campuses

Elementary GT Designated

RISD designates specific elementary campuses as GT cluster sites, where identified gifted-and-talented students receive pull-out enrichment. Access is through the district's formal GT identification process, not automatic by address. Contact RISD's Gifted Education department for current designated campus locations.

Campus-level GreatSchools ratings and enrollment figures are updated periodically. Verify current data at GreatSchools.org and txschools.gov.

Specialty Programs

Richardson ISD offers a range of specialized academic programs, but a key detail for relocating families: most are campus-limited, not districtwide. The programs below require active enrollment steps or address-verified campus assignment — don't assume a program is available at whatever campus your home feeds.

International Baccalaureate (IB)

Campus: Lake Highlands High School only — not districtwide.
Access: Students whose home address feeds to Lake Highlands High enroll directly. Students from other RISD campuses may apply for a school choice transfer; acceptance is not guaranteed.
Practical implication: Families specifically targeting the IB pathway should verify their address feeds to Lake Highlands High before making an offer on a home. If it doesn't, factor in the transfer process.

Gifted & Talented (GT)

Access: Districtwide identification process; GT cluster services concentrated at designated campuses per grade level. Identification requires formal assessment — not automatic by address.
Practical implication: GT-identified students may attend their neighborhood campus or access a designated GT cluster site. Contact RISD's Gifted Education office to understand current cluster assignments.

Dual Language Program

Campus: Available at select elementary campuses — not districtwide.
Access: Application-based, with priority typically given to students in the campus attendance zone. Spanish/English dual language available; verify which campuses currently offer the program directly with RISD.
Practical implication: Families prioritizing dual language should confirm which elementary campuses offer the program and whether their home address feeds to one of those campuses.

STEM Pathways

Campus: Available at all four high schools through AP STEM coursework and CTE career pathways; depth and specific course offerings vary by campus.
Access: No separate application — students access STEM pathways through course selection within their assigned high school.
Practical implication: Families can reasonably expect solid STEM course access regardless of high school assignment, though flagship AP science and engineering depth may vary.

Fine Arts Programs

Campus: Present districtwide; particularly recognized at Richardson High School, which has produced state-award-winning theater, orchestra, and choir programs.
Access: Fine arts are part of the standard curriculum with audition/tryout processes for competitive ensembles at the high school level.
Practical implication: Families prioritizing fine arts at a high level may specifically target the Richardson High attendance zone — verify your cluster assignment accordingly.

Career & Technical Education (CTE)

Campus: Offered at all four high schools through a range of pathways including business, health science, technology, and skilled trades.
Access: Available to all RISD high school students through course selection; some advanced or specialized CTE courses may be offered at specific campuses.
Practical implication: CTE credentialing and industry certification opportunities make RISD competitive for families whose students are interested in non-four-year-college pathways.

Enrollment Guide

Richardson ISD enrollment steps as of the 2025–26 school year. Time-sensitive details — windows, required documents, portal URLs — should be verified at risd.org before beginning the process, as the district updates requirements annually.

Step 1 — Verify Your Campus Assignment

Before completing any paperwork, use RISD's online address lookup tool to confirm which campuses your home address feeds. This is non-optional — RISD's boundaries cross multiple city limits, and your campus assignment may not match your city of residence.

Step 2 — Gather Residency Documentation

RISD requires proof of residency to establish enrollment. Typical documentation includes a current utility bill (gas, water, or electric) or mortgage/lease agreement in the parent or guardian's name, plus a government-issued photo ID. Contact the RISD enrollment office to confirm the current accepted document list.

Step 3 — Complete Online Registration

New student registration is handled through RISD's online portal. Visit risd.org and navigate to the enrollment section for the current registration link. You will need: student birth certificate, current immunization records, most recent report card or transcript, and residency documentation.

Step 4 — Enrollment Window (2025–26)

RISD typically opens online pre-registration for returning students in the spring, with new student enrollment available year-round on a rolling basis. Mid-year enrollment is accepted; contact the campus directly to schedule a registration appointment for enrollment outside the standard fall window.

Step 5 — Intra-District Transfers

Transfers between RISD campuses are possible but not guaranteed. Capacity at the receiving campus is the primary determining factor. Submit an intra-district transfer request through the RISD district office. Transfers for specialty programs (IB, dual language) follow program-specific application processes separate from the general transfer request.

Step 6 — Inter-District Transfers

Families relocating from another Texas school district to RISD are accepted on a space-available basis. Submit enrollment paperwork to the home campus and contact the RISD enrollment office for the current inter-district transfer policy and timeline for the 2025–26 year.

Step 7 — IEP / 504 Transfers

Under Texas law, Richardson ISD must convene an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting within 30 days of a student with an active IEP enrolling in the district. Bring all prior IEP, 504, evaluation reports, and special education records at enrollment. Contact RISD's Special Education department (part of Student Services) before or at enrollment to begin the ARD scheduling process. Do not assume the previous IEP rolls over without an ARD meeting.

Boundary Verification — Read This Carefully

Richardson ISD has one of the more complex boundary maps in DFW. The district's service territory does not align with any single city's limits — it is defined by the historical evolution of school district formation across what became multiple incorporated cities. The result: a patchwork of RISD and non-RISD territory that is not obvious from a map, a real estate listing, or even a city address.

Cities and Areas Served — With Important Caveats

  • Richardson (most): The majority of the city of Richardson falls within RISD, but not all of it. Parts of Richardson are served by Plano ISD, particularly near the northern city boundary.
  • Lake Highlands / Northeast Dallas: A substantial portion of RISD's territory lies within the city of Dallas — specifically the Lake Highlands neighborhood. These students have Dallas city addresses but attend RISD, not Dallas ISD.
  • Parts of Plano: Select ZIP codes and subdivisions in Plano are within RISD boundaries. Homes in these areas may carry a Plano mailing address but fall in Richardson ISD. The reverse is also true — some Plano-addressed homes are in Plano ISD, not RISD.
  • Parts of Garland: The eastern margin of RISD's territory extends into areas with Garland addresses, near the Berkner High School service area.

Common Edge Cases

  • Homes near the northern Richardson/Plano boundary: some streets alternate between RISD and Plano ISD block by block
  • New construction communities near Campbell Road or Spring Creek Pkwy areas: boundary verification is essential — these zones are active areas of RISD/Plano ISD split
  • Lake Highlands apartments and townhomes: the Lake Highlands area includes a mix of single-family and multi-family housing; confirm RISD zoning versus Dallas ISD for your specific address
  • Within RISD, attendance zone boundaries for middle and high school also vary — your elementary assignment does not automatically predict your high school cluster

How to Verify

  • RISD address lookup: risd.org — contact the enrollment office for address-specific campus assignment
  • TEA school locator: tea.texas.gov — cross-reference district and campus assignment by address
Home-Search Implication

Making an offer on a home without verifying your specific address in RISD's lookup tool can mean your child attends a different school — or a different district — than you planned. Boundary maps published by real estate portals and MLS systems are frequently inaccurate for districts with complex boundary histories like RISD. Verify directly with the district, not with a map. This is especially important if IB access or a specific high school cluster is part of your family's criteria.

Taxes & District Finances

2024 certified total tax rate: $1.17 per $100 of assessed value. This rate includes both the Maintenance & Operations (M&O) levy and the Interest & Sinking (I&S) bond levy. Richardson ISD's rate sits above the DFW district median — Plano ISD runs at $1.04, Grapevine-Colleyville ISD at approximately $0.92 — which is a real consideration when comparing homes across district lines.

What does $1.17 mean in practice? On a $450,000 home assessed at full value, annual school property taxes run approximately $5,265 — about $439 per month embedded in your escrow payment. Texas's $100,000 homestead exemption reduces the assessed value used for M&O calculation, providing some relief for primary residence owners. On a $550,000 home with the homestead exemption applied to the M&O portion, total school taxes will be somewhat below the straight multiplication — contact Dallas County or Collin County appraisal districts for an exact estimate based on the specific property.

Compared to neighboring districts: Plano ISD ($1.04) would cost roughly $585 less per year on a $450,000 home. Garland ISD ($1.17) is at parity with RISD. Grapevine-Colleyville ISD ($0.92) is meaningfully lower, though homes in that territory are generally located further west. The rate difference between RISD and Plano ISD is an important factor for families comparing homes on either side of the boundary.

Per-pupil expenditure: Richardson ISD spends in the range typical for mid-to-large Texas districts — verify the current figure at txschools.gov or the Texas Comptroller's school finance data for the most recent PEIMS-reported year.

Recent bond activity: RISD has historically passed facility bonds to maintain and update its older campus inventory — some campuses date to the 1950s and 1960s, unlike newer suburban districts where most facilities are more recent. Bond-funded improvements affect both facility quality and, over time, the I&S portion of the tax rate. Verify current bond project status at risd.org.

How Richardson ISD Compares

Families looking at RISD territory most often cross-shop with Plano ISD (which serves adjacent areas) and Garland ISD (which borders RISD to the east). Here's how the districts compare on key metrics:

District Enrollment TEA Grade (2023) Tax Rate (2024) Best For
Richardson ISD ~38,000 B (85) $1.17 IB access, fine arts, established neighborhoods
Plano ISD ~49,000 B (82) $1.04 Lower tax rate, larger program variety, name recognition
Garland ISD ~55,000 B (82) $1.17 Lower home prices at comparable tax rate, larger district

A few things the table doesn't capture: Richardson ISD's TEA score of 85 is actually higher than both Plano (82) and Garland (82) on 2023 data — a fact that surprises many families who expect Plano to lead on all academic metrics. The practical tradeoff is that Plano ISD offers a lower tax rate ($1.04 vs. $1.17), which on a $450,000 home adds up to roughly $585 more per year in RISD. Families who care specifically about the IB pathway will not find it in Plano ISD or Garland ISD — it's a Richardson ISD-only offering in this part of DFW.

Garland ISD serves the eastern sections of the region at the same $1.17 rate as RISD, with generally lower home prices in its territory. Families for whom campus culture and fine arts programming are priorities tend to lean toward RISD over Garland; families optimizing purely for price-per-square-foot of home may find the eastern RISD/Garland area more affordable.

Community Feel & What Parents Say

Richardson ISD draws families who appreciate depth over polish. Unlike the newest suburban districts — where the schools, neighborhoods, and community infrastructure are all a similar age — RISD territory is layered. Richardson itself is a city that grew up around the telecommunications industry (Ericsson, Fujitsu, Cisco, and AT&T have all had or have offices there), and the community retains an educated, civic-minded culture that shows up in school involvement and extracurricular life. Parent booster organizations at RISD high schools tend to be active and well-funded; athletics, theater, and band programs benefit from serious volunteer and donor engagement.

The Lake Highlands portion of the district has its own distinct character — a walkable, tree-lined urban neighborhood inside Dallas city limits, popular with young families and longtime residents alike. Parents in Lake Highlands frequently cite the neighborhood feel as part of why they chose RISD territory over a more typical suburban option, even knowing the tax rate is not the lowest in the area. The IB programme at Lake Highlands High draws motivated families specifically seeking that academic pathway.

Families who have lived in RISD territory note that the district's size and geographic diversity mean campus-to-campus variation is real and worth investigating. The Pearce cluster and Richardson High cluster feel different from the Berkner cluster — in campus culture, academic intensity, extracurricular depth, and the neighborhoods they draw from. Parents on forums like Reddit's r/Dallas and r/DFW consistently recommend that newcomers visit their specific assigned campus, not just research the district overall. The aggregate rating tells part of the story; the campus you'll actually attend tells the rest. One consistent positive across the district: RISD's fine arts and performing arts traditions are frequently called out as a genuine district-wide strength that distinguishes it from peers of similar size.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does my Richardson or Lake Highlands address guarantee enrollment in RISD? ▾
Not necessarily. Richardson ISD's boundaries cross four city limits — the city of Richardson, northeast Dallas (Lake Highlands), parts of Plano, and parts of Garland. A Richardson mailing address does not automatically mean RISD; some Richardson-addressed homes fall in Plano ISD near the northern city boundary. Similarly, Lake Highlands addresses are within Dallas city limits but many (not all) are in RISD rather than Dallas ISD. Always use RISD's address lookup tool at risd.org, or contact the enrollment office directly with the specific property address before making an offer.
What's the difference between Richardson High, Lake Highlands High, Pearce, and Berkner? ▾
All four are comprehensive high schools offering the full RISD curriculum, AP courses, and CTE pathways — but they have distinct identities. Richardson High is known for its performing arts and fine arts tradition, with state-recognized theater, orchestra, and choir programs. Lake Highlands High is the only campus offering the IB Diploma Programme, making it a destination for families specifically seeking IB. J.J. Pearce High has a reputation among RISD parents as academically competitive, with a strong AP culture. H.H. Berkner serves the eastern portion of the district near the Garland border. Your home address determines which high school your children attend; if you have a specific campus preference, verify your address feeds there before buying.
How do I access the IB programme at Lake Highlands High School? ▾
The International Baccalaureate Diploma Programme at Lake Highlands High School is available to students whose home address is in the Lake Highlands High attendance zone — they enroll through standard RISD registration. Students from other RISD high school zones may apply for an intra-district school choice transfer to access IB; transfer approval depends on capacity at Lake Highlands and is not guaranteed. If IB is a firm priority for your family, the cleanest path is buying a home in the Lake Highlands High attendance zone and verifying the assignment with RISD before purchase.
What does a TEA B (85) mean versus Niche's A−? Why do they differ? ▾
The TEA A-F system is a state accountability measure based primarily on STAAR test proficiency, student growth, and equity across student groups. A score of 85 (B) means RISD performs solidly on state-defined metrics, particularly relative to districts of comparable size and demographic mix. Niche's A− grade incorporates additional data points — SAT/ACT scores, teacher quality reviews, student-teacher ratios, and user survey data — which tend to weight factors like academic reputation and campus resources somewhat differently. Neither system is "right" and both are useful; the TEA score is more standardized and comparable across Texas, while Niche reflects a broader community perception signal. For most families, the more useful exercise is reviewing individual campus TEA scorecards at txschools.gov rather than relying on either district composite.
How does RISD handle IEP and 504 transfers when we move in from another state? ▾
Under Texas law, Richardson ISD must convene an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting within 30 calendar days of a student with an active IEP enrolling in the district. The ARD committee will review the student's existing IEP and determine how to implement it in RISD — this may mean adopting the existing plan, modifying it, or conducting new assessments. For 504 plans, the district similarly reviews the existing plan and determines appropriate accommodations. Bring all documentation — prior IEP, 504, evaluation reports, and records from the prior district — to your enrollment appointment. Contact RISD's Special Education/Student Services department before or at enrollment to begin scheduling the ARD meeting promptly. Do not assume any prior plan is automatically honored without an ARD meeting.
How does RISD's $1.17 tax rate compare to nearby districts? ▾
Richardson ISD's 2024 certified total tax rate of $1.17 per $100 assessed value is higher than some neighboring districts but at parity with others. Plano ISD runs at approximately $1.04 — meaning a $450,000 home in Plano ISD runs about $585 less per year in school taxes than the same-valued home in RISD. Garland ISD is at parity with RISD at $1.17. Grapevine-Colleyville ISD carries one of the lowest rates in DFW at approximately $0.92, though its territory is further west. The $1.17 rate reflects RISD's bond obligations for campus maintenance and improvement in an older, established district with facilities dating to the 1950s and 60s. Texas's $100,000 homestead exemption reduces the M&O assessed value for primary residences, providing partial relief. All rates should be verified with the Dallas and Collin County appraisal districts for the current certified year.
Do some homes with Plano or Garland addresses actually fall in Richardson ISD? ▾
Yes — and the reverse is also true. Some homes with Plano mailing addresses fall within Richardson ISD boundaries; some Richardson-addressed homes are actually in Plano ISD. This is a byproduct of RISD's district formation history, which predates much of the surrounding suburban development. The only reliable way to determine which district a specific home falls in is to use RISD's address lookup tool or contact the RISD enrollment office with the exact street address. Real estate listing platforms and tax records often display the city name, not the school district — these are not the same thing and should not be used as a substitute for direct district verification.
