Location & Commute

Where Is Highland Village? Commutes & Getting Around

Highland Village sits about 32 miles northwest of Downtown Dallas and roughly 40 miles northeast of Downtown Fort Worth, entirely in Denton County. FM 407 is the primary east-west corridor through the city, connecting to I-35E (south toward Lewisville and Dallas) and FM 1171 (east toward Flower Mound and Frisco). Lake Lewisville forms the city's northern boundary.

Highland Village's location places it solidly in the mid-commute tier for Dallas — further than Coppell or Flower Mound, but with a meaningful quality-of-life premium from the lake access and small-city feel. For families commuting to the DFW Airport corridor, Frisco/The Star, or the Lewisville employer belt, the drive is manageable. Downtown Dallas in rush hour is a genuine 45–55 minute commitment.

No DART in Highland Village. Highland Village is not a DART member city. DCTA (Denton County Transportation Authority) operates limited A-train rail service in Lewisville and Denton but does not serve Highland Village directly. A personal vehicle is essential. I-35E south can back up significantly between Lewisville and Dallas during rush hour — plan for 45–55 minutes to Downtown Dallas in the morning. The Lewisville Lake Toll Bridge (SH-121) is the faster westbound route toward DFW Airport.
Education

Lewisville ISD — Schools in Highland Village

Highland Village is served by Lewisville ISD — one of the largest and most respected ISDs in Denton County, with an A rating from both TEA and Niche. Most Highland Village students attend either Flower Mound High School or Marcus High School, both of which are among the strongest high schools in the Lewisville ISD system and carry 9/10 GreatSchools ratings.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Highland Village

Highland Village is one of DFW's most intentionally family-focused communities. With 16,000 residents, a fully built-out infrastructure, and Lake Lewisville on the doorstep, daily life here has a small-town feel that's genuinely hard to find this close to a major metro.

Youth Sports & Athletics

Highland Village Athletic Association (HVAA) runs baseball, softball, soccer, and flag football. Lewisville ISD feeds into Marcus HS — one of DFW's most competitive athletic programs. Pilot Knoll's boat launches fuel summer water sports.

Outdoor Living & Lake Access

Pilot Knoll Park on Lake Lewisville is Highland Village's signature — boat ramps, sandy beaches, and picnic areas managed by the Army Corps of Engineers. Grapevine Creek trail system runs through neighborhoods for walking and biking.

Shopping & Dining

In-city dining and retail is limited — Highland Village's small footprint means most shopping happens at the Lewisville/FM 2499 corridor or Grapevine Mills (~20 min). A few local restaurants near City Hall. Residents treat this as a feature: quieter streets, less commercial congestion.

Community Events & Culture

The city runs Movies in the Park, Highland Village Days festival, food truck events at the Recreation Center, and holiday programming. The City Council is active and responsive — a hallmark of smaller DFW cities where residents actually know their elected officials.

What Makes HV Unique

Fully built-out with no MUDs or PIDs — meaning no surprise tax districts on top of your property tax bill. Combined with 92% homeownership and $164K median income, the neighborhood stability is exceptional. Residents move in and stay for decades.

Community Feel

With only 16,000 residents, everyone knows their neighbors. Parent networks through LISD, youth sports, and church communities are strong. Newcomers consistently cite how easy it is to get plugged in — especially families with school-age kids.

The honest tradeoff: Highland Village has almost no nightlife, very limited dining variety, and you'll drive for most daily needs. If you want walkability, urban energy, or a wide restaurant scene nearby, this suburb isn't it. But if a tight-knit community, premium schools, and lake access sound like the right trade, HV delivers that better than almost anywhere in DFW.
Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & City Amenities

Highland Village is one of DFW's most car-dependent cities — a Walk Score of 1 reflects a community designed entirely around residential streets, not commercial walkability. There is no DART rail, no commuter rail, and no public bus. A personal vehicle is required for all daily life. What HV offers in return: 19 parks, lake access, and quiet residential streets built for family life.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Highland Village maintains 19 parks across roughly 6.5 square miles — a strong ratio for a city this size. The trail system follows Grapevine Creek through several neighborhoods, providing a connected green corridor for walking, jogging, and light biking.

Pilot Knoll Park

Direct access to Lake Lewisville — boat ramps, sandy beach area, fishing, and picnic facilities managed by the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers. The most significant outdoor asset in the city.

Grapevine Creek Trail System

Multi-use trail running through multiple neighborhoods along Grapevine Creek. Connects several community parks and is the primary recreational trail for residents.

Village Park

The city's main neighborhood park adjacent to the Recreation Center. Includes athletic fields, playgrounds, pavilion, and open green space frequently used for community events.

Campion Trail Connection

Highland Village connects to the broader Lewisville Lake Trail system via Campion Trail, extending trail access significantly beyond city limits for longer-distance cycling and running.

Coppice Park & Quail Run Park

Neighborhood parks with playgrounds and open areas scattered through residential sections. Typical DFW suburban green space — well-maintained and well-used by local families.

Lake Lewisville at Large

The reservoir itself covers 29,000 acres. Beyond Pilot Knoll, residents access other Army Corps parks around the lake (Lewisville Lake Park, Oakland Park) within a short drive for expanded camping, fishing, and water access.

New Construction

New Homes in Highland Village

Highland Village is a fully established suburb — the kind of place where the neighborhoods are set, the trees are mature, and the community feel is already there when you move in. There's little vacant land left for large-scale new residential development, so most families buying here are focused on the resale market. That's not a disadvantage: Highland Village has strong inventory across a range of price points, lot sizes, and neighborhood styles, and resale homes often come with finished landscaping, custom touches, and HOA communities that are already thriving. If you're weighing Highland Village against a suburb with active new construction, the honest trade-off is this — you're giving up the ability to choose your finishes and get a builder warranty, and gaining a suburb where the character and school patterns are well-established and predictable. For many relocating families, that's exactly what they're looking for.
