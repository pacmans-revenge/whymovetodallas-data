Getting Your Bearings

Where Is Northlake, Texas?

Northlake sits in Denton County at the intersection of I-35W and TX-170/Hwy 114, roughly 20 miles north of downtown Fort Worth and 18 miles from DFW International Airport. That airport proximity is unusually good for a suburb this far north — most families relocating from out of state find that running to DFW is far less painful than they expected. The city borders Roanoke and Trophy Club to the south and east, Argyle to the north, and the unincorporated Denton County land holding most of its master-planned communities to the west.

Northlake's growth has been explosive. The incorporated town had roughly 5,500 residents at the 2020 census; today the broader community including Pecan Square, Harvest, Canyon Falls, and the Highlands areas has grown to an estimated 30,000 people. The commercial node along SH 114 and I-35W is developing, but right now most of the retail action happens at Alliance Town Center just 10–15 minutes south — a full-scale shopping, dining, and entertainment district that effectively serves as Northlake's downtown while the town builds its own.

Transit reality: Northlake has no public transit — no DCTA bus routes, no rail, no connection to the DART system. A personal vehicle is not optional; it's your lifeline. Budget for two cars if both adults work outside the home. The tradeoff is genuinely good highway access: I-35W and TX-170/Hwy 114 give you a direct shot to Fort Worth, DFW Airport, and the Las Colinas corridor without fighting downtown Dallas traffic every day.
Education

Northwest ISD — Schools Deep Dive

Most of Northlake is zoned to Northwest Independent School District, which earned a B from the Texas Education Agency (81 out of 100) for the 2024–25 school year and holds an A- overall rating from Niche. Byron Nelson High School, which serves many Northlake communities, earned the AP Silver Award in the 2024 AP School Honor Roll. There is one critical exception: portions of Canyon Falls feed to Argyle ISD, which Niche ranks as the top district in Denton County and gives an A+ rating. If Canyon Falls is on your shortlist, verifying your specific address's ISD is not optional — it's the first call to make.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Northlake

Built for Active Families

Northwest ISD offers competitive athletics across Byron Nelson and V.R. Eaton High Schools, plus a growing youth sports scene within Northlake's master-planned communities. Pecan Square and Harvest both have organized recreational programs, and travel leagues for soccer, baseball, and softball are active throughout the Alliance corridor. If your kids are athletes, this area has the infrastructure to support it.

Green Space Woven In

What sets Northlake apart from many DFW suburbs is how much open land still exists. Harvest's community lake and 9+ miles of trails, Pecan Square's interconnected trail network, and Canyon Falls' nature areas give families a genuine sense of space rather than the wall-to-wall development that defines older suburbs. It feels like a step away from the city — intentionally.

Alliance Town Center 10 Minutes Away

Northlake itself is light on dining and retail — that's honest. But Alliance Town Center, just 10 minutes south, covers almost everything: Target, Costco, Whole Foods, plus a strong restaurant row. Weekly grocery runs, date nights, and school supply shopping all happen there. It's close enough that the lack of local walkable retail doesn't feel like a daily inconvenience for most families.

Community Events & Neighborhood Culture

Pecan Square's Food Truck Fridays and seasonal festivals, Harvest's annual harvest celebration, and Canyon Falls' regular community events give neighborhoods a real social calendar. These aren't just marketing — they're genuinely attended by residents and have become anchor traditions for the communities. For families moving from out of state, these events are often how people make their first friends.

Resort Amenities You Actually Use

This is what distinguishes master-planned communities from standard suburban subdivisions: the amenities are real and they get used. Pecan Square's resort-style pools and splash pad are packed on summer weekends. Harvest has a lazy river. Canyon Falls has a resort pool and fitness center. For families coming from California or the Northeast where community pools are rare, this is a meaningful lifestyle upgrade — and it's baked into the neighborhood, not a separate membership.

Everyone Is from Somewhere Else

Northlake is one of DFW's newest communities, which means almost everyone moved here within the last few years. That makes it unusually easy to connect — there aren't established social circles that take years to break into. Neighborhood Facebook groups are active, newcomers are welcomed, and the community skews young family. If you're worried about moving somewhere and not knowing anyone, Northlake is one of the more forgiving places in DFW to land.

Honest tradeoff: Northlake has no walkable downtown, no nightlife scene, and limited local dining beyond the big box corridor at Alliance. If proximity to urban amenities, a restaurant district, or live music venues matters to your lifestyle, you'll be driving 30–45 minutes to find them. Families who value new construction, open space, and a tight-knit neighborhood feel tend to love it — families who want a walkable urban village life typically don't.
Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & City Amenities

Northlake is a car-dependent suburb — that's the honest starting point. There is no public transit, no walkable commercial district, and most errands require a vehicle. What Northlake compensates with is trail-connected master-planned communities, genuine open land feel, and proximity to the outdoor amenities of Lake Grapevine and the Denton County park system within 20–25 minutes.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Northlake's park and trail infrastructure is one of its most underrated features — and it largely exists within the master-planned communities rather than as standalone city parks. Each major neighborhood contributes its own trail network, and together they add up to 20+ miles of walking and biking paths. The town's significant undeveloped land also means green space isn't being paved over quickly.

Northlake Town Park

The municipal park at the heart of town features playgrounds, a disc golf course, open lawn for events, and picnic pavilions. Modest in size but well-maintained, it anchors the town's growing parks program. The Northlake Parks & Recreation department is adding programming as the population grows.

Pecan Square Trails & Amenity Campus

Pecan Square's internal trail network connects the community's parks, pocket parks, and amenity center — covering several miles of paved paths suitable for walking, jogging, and family bike rides. The amenity center sits at the center with resort pools, a splash pad, and outdoor event lawn.

Harvest Lake & Trail Network

Harvest centers around a community lake with fishing access and 9+ miles of interconnected trails. The trails wind through natural prairie buffers between neighborhood sections, giving residents the feel of being in open countryside rather than a dense subdivision. Dog-friendly, well-lit, and heavily used by families.

Canyon Falls Nature Trails

Canyon Falls' trail system connects neighborhoods to a natural preserve area along the terrain's edge — one of the more scenic recreational corridors in Northlake. The community's open space easements protect significant natural land from development, which is a meaningful differentiator as North DFW builds out.

Northlake Disc Golf Course

An 18-hole disc golf course integrated into the town park grounds — free to play, beginner-friendly, and a popular weekend destination for older kids and adults. It's a simple amenity, but one that's consistently cited by residents as a go-to low-key outdoor activity.

Lake Grapevine (~20 min)

For serious outdoor recreation — boating, kayaking, hiking the Knob Hills Trail, camping — Lake Grapevine is roughly 20 minutes southeast. At 7,380 acres with four marinas and 26 miles of shoreline, it's the area's major outdoor anchor. Many Northlake families make it a regular weekend destination.

Crime & Safety

How Safe Is Northlake?

Northlake is consistently one of the safest communities in DFW. With a violent crime rate of 0.82 per 1,000 residents — versus the U.S. average of 4.0 — and a property crime rate well below the Texas average, it earns an A+ overall grade from CrimeGrade.org. The combination of a small-town character, newer master-planned communities with active neighborhood watch networks, and a town police force focused on a contained geography keeps crime rates exceptionally low. This is one of the reasons families with young children specifically seek out Northlake when researching North DFW options.

Honest Assessment

Pros & Cons of Living in Northlake

I won't sell you on Northlake. I'll give you the real picture so you can decide if it's right for your family.

✓ What Northlake Does Well

  • Master-planned community quality — Pecan Square, Harvest, Canyon Falls, and Highlands are among the best-executed master-planned communities in DFW. Resort amenities, landscaping, and community design are genuinely impressive — not just marketing photos.
  • Top-tier safety — A+ CrimeGrade.org, 0.82 violent crimes per 1,000 residents. One of the safest communities in Denton County. Residents consistently cite this as a primary reason they chose Northlake.
  • AllianceTexas 10 minutes away — If your job is in the Alliance corridor (American Airlines, FedEx, Amazon, BNSF, Lockheed Martin), your commute is exceptional. Few suburbs this nice sit this close to 73,000+ jobs.
  • New construction pricing — You can still buy a brand-new 4-bedroom home in Northlake for $450K–$650K, which is meaningfully less than comparable new construction in Frisco or Southlake. The window on that pricing is narrowing as the area matures.
  • ISD optionality — Canyon Falls addresses may fall in Argyle ISD (Niche A+, #1 in Denton County), giving education-focused buyers access to one of North Texas's strongest school districts at Northlake price points.
  • Open land feel — Significant undeveloped land within and around Northlake gives it a spaciousness that's rare in high-growth North DFW suburbs. Trail systems feel more like the country than the suburbs.
  • No state income tax — Meaningful savings, especially for high earners relocating from California, New York, or Illinois.

— What to Weigh Carefully

  • MUD/PID taxes are real and significant — All four major new construction communities have Municipal Utility District or Public Improvement District taxes that can add $0.50–$1.00+ per $100 to your effective rate. That's $2,800–$5,500/year additional on a $557K home. This is not a small line item — always verify the specific rate for the address before closing.
  • Split ISD — always verify — Canyon Falls straddles the Northwest ISD / Argyle ISD boundary. You cannot assume which district your specific address is in. I've seen buyers close and discover they're in the wrong district for their school plans. Verify at the ISD level by address before making an offer.
  • Long Dallas commute — Downtown Dallas is 45–55 minutes; Plano/Legacy West is 55–65 minutes. If both spouses commute in different directions — one to Alliance, one to Plano — the math gets harder. Map your specific route during rush hour before committing.
  • Limited local retail and dining — Northlake has almost no walkable commercial district. Alliance Town Center handles most needs 10 minutes away, but daily errands always involve a drive. This isn't unusual for a growing suburb, but it's an adjustment for families used to urban walkability.
  • Car-dependent — completely — No public transit exists in Northlake. Two vehicles are not optional for a two-adult household. Budget this into your total cost of living picture.
  • Growth-related disruption — Northlake is one of the fastest-growing communities in Texas. That means active construction in and around every major neighborhood, traffic on FM 407 that will only increase, and school rezoning risk as Northwest ISD builds capacity to accommodate growth.

On a $557,000 home, that's roughly $8,300–$9,400 per year after the standard homestead exemption on the school portion.

The critical caveat: all four major new construction communities in Northlake — Pecan Square, Harvest, Canyon Falls, and Highlands — have Municipal Utility District (MUD) or Public Improvement District (PID) assessments that can add $0.50–$1.00+ per $100 on top of the base rate. That pushes the effective rate to $2.20–$2.70 per $100 in some phases, adding $2,800–$5,500 per year. Always verify the exact MUD/PID rate for a specific address at Denton CAD before closing. Source: Texas Comptroller, 2024 certified rates.

The price difference is stark. A comparable home — 4 bedrooms, master-planned community, good schools — costs $450K–$700K in Northlake versus $1.5M–$3M in the SF Bay Area or Westchester County. Even factoring in Northlake's property taxes (including MUD assessments), most California and New York families save $20,000–$40,000 per year between housing costs and the complete elimination of state income tax.

Beyond cost, Northlake offers something California families in particular find striking: brand-new homes, open land, uncrowded roads (for now), and resort amenities that cost seven figures in most coastal markets. The trade is a car-dependent lifestyle and a longer commute to Dallas proper — but for families working in the AllianceTexas corridor, neither concern applies. I talk through this math with every out-of-state family I work with.

Pecan Square is the most established master-planned community, with resort amenities, active events programming, and homes from $380K–$1M+. Harvest is designed around outdoor living — a community lake, 9+ miles of trails, and the popular annual harvest festival. Canyon Falls straddles the Northlake/Argyle ISD school boundary: some addresses feed Argyle ISD (Niche A+, #1 in Denton County), which is a significant advantage for education-focused families; homes range from $400K–$1M+. Highlands at Northlake is the most upscale community, with larger lots and custom-build options from $550K to $1M+. As a split-ISD suburb, I always verify school district assignment by specific address before any offer.

It depends on your timeline and how confident you are in the area. Rental supply in Northlake is limited — it's almost entirely an ownership market, and available rentals get picked up quickly. If you're confident in the AllianceTexas or Fort Worth corridor for work and you've researched schools and neighborhoods thoroughly, buying makes sense from day one. If you're uncertain about the commute or which school district zone you'd be in, renting briefly in nearby Roanoke or Trophy Club (more rental inventory) gives you time to verify addresses before committing. I help families map this out based on their specific situation.

Northlake is one of the safest communities in DFW. It has a violent crime rate of approximately 0.82 per 1,000 residents — well below the U.S. average of 4.0 — and a property crime rate of roughly 5.16 per 1,000, compared to the Texas average of 22.4. CrimeGrade.org assigns Northlake an A+ overall crime grade. As a newer, smaller town with tight-knit master-planned communities and active neighborhood watch networks, crime rates here are consistently among the lowest in Denton County. Safety is one of the most frequently cited reasons families specifically choose Northlake over comparable suburbs.

No. Northlake has no meaningful public transit service. The nearest DCTA A-train station is in Denton, approximately 25–30 minutes north, and the regional TRE and DART rail systems are far to the south and east. Northlake is a fully car-dependent suburb — plan for two vehicles for a two-adult household. This is a genuine tradeoff to weigh, particularly if you're relocating from a city with strong transit infrastructure. The upside is that for most residents working in the AllianceTexas corridor, the car-dependent lifestyle is offset by genuinely short commutes.

Northlake itself is home to MP Materials (rare earth magnet manufacturing, ~1,500 jobs, $1.25 billion investment), Southwire (electrical wire and cable, ~250 employees), and Henry Schein (healthcare distribution, ~300 jobs). The bigger story is AllianceTexas — the 17,000+ acre mixed-use development 10 minutes south in Fort Worth — which hosts 602 companies and 73,000+ jobs, including American Airlines MRO, BNSF Railway, FedEx Express, Amazon, Lockheed Martin, and Raytheon. For families relocating for a role at any of these employers, Northlake is one of the closest residential options with quality schools and family infrastructure.

For the AllianceTexas corridor and Fort Worth, traffic is manageable — FM 407 (Justin Road) connects to I-35W, and the drive to Alliance is 10–18 minutes outside of peak rush hour. The harder reality is commuting east: Downtown Dallas is 45–55 minutes and Plano/Legacy West is 55–65 minutes, with I-35W and Highway 114 carrying significant load during morning and evening peaks. DFW Airport is 20–28 minutes via Highway 114. Families with both spouses commuting in different directions — one to Alliance, one to Plano or Dallas — should map both routes during actual rush hour before committing to Northlake. It's the single most common thing I have clients do before they decide.
