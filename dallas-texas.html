Location & Commute

Where Is Dallas? Getting Around the Urban Core

Dallas is the urban center from which the entire DFW metro radiates outward. Unlike Frisco, Allen, or Flower Mound — which are suburbs of Dallas — the city proper spans roughly 385 square miles and sits at the intersection of the metro's major highway corridors. I-35E runs north-south through the heart of the city toward Fort Worth and south toward Waco. I-75 (Central Expressway) heads northeast toward Plano, Allen, and McKinney. The I-635 LBJ Freeway forms the outer loop, and the Dallas North Tollway (DNT) runs north from downtown toward Addison, Plano, and Frisco.

What separates Dallas from every suburb in DFW: DART light rail. The Dallas Area Rapid Transit system runs 93 miles of track with 64 stations, connecting 13 North Texas cities. If you live in Uptown, Lakewood, or near a DART station, you genuinely do not need a car for your downtown commute. That is simply not true of any DFW suburb.

Rush hour reality: I-35, I-75 (Central Expressway), and the downtown connector loops are legitimately slow during peak hours — 7–9am and 4:30–7pm. If your office is downtown Dallas or accessible by DART, this doesn't affect you much. If your daily commute goes outbound on I-35E toward Arlington, plan on adding 20–35 minutes.
Schools & Education

Dallas ISD: Mixed Overall, Elite at the Top

Dallas Independent School District serves the city proper and is one of the largest districts in Texas with over 140,000 students. The district's overall Niche grade of B reflects a wide range — from nationally ranked magnet schools to underperforming neighborhood campuses. For families relocating to Dallas, the critical distinction is this: school quality here is determined by which campus you attend, not which city you live in. The best schools in DISD require research, sometimes an application, and in some cases a lottery. The reward is access to programs that no DFW suburb can match.

Cost of Living

What Dallas Costs — vs. Where You're Coming From

For families relocating from California, New York, or Illinois, Dallas proper offers a significant cost reduction even compared to DFW suburbs — with the added advantage of zero Texas state income tax. The median SFR price of ~$492K is lower than Frisco ($692K), Southlake ($800K+), or Allen ($540K).

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Dallas

Pro Sports Capital of DFW

Dallas is the sports hub: Cowboys at AT&T Stadium (Arlington, 20 min), Mavericks and Stars at American Airlines Center (downtown), FC Dallas at Toyota Stadium (Frisco, 35 min), Rangers at Globe Life Field (Arlington, 20 min), Wings (WNBA), and the Dallas Rattlers (lacrosse). Your kid will grow up with live professional sports accessible at least once a month.

Family Events All Year

The State Fair of Texas at Fair Park (October — the largest state fair in the US) is a Dallas institution. The Dallas Arboretum runs family programming nearly every weekend from spring through fall. Klyde Warren Park hosts free outdoor concerts, fitness classes, and children's events regularly. The Dallas Zoo, Perot Museum of Nature and Science, and the Children's Aquarium are all close.

Dallas Arts District

The largest contiguous urban arts district in the United States — 68 acres in downtown Dallas anchored by the AT&T Performing Arts Center, Dallas Museum of Art, Nasher Sculpture Center, Crow Museum of Asian Art, and the Morton H. Meyerson Symphony Center. Family memberships to these institutions are practical investments here, not occasional splurges.

Dining Scene Unmatched in DFW

Over 2,100 restaurants mapped within city limits — by far the deepest food scene in the metro. Bishop Arts District for independent dining. Knox/Henderson for upscale casual. Uptown for rooftop bars and patio culture. Deep Ellum for late-night and live music. Dallas has James Beard-nominated chefs, Michelin-caliber tasting menus, and authentic ethnic neighborhoods representing cuisines you simply won't find in Frisco.

Genuine Urban Diversity

Dallas earns Niche's A+ for diversity — the highest grade in DFW. The city has deep cultural communities: deep-rooted Hispanic neighborhoods in Oak Cliff and East Dallas, Vietnamese communities along Greenville, Korean and Indian communities in North Dallas, and one of the largest Ethiopian/Eritrean populations in Texas. For families relocating from cities like Los Angeles, Chicago, or New York, Dallas feels culturally familiar in a way no suburb does.

Neighborhood Identity

Dallas is a city of distinct neighborhoods — from the bungalows of M-Streets to the estates of Preston Hollow to the condos of Uptown. Each has its own block associations, social culture, farmers markets, and local businesses. You're not moving to a master-planned community; you're joining a neighborhood with a 40-year identity. For families who want to belong to a place rather than just a zip code, that matters.

The honest tradeoff: Dallas's urban energy is genuine — but it comes with urban realities. Rush-hour traffic on I-35 and Central Expressway can be legitimately frustrating. Crime data citywide is higher than any DFW suburb, and some neighborhoods require real homework. The school district requires active engagement to find the right fit rather than relying on your address alone. Families who want everything handled by geography — great neighborhood school, low crime, quiet streets — will likely be happier in Frisco or Flower Mound. Dallas rewards preparation and intentionality.
Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & City Amenities

Dallas is the only DFW city that earns its walkability score genuinely. The Katy Trail, Klyde Warren Park, White Rock Lake, Deep Ellum, and the Uptown corridor deliver car-free experiences that simply don't exist in the suburbs. DART light rail connects to 12 neighboring cities. That said, Dallas covers 385 square miles — walkability is real in some neighborhoods and non-existent in others. Where you live within the city determines your walkability experience as much as the city's overall score does.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Dallas maintains over 600 parks and green spaces citywide — more absolute park count than any DFW suburb, though per-capita access varies by neighborhood.

White Rock Lake Park

1,015 acres of lakeside parkland in East Dallas. 9.3-mile perimeter trail for running, cycling, and skating. Dallas Arboretum on the eastern shore. Boating, fishing, dog-friendly trails. The closest thing to a true nature escape inside city limits.

Klyde Warren Park

5.2-acre park built over the Woodall Rodgers Freeway in downtown Dallas. Free fitness classes, concerts, food trucks, children's park, and weekend events. Steps from the Dallas Arts District. One of the best urban public spaces in Texas.

Katy Trail

3.5-mile urban rail-trail connecting Uptown to Highland Park. One of the most-used urban trails in Texas. Paved, well-lit, popular for morning runs, cycling commutes, and evening walks. Connects directly to McKinney Avenue's restaurants and bars.

Flag Pole Hill Park

105 acres in Lake Highlands with a large trail network, amphitheater for summer concerts, natural grass hills, and one of the few actual "sledding hills" in Dallas after rare winter weather events. Family-sized weekend programming.

Reverchon Park

45 acres in Oak Lawn near Uptown. Baseball and softball diamonds, tennis courts, a community center, and a large open field. One of Dallas's oldest parks — a neighborhood anchor for the Oak Lawn and Cedar Springs communities.

Bachman Lake Park

3-mile loop trail around Bachman Lake in northwest Dallas. Fishing pier, playground, picnic areas, and a newly renovated recreation center adjacent. Popular with families in the Love Field and Northwest Highway corridors.

Safety

Crime in Dallas — Citywide Numbers & What They Mean

Dallas has higher crime than any DFW suburb — this is simply true and worth stating plainly. The citywide averages reflect a city of 1.3 million people with significant variation by neighborhood. Preston Hollow, Lakewood, Lake Highlands, and the M-Streets neighborhoods have crime rates far below the citywide average — comparable to some DFW suburbs. East Oak Cliff and some parts of Southeast Dallas have higher rates. Always research the specific neighborhood and street before buying, not just the city-level statistic.

FAQ

Questions Families Ask About Moving to Dallas

Dallas can be an excellent place to raise a family — with the right preparation. The city offers world-class magnet schools, a rich cultural and arts scene, incredible dining diversity, and proximity to downtown employers. It also requires more active decision-making than a master-planned suburb: school selection involves research and sometimes applications, neighborhood-level crime data varies significantly, and traffic during rush hours is real. Families who do their homework on neighborhoods and schools consistently love living in Dallas. Families expecting the same ease-of-entry as Frisco or Allen sometimes feel the city requires more management. Knowing which kind of family you are before you decide is the most important step.

The most consistently family-friendly neighborhoods are Lake Highlands (established homes, good DISD feeder schools, White Rock Lake access), Lakewood (craftsman bungalows, Lakewood Elementary, White Rock Lake trail), Preston Hollow (estate-style homes, top private schools nearby, quiet streets), and the M-Streets / Greenland Hills area (historic Craftsman homes, central location, strong block-association culture). Uptown and Oak Lawn attract younger families who prioritize walkability and urban access. Oak Cliff and Bishop Arts offer affordability and cultural richness at a lower price point. The best neighborhood for your family depends on your school strategy, budget, and lifestyle priorities — and I'm happy to walk you through that comparison.

Booker T. Washington High School for the Performing and Visual Arts is nationally recognized for arts education. Thomas Jefferson Science/Engineering Magnet is a strong STEM pathway. The challenge: these campuses require competitive applications. Families who secure a spot in a magnet school get access to exceptional education. The toll road network (DNT, Hwy 114, SH 183) provides faster alternatives at a cost. Budget for tolls if your commute uses the DNT regularly.

For gifted and high-academic tracks: Sudie L. Williams TAG Elementary (selective magnet, grades 2–5) is one of the best in the nation but requires testing and application. For neighborhood schools: Lakewood Elementary (9/10 GreatSchools) is one of the most coveted in Dallas, located in the Lakewood neighborhood near White Rock Lake. Uplift Luna Preparatory (charter network, PK–12) has strong academic results and serves the Oak Cliff/Bishop Arts area. Highland Park ISD borders Dallas to the north but is a completely separate school district — technically accessible only to residents of Highland Park and University Park. Charter options through Uplift Education, KIPP, and Momentous Schools provide additional strong alternatives throughout the city.

Dallas is in a different category entirely — a Walk Score of 93 (Walker's Paradise) compared to 31–48 for most DFW suburbs. Neighborhoods like Uptown, Oak Lawn, Lakewood, Greenland Hills, and Knox/Henderson genuinely allow residents to walk to restaurants, coffee shops, groceries, and parks without a car. DART light rail connects Dallas to 13 surrounding cities with 64 stations and 93 miles of track — the largest light rail system in the US. If walkability and urban mobility matter to your family, Dallas is the only DFW city that can deliver them. This is a meaningful quality-of-life difference, especially for families relocating from cities like Chicago, New York, or San Francisco.

Dallas is home to several major corporate headquarters: AT&T (global HQ), Southwest Airlines (Love Field HQ), JPMorgan Chase Southwest regional HQ, Comerica Bank, Match Group (Tinder, Hinge, Match), Neiman Marcus, and UT Southwestern Medical Center — one of the largest and most prestigious academic medical systems in the US. Within 20–30 minutes: American Airlines (near DFW Airport), Toyota North America (Plano), Charles Schwab (Westlake), State Farm (Richardson), and Liberty Mutual (Plano). The entire DFW metroplex is consistently ranked in the top 3–5 metro areas in the US for corporate headquarters relocations, with Texas's business environment driving continued growth.
