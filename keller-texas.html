Location & Commute

Where Is Keller? Commutes & Getting Around

Keller sits in the northwestern corner of Tarrant County, roughly 15 miles northwest of Downtown Fort Worth and about 32 miles west of Downtown Dallas. State Highway 114 cuts east-west through the city, connecting Keller directly to DFW International Airport and the Las Colinas/Irving corridor in under 25 minutes. US-377 (Keller Parkway) runs north-south through the heart of town, linking neighborhoods to Southlake to the east and Roanoke to the north.

Keller's position makes it one of DFW's best-kept secrets for Fort Worth-side commuters and frequent flyers alike. The drive to DFW Airport is genuinely 22 minutes on SH-114 — no toll roads required. American Airlines employees, Lockheed Martin engineers, and the rapidly growing Westlake/Southlake corporate corridor are all within a 15–25 minute drive. Families commuting to Dallas will face a longer haul (35–45 min in traffic), so it's an honest trade-off to consider.

A personal vehicle is required for all daily errands. That said, SH-114 and Rufe Snow Drive are well-maintained corridors, and Keller avoids the worst of DFW's gridlock because most residents commute west (toward Fort Worth and Alliance) rather than against the east-bound rush.
Education

Keller ISD — Schools in Keller, Texas

Keller Independent School District serves approximately 32,000 students across Keller, Roanoke, and portions of North Fort Worth. The district has earned a national reputation for strong academics, championship athletics, and fine arts programs — and Keller High School holds a National Blue Ribbon Award from the U.S. Department of Education. Over 78% of rated campuses scored a B or higher on TEA's 2024-25 accountability ratings.

Where to Live

Best Neighborhoods in Keller for Families

Keller's neighborhoods range from established 1990s communities with mature trees and large lots to newer developments near the SH-170 corridor. Most are single-family, owner-occupied, and HOA-governed — which keeps the community well-maintained and cohesive. The city doesn't have a dense urban core, but the trail system and Bear Creek park network tie neighborhoods together in a way that feels genuinely connected.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Keller

Keller ISD Athletics — A Community Identity

Keller ISD fields some of Tarrant County's most competitive high school programs. Friday night football at Keller Memorial Stadium is a genuine community event. Youth sports through Keller Youth Association (KYA) offers soccer, baseball, softball, and basketball — starting as young as 3 years old.

Bear Creek & Trail Networks

Bear Creek Park is Keller's flagship outdoor destination — splash pad, pavilions, sports fields, walking trails, and a fishing pond. The city maintains 30+ parks connected by paved trails. Lake Grapevine is a 10-minute drive for boating, kayaking, and shoreline trails when you want a bigger outdoor escape.

Old Town Keller & Nearby Options

Old Town Keller's historic district offers local restaurants and shops with small-town character. Families drive 10–15 minutes to Alliance Town Center (Costco, Dick's, restaurants) to the north or Southlake Town Square (upscale dining, retail) to the south — getting the best of both worlds without paying either city's prices.

Community Events & Local Traditions

The Keller Farmers Market runs seasonally at Bear Creek Park. The city hosts a Fourth of July parade and fireworks, Festival of Lights in December, Movies in the Park during summer, and regular concerts at the Old Town Amphitheater. These aren't manufactured events — they've been running for years and draw actual residents.

Gateway to the Westlake Corridor

One of Keller's quietly compelling advantages: you're 10–12 minutes from Westlake, where Charles Schwab and Fidelity have major campuses. That proximity means a real concentration of high-income, professionally-educated neighbors — the kind of economic adjacency that tends to support strong schools, active PTAs, and well-funded community amenities.

Easy to Connect

Keller draws a mix of long-term DFW families and relocators from California, Illinois, and the Northeast — especially corporate transferees bound for Schwab, Fidelity, and the Fort Worth corridor. HOA communities are active, KISD booster clubs are well-organized, and the steady influx of relocating families means you're rarely the only one starting fresh.

The honest tradeoff: Keller does not have a walkable town center in the traditional sense. Old Town Keller is charming but small — it's not Southlake Town Square. There's no rail transit of any kind, nightlife is minimal, and the dining scene is solid but not remarkable on its own. If you want a vibrant restaurant row walking distance from home, Keller isn't it. What it offers instead is a well-run, family-oriented city where the tradeoffs are predictable and the infrastructure (schools, parks, safety) is genuinely strong.
Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & City Amenities

Keller is car-dependent — there is no DART, no DCTA rail, and no meaningful bus service within the city. A personal vehicle is not optional for daily life. What Keller compensates with is a well-developed parks system, Keller Pointe (one of the best city recreation facilities in Tarrant County), and proximity to Lake Grapevine for families who want more than a neighborhood trail.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Keller maintains over 30 parks encompassing hundreds of acres of green space, with a connected trail network running through established neighborhoods. The parks range from pocket parks tucked into subdivisions to the flagship Bear Creek Park complex. Lake Grapevine, just 10 minutes east, extends the outdoor options significantly for boating, fishing, and hiking families.

Bear Creek Park

Keller's largest and most popular park — multi-field sports complex, beloved splash pad, walking trails, fishing pond, pavilions, and a covered amphitheater. The Farmers Market runs here seasonally. This is the park families use most.

City Point Park

A newer addition at Keller Town Center — open lawn, walking paths, and event space in the heart of the city's town center district. Dog-friendly and frequently used for community gatherings and weekend events.

Hidden Lakes Trail Corridor

The Hidden Lakes community's internal trail network connects to the broader Keller trail system. Paved multi-use paths wind through the neighborhood past the two namesake lakes — a popular route for morning runs and family evening walks.

Lake Grapevine (10 min)

A 7,280-acre Corps of Engineers lake with public boat ramps, fishing access, Murrell Park, and North Shore Trail. The trail system is mountain-bike and hiker-friendly. Families boat here on weekends — it's Keller's best outdoor escape that doesn't require a long drive.

Comanche Springs & Whitestone Park

Two well-used neighborhood parks in central and north Keller offering playgrounds, pavilions, and open green space. These are the walkable "everyday parks" that subdivisions in their vicinity use for afternoon play and weekend gatherings.

Keller Cross Timbers Preserve

A natural area in north Keller preserving Post Oak Cross Timbers woodland — a native ecosystem increasingly rare in developed DFW. Maintained trail loop through undisturbed woodland. One of the few places in Tarrant County where the land still looks like pre-development Texas.

Safety & Crime

Is Keller Safe?

Keller consistently ranks as one of the safer mid-sized cities in Tarrant County. The violent crime rate is well below the national average of approximately 4.0 per 1,000 residents, and property crime is below both state and national benchmarks. Niche assigns Keller an A+ for safety. The Keller Police Department operates dedicated neighborhood patrol zones with community policing as a stated priority.

Violent Crime Rate

~0.77 per 1,000 residents — approximately 5x below the U.S. national average of ~4.0/1,000. Keller's violent crime rate is among the lowest in Tarrant County for a city its size.

Property Crime

Property crime in Keller is below the Texas state average and well below the national average. Most incidents are opportunistic (vehicle break-ins in unlocked cars). NeighborhoodScout places Keller in the top 9% of safest U.S. cities.

Keller Police Department

The Keller PD maintains a community policing model with dedicated patrol zones by neighborhood. Niche rates Keller A+ for safety. Regular community engagement events, active citizens' patrol program, and a consistently staffed department relative to city size.

Honest Assessment

Living in Keller — Pros & Cons

✓ What Families Love

  • Keller ISD quality — top-rated Tarrant County district; TEA B (85/100), Niche A, strong UIL athletics, and dedicated feeder patterns from elementary through high school
  • Safety — violent crime rate ~0.77/1K (roughly 5x below the national average); Niche A+ for safety; consistently one of Tarrant County's lowest-crime cities
  • No state income tax — families moving from California, Illinois, or New York typically save $8,000–$20,000+ annually at comparable income levels
  • Three employment corridors — Fort Worth major employers 15–25 min away, Alliance logistics hub 8–10 min north, Charles Schwab and Fidelity 10–12 min southeast in Westlake
  • Established neighborhoods — mature trees, developed landscaping, and neighborhood character that newer growth-corridor suburbs lack
  • Keller Pointe — one of the strongest city-owned recreation facilities in Tarrant County: aquatic center, fitness, courts, climbing wall
  • DFW Airport access — 15–20 minutes to DFW International, a practical advantage for frequent travelers that most North DFW suburbs can't match

— What to Know

  • Fully car-dependent — no transit of any kind; a vehicle is required for every errand, school run, and commute without exception
  • Property taxes — the ~$1.73/$100 combined rate is typical for North DFW, but on a $674K home it runs ~$11,600/year before the homestead exemption; first-time Texas buyers are often surprised
  • Peak-hour traffic on SH-114 — the primary east-west artery toward DFW can add 15–20 minutes during morning commute windows
  • Limited condo/townhome inventory — Keller is overwhelmingly single-family; buyers who want a low-maintenance attached home will find very few options within city limits
  • Dining scene requires driving — Old Town Keller is charming but small; most residents drive to Southlake Town Square or Alliance Town Center for real dining variety
