Location & Commute

Where Is Keller? Commutes & Getting Around

Keller occupies the northwestern edge of Tarrant County, bordered by Southlake to the south, Colleyville to the southeast, and North Richland Hills to the east. It anchors the I-35W corridor heading toward AllianceTexas — one of DFW's most active industrial and corporate employment zones. TX-114 cuts through the southern portion, connecting residents east to DFW Airport and Las Colinas in under 25 minutes, and west toward Roanoke and Decatur.

For families with a spouse commuting to Fort Worth or the Alliance area, Keller is a natural fit — proximity is outstanding. The honest tradeoff: eastern corridors like Plano, Legacy West, and Frisco run 45–55 minutes or more in traffic. If you're split-commuting — one partner heading west, one heading east — map both routes before committing to a neighborhood. I do that exercise with clients every week.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Keller

Youth Sports Culture

Keller is deeply athletic. Keller Sports Park is a 95-acre complex with 12 soccer fields, multiple baseball and softball diamonds, and a football field. KYSA (Keller Youth Soccer Association) is one of the largest youth soccer programs in Tarrant County. Weekend schedules fill fast — this is a town where kids play.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Bear Creek Park anchors the trail network with connecting paths through established neighborhoods. Hidden Lakes Community Park has a splash pad that draws families all summer. The city's 40+ parks mean most neighborhoods have green space within a 5-minute bike ride — unusually good coverage for this part of DFW.

Old Town & Town Center

Old Town Keller along Main Street has the feel of a small-town downtown — local restaurants, a farmers market, boutique shops, and community events on the green. For bigger retail, Southlake Town Square is 10 minutes east. Families rarely need to drive far for either date nights or school-supply runs.

Events & Community Traditions

The Keller Farmers Market runs on Saturdays through the growing season in Old Town. Keller's annual Fall Festival and 4th of July celebrations draw thousands — these aren't perfunctory events, they're real community touchstones. The small-town-in-the-city feel is something residents genuinely reference when asked why they stayed.

Military Community Presence

NAS Fort Worth Joint Reserve Base (Carswell) is 25 minutes south, and Keller has a meaningful veteran and active-duty military population. Schools are experienced with mid-year enrollment. The community understands PCS timelines, and VA loan transactions are routine here — a real difference for military families on tight timelines.

Easy to Connect

Keller's established neighborhood culture — with active HOAs, involved PTAs, and sports league social calendars — makes it easier than average for relocating families to build community quickly. People tend to stay once they arrive. That stability means neighbors know each other, which relocators consistently say was one of the biggest surprises — in a good way.

The honest tradeoff: Keller has almost no walkable commercial density — every errand requires a car. The eastern commercial corridor along 377 has older strip retail that feels dated compared to the master-planned town centers in Frisco or Prosper. And if your job is in Dallas proper (not Fort Worth or the mid-cities), you're looking at a genuine 45–55 minute commute in morning traffic. That's the deal families make in exchange for the school quality and price point.
Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & City Amenities

Keller is car-dependent — there is no DART rail, no DCTA bus service, and no meaningful public transit. A personal vehicle is non-negotiable for daily life. Where Keller compensates is in its parks system and recreation infrastructure: 40+ parks, a trail network connecting neighborhoods, and Keller Pointe, one of the best full-service recreation centers in Tarrant County.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Keller maintains 40+ parks covering hundreds of acres, with a trail network that connects major parks through established neighborhoods. The city's signature outdoor asset is Keller Sports Park — a dedicated athletic complex that goes beyond typical neighborhood parks — paired with Bear Creek Park's trail system for everyday walkers and cyclists.

Keller Sports Park

A 95-acre dedicated athletic complex with 12 soccer fields, multiple baseball and softball diamonds, a football field, and a concession complex. The hub for KYSA, youth baseball leagues, and ISD recreational programming. On weekend mornings in spring and fall, this place is packed — in the best way.

Bear Creek Park & Trail

One of Keller's most-used parks, with multi-use trails along Bear Creek that connect to surrounding neighborhoods. Picnic pavilions, playgrounds, and creek access make it the go-to for weekday afternoon walks and weekend picnics. Trail extensions link to the broader city trail network.

Hidden Lakes Community Park

A neighborhood favorite featuring a splash pad (open Memorial Day through Labor Day), playgrounds, covered pavilions, and open green space. The splash pad is genuinely popular — plan to arrive early on hot summer weekends if you want a parking spot.

Bicentennial Park

One of Keller's older parks, with open athletic fields, shaded picnic areas, a playground, and a small pond. A community staple for casual neighborhood use — less destination-worthy than Sports Park but reliably available when you just need open space 5 minutes from home.

Town Hall Hills Park

Adjacent to Old Town Keller, this park offers open green space, trails, and a community gathering area used for farmers market overflow and seasonal events. Good connector between Old Town activities and the trail network heading south and west through the city.

Bob Woodruff Park & Nearby Trails

Just across the Keller border in Euless, Bob Woodruff Park offers a 5-mile trail, disc golf, and lake access — a quick 10-minute drive that Keller families regularly use for longer hikes and outdoor adventures the city's own parks don't accommodate. Good to know it's there.

Property crime is similarly below average, and Niche consistently rates Keller's crime and safety as an "A" category. For families relocating from higher-crime metro areas, the difference is noticeable.

New Construction

New Homes in Keller

Keller is a fully established suburb — the kind of place where the neighborhoods are set, the trees are mature, and the community feel is already there when you move in. There's little vacant land left for large-scale new residential development, so most families buying here are focused on the resale market. That's not a disadvantage: Keller has strong inventory across a range of price points, lot sizes, and neighborhood styles, and resale homes often come with finished landscaping, custom touches, and HOA communities that are already thriving. If you're weighing Keller against a suburb with active new construction, the honest trade-off is this — you're giving up the ability to choose your finishes and get a builder warranty, and gaining a suburb where the character and school patterns are well-established and predictable. For many relocating families, that's exactly what they're looking for. Keller, Texas — FAQ for Relocating Families

Keller is consistently rated one of the best family suburbs in the DFW metro. Niche gives Keller an "A+" overall grade and an "A" for family-friendliness — driven by Keller ISD's strong academic performance, very low violent crime (roughly 0.8 per 1,000 residents vs. the national average of ~4.0), and a community culture built around youth sports, parks, and neighborhood involvement. Families who value school quality, safety, and an established suburban feel without paying Southlake prices find Keller genuinely difficult to beat in Tarrant County.

Keller ISD holds a TEA "A" rating (Distinction Designation in Student Achievement). Niche rates the district an overall "A" with an "A" for college preparation. Fossil Ridge High School and Keller High School are both consistently in the top tier of Tarrant County high schools by test scores and college enrollment. Elementary campuses like Bear Creek, Indian Springs, and Shady Grove Elementary hold GreatSchools ratings of 7–9/10. The district serves approximately 35,000 students and is known for strong athletics, fine arts, and dual-language programs at the elementary level.

The median single-family home sale price in Keller is approximately $674,000 as of early 2026 (NTREIS 12-month rolling average). Prices range from the low $400s for smaller resale homes to $1.5M+ for larger custom builds in premium neighborhoods. Families whose jobs are west of Dallas — Alliance, Fort Worth CBD, NAS JRB — are often pleasantly surprised by how workable the Keller commute actually is.
