Getting Your Bearings

Where Is Roanoke, Texas?

Roanoke sits in the northwestern corner of the DFW metroplex, roughly 25 miles from downtown Fort Worth and 35 miles from downtown Dallas. It straddles Denton and Tarrant counties — most of the city falls in Denton County — and is positioned directly along Highway 114, the corridor that connects DFW Airport to the east and the Alliance employment development to the north via Hwy 377. That geographic position is a big part of why so many families end up here: you're close to everything in the northwest quadrant without living in a high-density suburb. The city proper has about 10,000 residents, which means you actually know your neighbors and the Oak Street downtown feels like a neighborhood gathering place rather than a tourist corridor.

The Alliance Corridor is essentially your backyard. Within 8–10 miles, companies like Amazon, FedEx, Bell Textron, BNSF Railway, and American Airlines Maintenance have major operations. DFW Airport is a 22-minute straight shot east on Hwy 114 — a significant perk if anyone in your household travels for work or you're relocating from a city that requires frequent return trips. For families comparing Roanoke to Southlake or Westlake, the tradeoff is real: you'll have less luxury retail and fewer high-end restaurants within city limits, but you'll often spend 20–30% less on the house while being a 12-minute drive from Southlake Town Square.

That simplicity is underrated.
Education

Northwest ISD — Schools Deep Dive

Roanoke is served by Northwest Independent School District, one of the consistently strong ISDs in North Texas. The district earned a B (81/100) from TEA for 2024–25 — solid student achievement and graduation outcomes across a district that serves families from Roanoke through Trophy Club, Haslet, and parts of northern Fort Worth. Niche grades it A+ for public schools overall. The standout campus in the system — James M. Steele Early College High School, located right in Roanoke — consistently ranks among the top 30 high schools in Texas. If your high schooler is academically motivated, Steele is a legitimate path to earning college credit tuition-free before graduation. That's a real, tangible benefit that most families moving from out of state don't expect to find in a suburb this size.

Where to Live

Best Neighborhoods in Roanoke for Families

Roanoke's housing stock is a mix of established single-family neighborhoods built in the 1990s and 2000s, newer master-planned communities on the Trophy Club and Northlake borders, and a handful of communities specifically positioned for Alliance corridor workers. The city proper is compact — about 10,000 residents — so "neighborhood" in Roanoke often means knowing your elementary school feeder zone as much as the subdivision name. Here's where families tend to land and why.

Cost of Living — Roanoke vs. Where You're Coming From

The conversation I have with almost every out-of-state family starts the same way: sticker shock — not in the bad direction. A $593K median home in Roanoke buys a 2,400–3,200 sq ft house with a real yard, a two-car garage, and a school district that doesn't require a private-school backup plan. For families relocating from California, New York, or Illinois, the numbers can feel too good to be true. They're not — Texas just trades differently: you pay more in property taxes, and you pay zero in state income tax. Here's what that looks like side by side.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Roanoke

Roanoke surprises people. From the outside it looks like a small stop on I-35W between Fort Worth and Denton. Up close it's a tight-knit community with a genuinely walkable restaurant strip, a thriving youth sports culture, and the kind of neighbors-know-each-other feel that's hard to find this close to a major airport corridor.

Sports & Athletics

Northwest ISD athletics are a community anchor — Byron Nelson HS football nights draw the whole town out. Youth leagues are active year-round: Texas Extreme Baseball Academy trains travel ball players at the competitive level, and Northwest Youth Soccer runs recreational and competitive programs serving hundreds of Roanoke kids. The rec center hosts adult leagues too, so parents aren't just spectators.

Trails & Outdoor Access

Roanoke's trail network connects neighborhoods to parks and the city core — Cannon Parkway Linear Park is the spine, running east-west through town with a dedicated hike-and-bike path. Eagle Mountain Lake (15 minutes west) adds fishing, boating, and lakeside dining for weekend escapes. Families heading north reach Northlake and Ponder's rural open space quickly when they need a true break from suburbia.

Shopping & Dining

Oak Street is Roanoke's crown jewel — the city earned the designation "Unique Dining Capital of Texas" for good reason. You'll find locally-owned restaurants, craft food concepts, and a walkable street scene that feels nothing like the strip-mall sprawl of neighboring suburbs. For big-box retail, Keller (15 min south) and Fort Worth Premium Outlets (20 min south via I-35W) cover the rest.

Events & Community Culture

Roanoke punches above its weight for community events. The annual Roanoke Fall Festival shuts down Oak Street for a full weekend of food, live music, and local vendors. The Fourth of July parade is a true small-town tradition — floats, kids on bikes, and fire trucks — and the city's holiday lights on Oak Street draw visitors from across the region. These aren't manufactured events; they feel like they actually belong to the community.

Roanoke's Unique Angle

The "Unique Dining Capital of Texas" designation isn't marketing spin — it was awarded by the Texas Legislature. Oak Street is one of the few spots in the entire DFW metro where you can actually walk from restaurant to restaurant on a Friday night. For families who moved from cities and miss a real walkable neighborhood strip, this is genuinely meaningful — and it's baked into daily life, not a 30-minute drive away.

Community Feel

With just under 10,000 residents, Roanoke is small enough that people recognize each other at the grocery store and the school pickup line. Established neighborhoods like Country Acres and Fairway Ranch have the kind of street-level community where kids still ride bikes and neighbors wave. HOAs here tend to be functional rather than adversarial — maintaining the aesthetic without the drama that comes with higher-density newer suburbs.

Honest tradeoffs: Roanoke has no movie theater, no major mall, and limited late-night entertainment within city limits. Teenagers will tell you there's "nothing to do" — which means they're comparing it to a city, not a suburb. For families, the tradeoff is intentional: you get a quieter, more connected community at the cost of urban convenience. Everything else is 15–25 minutes away.

Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & City Amenities

Roanoke is a car-dependent suburb for most destinations — that's the honest starting point for any DFW suburb off the commuter rail grid. What makes Roanoke different is Oak Street: one of the only walkable commercial corridors in the Alliance/Northwest corridor. Outside the Oak Street core, neighborhoods are connected by a trail network that makes cycling practical within the city, even if it doesn't substitute for a car for commuting or errands. There is no DART rail, no DCTA commuter service — driving is the default, and the highway network (I-35W, Hwy 114, Hwy 170) is what makes the suburb function.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Roanoke has 24 parks and green spaces mapped within city limits, anchored by a linear trail corridor that runs through the heart of the city. Park access is one of Roanoke's real quality-of-life advantages — ranking #9 of 65 DFW suburbs for parks per resident (88th percentile). Most neighborhoods are within a short bike ride of at least one community park or trail access point.

Cannon Parkway Linear Park

The spine of Roanoke's trail system — a dedicated hike-and-bike path running east-west through the city, connecting neighborhoods to the downtown core and community park. Paved surface, well-lit, used for running, cycling, and dog walking. Ties into the larger North Tarrant/Denton County trail network at its endpoints.

Community Park

Roanoke's largest multi-use park, adjacent to the recreation center. Features baseball and softball fields, soccer fields, basketball courts, a playground, and a covered pavilion. This is where most of Roanoke's organized youth sports leagues play, and on weekends it's the busiest park in the city.

Fairplay Park

Neighborhood park in central Roanoke with a playground, open green space, and picnic area. Quiet and family-friendly — the kind of park you walk to on a weeknight, not drive to on a weekend. Popular with younger kids and families with strollers.

Parks at Roanoke / Linear Park

Connects key residential neighborhoods along the northern edge of the city to the trail network. Good access point for cyclists heading toward Alliance or Northlake. Shaded seating and open lawn area.

Briarwyck Park

Neighborhood park serving the Briarwyck subdivision — playground, open field, and trail connector. Well-maintained by the city with regular upgrades. One of the more popular neighborhood parks on the city's western edge.

Eagle Mountain Lake (nearby)

Not in Roanoke proper, but ~15 minutes west via Highway 199. Lake Travis of the Alliance area — boating, fishing, kayaking, and lakeside restaurants. Roanoke families treat it as their summer weekend destination. Fort Worth Nature Center & Refuge is nearby as well.

Pros & Cons of Living in Roanoke

I won't sell you on Roanoke. I'll give you the real picture so you can decide if it's right for your family.

✓ What Roanoke Does Well

  • Oak Street dining scene — #4/65 DFW suburbs for restaurants per resident (95th percentile). One of the only genuinely walkable dining strips in the Alliance/Northwest corridor. Real independently-owned restaurants, not just chains.
  • Northwest ISD quality at a lower price — A+ rated schools including Byron Nelson HS, served from a suburb that costs $150K–$250K less than Southlake or Westlake. You're buying the same school district at a meaningful discount.
  • No MUD/PID on established neighborhoods — Unlike Prosper, Frisco new construction, and Little Elm where builders layer MUD surcharges on top of base tax rates, most established Roanoke neighborhoods carry no additional district debt. Effective tax rate stays lower.
  • Alliance corridor access — FedEx, Amazon, Lockheed Martin, Bell Textron within 10–15 minutes via I-35W. If you're in logistics, aerospace, or distribution, Roanoke puts you closer than almost any other family suburb.
  • Small-town community feel — Under 10,000 residents, active neighborhood culture, and a city events calendar that actually brings people together. Feels different from the master-planned anonymity of larger DFW suburbs.
  • Strong park network — #9/65 DFW suburbs for green space per resident. 24 parks, Cannon Parkway linear trail, and Eagle Mountain Lake 15 minutes west for weekend lake days.

— What to Weigh

  • Limited big-box retail — Roanoke's small population base means no major mall, no Target, no Costco within city limits. You'll drive to Keller, Grapevine, or Fort Worth for most major shopping — plan on a 20-minute drive as a regular occurrence.
  • Car-dependent outside Oak Street — The Walk Score of 70 applies almost exclusively to the Oak Street corridor. In most residential neighborhoods, you need a car for everything — groceries, school pickup, youth sports. This is standard for DFW but worth being clear-eyed about if you're coming from a city.
  • I-35W traffic into Fort Worth — The morning peak southbound on I-35W toward downtown Fort Worth and Alliance can add significant time (30–45+ minutes in heavy traffic). If your job is downtown Fort Worth, factor this in honestly — the commute is manageable but not short.
  • No major hospital in town — Nearest ERs are Texas Health Harris Methodist Southlake (~15 min) and Texas Health Fort Worth (~20 min). Not an emergency in most situations, but worth knowing before you move.
  • Split ISD — verify by address — Most of Roanoke is Northwest ISD (Denton County), but the city straddles the Denton/Tarrant county line. Confirm school assignments and applicable tax rates by specific address before making any offers.

Always verify the specific school assignment for any address you're considering before making an offer — call NISD enrollment directly at (817) 215-0000.

For most Roanoke addresses in Denton County, the combined rate is approximately $1.61 per $100 assessed value, broken down roughly as: City of Roanoke (~$0.47), Northwest ISD (~$0.98), Denton County (~$0.18), and Denton County community college. Tarrant County addresses within Roanoke carry a slightly different county rate (~$0.19). Texas has no state income tax, which significantly offsets property taxes compared to California, New York, or Illinois. The standard homestead exemption reduces your taxable value — $40,000 off for school district, plus an additional city exemption. Importantly, most established Roanoke neighborhoods carry no MUD or PID surcharge, keeping effective rates lower than comparable new-construction suburbs.

The cost-of-living gap is substantial. A family earning $250,000 in California pays $23,000+ in state income tax; in Texas that's $0. A home comparable to what costs $900K–$1.2M in the Bay Area or $800K+ in Westchester typically runs $500K–$650K in Roanoke. Groceries, gas, and dining run 15–25% cheaper. The tradeoffs: no ocean, no mountains, summer heat (95–100°F for 60+ days), and less cultural/culinary diversity than a major metro. Families who run the full math — after-tax income, housing cost, day-to-day expenses — typically come out $30,000–$60,000 ahead annually in Roanoke versus their origin market.

The most family-popular areas include Fairway Ranch (established neighborhood, large lots, mature trees, easy trail access), Country Acres (quiet residential feel, walkable to Oak Street, larger lots), Briarwyck (newer construction, family-friendly streets, close to the rec center), and Parks at Roanoke (newer master-planned area with direct trail connectivity). The key differentiator between Roanoke neighborhoods is lot size and construction era — established areas give you larger lots and mature landscaping; newer areas give you updated floor plans and builder warranties.

For most families relocating to DFW from out of state, renting first is a good strategy — but Roanoke's rental market is thin. Most single-family rental inventory in the area sits in neighboring Keller, Haslet, or Fort Worth. If renting first is your plan, be prepared to base yourself in a neighboring city and explore Roanoke on weekends. One thing to know about Roanoke specifically: the city is small enough that a couple of weekend visits gives you a very accurate picture of what daily life looks like. I usually recommend two visits — one weekday morning (school traffic, commute feel) and one Saturday afternoon (Oak Street, parks, activity level). That tends to confirm or rule out Roanoke pretty quickly.

Yes, by most measures. Roanoke's violent crime rate (~1.1 per 1,000 residents) is well below the Texas average (~4.0) and the national average (~4.0). Property crime (~12.3 per 1,000) is also below state and national benchmarks. Niche gives Roanoke a B for Crime & Safety. The city has a responsive police department and a close-knit community where unusual activity tends to get noticed and reported. Like any suburb, car break-ins are the most common incident — well-lit garages and not leaving valuables visible are the standard precautions.

No meaningful public transit. There is no DART rail service, no DCTA commuter rail, and no regular bus routes connecting Roanoke to Fort Worth or Dallas. The entire Alliance/Northwest corridor operates the same way — personal vehicles are the default for everything. If you're relocating from a city where you used transit regularly, this is a genuine lifestyle adjustment. Most families have two cars. The highway network (I-35W, Hwy 114, Hwy 170, Hwy 287) is excellent and designed for high-capacity suburban commuting — but it's still driving.

Roanoke's location in the Alliance/I-35W corridor puts it within 10–20 minutes of some of North Texas's largest employer concentrations. The AllianceTexas development anchors the north — FedEx Southwest Hub, Amazon fulfillment, Lockheed Martin Aeronautics, and Bell Textron are all within 10–15 minutes. Charles Schwab's campus in Westlake and Sabre Corporation in Southlake are 15–20 minutes east via Hwy 114. American Airlines Group and DFW Airport operations are 25–30 minutes south. Healthcare employers — Texas Health Harris Methodist — are in Keller/Fort Worth, about 20 minutes. In Roanoke itself, the Walmart Distribution Center is the largest single employer.

Traffic depends heavily on your destination. Going north toward Alliance and Denton: I-35W northbound is light most times of day — this direction is generally easy. Going south toward Fort Worth and DFW Airport: I-35W southbound in the AM peak (7–9am) can back up significantly, adding 15–25 minutes to a typical commute. Going east toward Keller, Southlake, and DFW Airport: Highway 114 is the main route and generally moves well outside peak hours, with moderate congestion near Trophy Club and Roanoke Road intersections. The construction on I-35W's managed lane project has been ongoing in this corridor — check TxDOT updates for current conditions if you're evaluating a specific commute.
