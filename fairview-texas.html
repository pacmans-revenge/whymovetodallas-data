Getting Your Bearings

Where Is Fairview, Texas?

Fairview is a small, affluent city in eastern Collin County, tucked between Allen to the south and McKinney to the north along the US-75 corridor. It occupies roughly 7 square miles east of Highway 75, giving it a distinct low-density character compared to its faster-growing neighbors. The city's position along this corridor is one of its strongest practical assets — US-75 provides direct, predictable access south to Plano and downtown Dallas, and the SH-121/SH-75 interchange nearby opens fast routes west toward Frisco, The Star, and DFW Airport.

For commuters, Fairview punches well above its size. The Plano/Legacy West employment corridor — home to Toyota, JPMorgan Chase, and Liberty Mutual — is just 15 miles south. The Allen/McKinney tech corridor, anchored by Samsung, HP Enterprise, and Cisco, is essentially next door. Downtown Dallas is a straight shot down 75 in under 40 minutes off-peak. Fairview isn't a suburb you move to despite the commute — it's a suburb where the commute is actually one of the selling points.

Budget for that reality before choosing a neighborhood.
Education

Lovejoy ISD — Schools Deep Dive

Lovejoy ISD is one of the most compelling small school districts in Texas — and it's the primary reason families choose Fairview over larger, faster-growing neighbors. With just over 4,000 students across five campuses, Lovejoy runs lean enough to maintain genuine community cohesion while delivering academic outcomes that rival districts three times its size. The TEA rates it an A (94/100). Niche gives it an A+ overall and A+ for college prep. In the last available data, 96% of Lovejoy graduates enrolled in a two- or four-year college. That number isn't accidental — it reflects a community that invests heavily in education and a district that delivers on that investment consistently.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Fairview

Lovejoy Leopards Athletic Culture

Lovejoy ISD punches well above its enrollment size in athletics. The Leopards compete at the 5A level across football, basketball, baseball, soccer, and track — and the community shows up for every game. Youth sports associations through the district feed into high school programs, so kids develop within a single system from early childhood. It's not Frisco-level scale, but it's genuine, tight-knit athletic community that many families prefer.

The Heard Wildlife Sanctuary — Next Door

The Heard Natural Science Museum & Wildlife Sanctuary sits on 289 acres directly adjacent to Fairview — a genuine natural asset that most DFW suburbs can't claim. Birding trails, raptor rehabilitation, native plant gardens, and family educational programs run year-round. For families who hike, birdwatch, or want their kids connecting with nature rather than screens, this is one of the most distinctive quality-of-life features anywhere in North Texas.

Allen Anchors the Shopping & Dining Scene

Fairview has limited in-city dining and retail — this is honest and worth knowing upfront. What it does have is Allen five minutes away: Allen Premium Outlets, Watters Creek at Montgomery Farm (walkable outdoor retail), Village at Allen, and dozens of restaurant options across every price tier. Families who live in Fairview effectively treat Allen as their commercial hub. The inconvenience is minimal; the expectation just needs to be set correctly before you buy.

Small-Town Events Calendar

Fairview leans into its small-city identity — the Fairview 5K, holiday celebrations at Village Green Park, Heritage Ranch community events, and school district functions create a genuine community calendar. It's not the packed social scene of Frisco or McKinney, but residents consistently describe it as the kind of place where you actually know your neighbors' names. The city's HOAs and school parent organizations are unusually active for a community this size.

Heritage Ranch Golf & Country Club Living

Heritage Ranch is one of the few communities in DFW where you can actually live on a golf course — not just near one. The D.A. Weibring-designed course, clubhouse, pool, fitness center, and tennis courts create an amenity package that rivals dedicated country clubs. For families relocating from California communities that included club memberships as standard, Heritage Ranch's HOA amenities represent a similar lifestyle at a fraction of the annual cost.

A Community Where You Actually Stay

Fairview's 89% owner-occupancy rate tells a story that raw population numbers don't: this is a community of people who chose to be here and stay. The neighborhood Facebook groups are active. Parents at Lovejoy games actually talk to each other. Block parties happen in Heritage Ranch. If you're a family relocating from a major metro where anonymity was the norm, this is a meaningful culture shift — and most families who move here consider it one of Fairview's best-kept secrets.

The honest tradeoff: Fairview has no walkable downtown. There's no restaurant strip to stroll after school pickup, no neighborhood coffee shop within walking distance, no Friday-night destination that doesn't involve a car. Families who move here from California cities sometimes underestimate how much they'll miss that urban energy. What Fairview offers instead — mature neighborhoods, elite schools, open space, and a community that actually knows itself — is a different kind of quality of life, not a lesser one. But it is genuinely different, and worth naming before you sign.
Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & City Amenities

Fairview is a car-dependent suburb — that's the honest starting point for every family evaluating it. Walk Score is 0. There is no DART service, no commuter rail, and no meaningful transit infrastructure. A personal vehicle is required for every errand, every school run, and every restaurant trip. What Fairview offers instead is something its walkability score doesn't capture: 289 acres of natural sanctuary adjacent to city limits, a parks system ranked #4 of 65 DFW suburbs, and trail access woven into the established neighborhoods in ways that make the outdoors genuinely accessible without ever getting in a car.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Fairview's parks system ranks #4 of 65 DFW suburbs for green space per resident — a remarkable number for a city of 10,570 people, driven largely by the Heard Natural Science Museum & Wildlife Sanctuary that sits adjacent to the city. Within city limits, the parks network is intentionally distributed across neighborhoods so most residents have a trail access point or playground within walking distance from home.

Heard Natural Science Museum & Wildlife Sanctuary

289 acres of native Texas habitat directly adjacent to Fairview — birding trails, a raptor rehabilitation center, native plant gardens, and rotating family exhibits. One of the most distinctive natural amenities anywhere in DFW; most residents consider it Fairview's single best outdoor asset.

Creekside Park

Fairview's most-visited city park — paved trails along the creek corridor, open playgrounds, multi-use fields, and shade pavilions. Popular with families for weekend walking and kids' sports practice. Trail connection extends into adjacent green corridors.

Beaver Run Park

A natural creek corridor park with walking paths, mature tree canopy, and a quiet, neighborhood feel. Less developed than Creekside by design — families who prefer unstructured nature walks over manicured recreation areas gravitate here.

Monarch Park

Neighborhood park serving central Fairview — playground equipment, open lawn, and picnic areas. Sized appropriately for local use; not a destination park, but exactly the kind of park parents appreciate having within walking distance of home.

Village Green Park

Fairview's central community gathering space — the site of city events, the Fairview 5K, holiday celebrations, and weekend family activities. A splash pad element makes it a summer destination for young children. Small scale, but well-maintained and genuinely used.

Heritage Ranch Golf Course

The D.A. Weibring-designed championship course within Heritage Ranch — 18 holes on a well-maintained course with driving range and practice facilities. Available to Heritage Ranch residents; private club access is one of the community's signature amenities.

Crime & Safety

How Safe Is Fairview?

Fairview is one of the safest communities in Collin County — which is itself one of the safest counties in Texas. Violent crime rates are near zero by any standard measure. Property crime is low but not absent: typical suburban patterns of vehicle break-ins and opportunistic theft exist, and Heritage Ranch's gated entry provides an additional deterrent layer for that community. Niche rates Fairview's crime and safety at 83/100 (grade B). For families relocating from high-crime metro areas, the safety profile here is a genuine, material quality-of-life improvement.

Pros & Cons of Living in Fairview

I won't sell you on Fairview. I'll give you the real picture so you can decide if it's right for your family.

✓ What Fairview Does Well

  • Lovejoy ISD is elite — TEA A (94/100), Niche A+, 96% college matriculation rate. One of the best small school districts in Texas, with a unified feeder pattern that keeps your kids in the same academic community from kindergarten through graduation.
  • Near-zero violent crime — Fairview's violent crime rate is among the lowest in Collin County. NeighborhoodScout reports it at approximately 0.5 per 1,000 residents vs. a U.S. average of 4.0. This is a genuine, measurable safety advantage.
  • Parks ranked #4 of 65 DFW suburbs — The Heard Natural Science Museum & Wildlife Sanctuary adjacent to city limits, Creekside Park trail system, and Beaver Run natural corridor give Fairview outdoor access that most DFW communities can't match.
  • Mature, low-density neighborhoods — Established streets with mature trees, larger lots, and neighbors who stay. A fundamentally different character from the growth-town pace of Frisco or Prosper.
  • US-75 corridor puts everything within reach — Plano/Legacy West 15 min, Allen/McKinney tech hub 10 min, Downtown Dallas 35 min. Better commute access than the price and density suggest.
  • 89% owner-occupancy — A genuinely invested, stable community. Neighbors who own tend to care about streets, schools, and each other in ways that renters-heavy communities often don't.
  • No state income tax — Meaningful savings, especially for high earners from CA, NY, or IL.

— What to Weigh Carefully

  • Older community demographics — Median age of 50+; only 24% of households have children. Notably lower than Frisco (41%), Allen (40%), or McKinney (38%). Families with young kids will find fewer same-age playmates in the neighborhood than in younger suburbs.
  • Heritage Ranch has significant 55+ sections — This is the largest community in Fairview and portions are age-restricted. Families must verify their specific address is in the unrestricted section — a critical pre-offer step.
  • Limited in-city retail and dining — Virtually all grocery shopping, restaurants, and entertainment require a 5–10 minute drive to Allen or McKinney. There is no walkable commercial district within Fairview city limits.
  • Entry price is elevated — Median SFR at $749K leaves a limited inventory below $600K. Families stretching a budget will find more options per dollar in McKinney, Allen, or Plano.
  • Very limited rental market — If you want to rent before buying to test the area, Fairview has almost no rental inventory. A neighboring Allen or McKinney rental while you shop is the practical approach.
  • Car-dependent, fully — No transit whatsoever. Two-car households are the norm, and budget for fuel and maintenance accordingly.

Common Questions

Fairview, Texas — Frequently Asked Questions

Fairview is an excellent choice for families who prioritize elite school quality, low crime, and a quieter community feel over urban amenity density. Lovejoy ISD earns an A+ from Niche and a 94/100 TEA rating, and 96% of graduates enroll in college. The tradeoff is a mature community where only 24% of households have children — lower than most DFW suburbs — so families with young kids may find fewer neighborhood playmates than in Frisco or Allen.

Lovejoy ISD earns an A rating from the Texas Education Agency (TEA score of 94/100) and an A+ overall grade from Niche with A+ specifically for college prep. The district serves approximately 4,092 students across five campuses — LCDC (early childhood), Hart and Puster elementary schools, Sloan Creek Intermediate, Willow Springs Middle School, and Lovejoy High School. Lovejoy High is ranked 65th in Texas by U.S. News & World Report, and 96% of graduates enroll in a two- or four-year college.

The combined property tax rate in Fairview is approximately $1.8773 per $100 of assessed value — or roughly 1.88%. That breaks down as: Lovejoy ISD ($1.2552) + City of Fairview ($0.3103) + Collin County ($0.2306) + Collin College ($0.0812). On a $749,000 home, that amounts to approximately $14,070 per year before the homestead exemption.

Texas offers a homestead exemption that reduces your assessed taxable value — for most owner-occupied residences, this provides meaningful annual savings. Rates are certified annually by the Texas Comptroller. Always verify the current rate and any special district assessments for a specific property address before closing. New construction in Fairview is rare enough that MUD/PID districts are uncommon in established neighborhoods, but worth confirming on any new build.

The most significant financial difference is state income tax: California charges 9.3%+ (up to 13.3%) and New York adds state and city tax stacking to similar levels. Texas charges zero. For a household earning $250,000, that's roughly $23,000+ saved annually before considering any other cost difference. Even with Fairview's 1.88% property tax rate — higher than California's Prop 13-capped ~1.1% — most California families come out substantially ahead overall.

On housing, Fairview's $749K median is below the Bay Area and comparable to or below much of suburban New York. The schools are competitive — Lovejoy ISD's outcomes match or exceed many high-cost California school districts. What families give up is density, walkability, and urban culture. What they gain is space, lower total tax burden, and a school district that actually delivers on the investment.

Heritage Ranch is Fairview's largest community with a golf course, pool, and fitness center — but it has significant 55+ sections, so families must verify their specific address is in the unrestricted portion before making an offer. Chamberlain Place is gated luxury with large lots ($900K–$1.5M+). The Estates at Fairview and Thompson Springs offer established single-family homes in the $650K–$900K range with mature trees. For the most accessible entry point, the Town Center area has options in the $550K–$750K range. All Fairview neighborhoods feed the same Lovejoy ISD campuses.

Renting first is a good instinct — but Fairview has very limited rental inventory given its 89% owner-occupancy rate. Practically speaking, most families who want to "try before buying" rent in adjacent Allen or McKinney instead, which gives them Lovejoy ISD access (for Lucas/Fairview address zones) or a comparable school district (Allen ISD) while they search. This approach gives you time to understand neighborhood differences without missing the limited Fairview inventory while you wait on a lease to expire.

Fairview is one of the safest communities in Collin County. Violent crime rates are approximately 0.5 per 1,000 residents — compared to a U.S. average of 4.0 — making it statistically among the lowest-crime cities in North Texas. Property crime runs around 10 per 1,000, below the Texas average of 22.4. Niche rates Fairview's crime and safety at 83/100 (B grade). Heritage Ranch's gated entry adds an additional deterrent layer for that community specifically.

A personal vehicle is not optional — it is essential for every daily activity including school pickup, grocery runs, and commuting. Households with two working adults should budget for two vehicles. This is true of nearly all DFW suburbs outside the immediate Dallas urban core, but it is worth naming clearly for families relocating from transit-connected cities.

Fairview is a residential city with limited in-city employment, but its US-75 corridor location provides fast access to three major employment hubs. The Allen/McKinney tech corridor (10–15 min) includes Samsung Electronics, HP Enterprise, Cisco Systems, Tyler Technologies, and NTT Data. The Plano/Legacy West hub (15–20 min) anchors Toyota North America, JPMorgan Chase, Liberty Mutual, and Fannie Mae. The Richardson Telecom Corridor (22–28 min) hosts AT&T, Ericsson, and State Farm regional offices. DFW Airport — home of American Airlines headquarters — is about 40–50 minutes west via SH-635.

US-75 is Fairview's primary artery, and like most major DFW corridors it has peak-hour congestion — southbound during the 7–9 AM window adds 15–20 minutes to a downtown Dallas or Plano commute. The Allen/McKinney tech corridor north of Fairview runs cleanly most mornings. SH-121 access west toward Frisco is typically smooth outside construction periods. Compared to I-35, I-635, or I-30 commutes in the western DFW suburbs, the US-75 corridor from Fairview performs relatively well — but "relatively well" still means a personal vehicle and some patience at peak hours.
