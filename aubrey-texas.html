Getting Your Bearings

Where Is Aubrey, Texas?

Aubrey sits in northern Denton County along US Highway 380 — the spine of one of the fastest-growing corridors in all of North Texas. It's roughly 12 miles west of Prosper and 12 miles east of downtown Denton, which puts it in an enviable middle ground: close enough to Frisco's tech corridor for a manageable commute, far enough out to keep home prices 30–40% below what you'd pay in Prosper or Little Elm. The key access point is US-380, which runs east–west through the city and connects directly to the Dallas North Tollway and US-75 heading south into the greater metro.

The Aubrey of 2026 is genuinely two places: the original small town of Aubrey proper — a quiet, charming Texas community with a main street feel — and a rapidly growing collection of master-planned communities (Providence Village, Paloma Creek, Sandbrock Ranch) that sit within the Aubrey zip code (76227) but in many cases are technically unincorporated Denton County. This distinction matters more than it sounds, because it determines which school district your kids attend. One street east or west can mean a different ISD entirely. I walk every client through this before we look at a listing.

Education

Schools in Aubrey — The ISD Split You Need to Know

This is the most important thing to understand before you buy in Aubrey: the 76227 zip code is served by two separate ISDs. Aubrey Independent School District serves the city of Aubrey proper and the northern portions of Providence Village. Denton Independent School District serves Sandbrock Ranch, Paloma Creek, and the southern portions of Providence Village. The dividing line is roughly Fishtrap Road in Providence Village. A home half a mile in one direction can land your kids in a completely different school system — different ratings, different campuses, different feeder patterns. Always verify by specific address before making an offer.

ISD verification rule: Use Aubrey ISD's attendance zone tool or Denton ISD's zone lookup — or call me. I check ISD zoning on every offer I write in this zip code. Do not rely on Zillow or Redfin to get this right.

Aubrey Independent School District

Aubrey ISD serves the city of Aubrey and parts of Providence Village. It is a growing district — enrollment has roughly doubled over the past decade as master-planned communities expanded into the service area. The rapid growth is both an opportunity and a challenge: newer facilities are being built, but the district's TEA accountability score dipped in 2025.

Day-to-Day Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Aubrey

Aubrey's lifestyle is defined by open space, water, and the deliberate pace of a North Texas community that hasn't been fully discovered yet. It's not a "big city suburb" — it's a place where kids ride bikes in the neighborhood, families know their neighbors, and Lake Ray Roberts is 20 minutes away for a weekend that doesn't cost anything. If that's what you're after, Aubrey delivers it in spades.

Lake Ray Roberts State Park

A 29,000-acre lake with hiking trails, equestrian paths, camping, fishing, and boating — 20 minutes from Aubrey. This is the lifestyle anchor that sets Aubrey apart. Most DFW suburbs don't have anything like it within reasonable driving distance.

Community Pools & Amenity Centers

Paloma Creek, Sandbrock Ranch, and Providence Village each have resort-style pools, splash pads, and fitness centers built into HOA fees. For families, this is real value — you're not driving 20 minutes to a rec center; you're walking to your neighborhood pool.

Small-Town Events & Community Culture

Aubrey hosts a Fourth of July celebration, a Fall Festival, and various community events through the year. The master-planned communities add their own pool parties, movie nights, and holiday events. It has the neighborhood fabric that larger suburbs have to manufacture.

US-380 Corridor Dining & Retail

Aubrey's local dining and retail options are growing but still limited — think a handful of solid local spots, a HEB nearby, and a short drive to Little Elm, Denton, or Frisco for broader options. The trade-off for space and affordability is that you'll drive for most shopping trips. That's the honest picture.

Trails, Equestrian, & Outdoor Recreation

Aubrey's rural roots are still visible — equestrian properties exist near the city, and the master-planned communities have trail systems built in. Lake Ray Roberts adds equestrian-specific trails and horse camping that are genuinely rare this close to a major metro.

Youth Sports & Family Recreation

Community youth baseball, soccer, football, and basketball leagues operate through both Aubrey ISD and community-organized programs. The master-planned communities add swimming programs, youth fitness, and organized recreation. It's an active family community — kids are outdoors and busy.

Honest tradeoff: Aubrey trades urban convenience for space, quiet, and nature access. If you want walkable coffee shops, a vibrant restaurant scene, or weekend options without a car, this isn't the right fit. If you want a big back yard, a community pool your kids actually use, and a lake 20 minutes away — Aubrey is hard to beat at this price point. Many families relocating from California or out of state find exactly what they were looking for here.
Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & Outdoor Access

Aubrey is a car-dependent suburb — that's the baseline. There are no walkable commercial districts and no public transit. What it trades in walkability it more than compensates for in outdoor access: Lake Ray Roberts State Park, community trail networks in every master-planned community, and the wide-open North Texas landscape. Families here don't walk to coffee — they walk to their neighborhood pool or ride trails through the community.

Crime & Safety

Aubrey is a safe suburban and rural community. Niche gives the area a B for Crime & Safety — solid for a small, growing suburb. Property crime rates are below Texas state averages, and the violent crime rate is very low in absolute terms. One important context note: Aubrey's small city population (~8,500) means any statistical per-capita crime rate can swing dramatically from a single incident in a given year. Some national crime databases show an elevated violent crime rate for Aubrey — this reflects a small-city base rate artifact, not a meaningful safety concern for families moving to the master-planned communities.

Honest Assessment

Pros & Cons of Living in Aubrey

I won't sell you on Aubrey. Here's the real picture so you can decide if it's right for your family.

✓ What Aubrey Does Well

  • Exceptional value — $350K buys a 2,400–3,000 sq ft home here. The same money gets you half that in Frisco or Prosper. For families who need space, this is the strongest value proposition in North DFW.
  • New construction inventory — Multiple active builder communities mean you can customize, get builder incentives (rate buydowns, closing cost help, upgrades), and move in quickly without bidding wars.
  • Lake Ray Roberts proximity — A 29,000-acre lake with camping, hiking, fishing, and equestrian trails. Less than 25 minutes away. No comparable suburb in DFW has this.
  • Master-planned community amenities — Paloma Creek, Sandbrock Ranch, and Providence Village deliver resort-style pools, trails, and fitness facilities included in HOA fees.
  • Room to breathe — Bigger lots, less density, genuine rural character at the edges. If your family needs space to spread out, Aubrey delivers it.
  • Buyer's market conditions — 6 months of supply and 48 days on market means you can negotiate. That's rare in DFW right now.
  • No state income tax — Significant savings for high earners, especially those relocating from California, New York, or Illinois.

— What to Weigh Carefully

  • Dual ISD split — address-by-address verification required — The 76227 zip code spans both Aubrey ISD (TEA C/79) and Denton ISD (B). The difference is significant for families with school-age kids. One street can change the district. Verify every address before you fall in love with a house.
  • Aubrey ISD rating — If you land in Aubrey ISD, the district carries a TEA C rating — the weakest academic rating in this area. Some families are fine with it; others want a B or A ISD regardless of price.
  • MUD/PID fees in new communities — Sandbrock Ranch, Paloma Creek, and parts of Providence Village carry special district fees that add $1,750–$5,250/year indefinitely. These don't go away. Model the full tax picture before buying.
  • Car-dependent — all of it — Walk Score of 7. No transit. Two cars required. Every errand, every activity, every school pickup involves driving. This is a meaningful quality-of-life factor, especially if you have a spouse returning from a walkable city environment.
  • Limited local retail and dining — The dining and shopping options in Aubrey proper are thin. HEB is close, but variety means driving 15–25 minutes to Little Elm, Denton, or Frisco. Not a dealbreaker, but plan for it.
  • US-380 congestion is growing — The corridor is developing fast and the road hasn't kept up. Peak-hour driving on US-380 is increasingly slow. It won't get better before it gets addressed with infrastructure.
  • City services and infrastructure still maturing — Aubrey proper is a small city. Roads, municipal services, and emergency response infrastructure are not at the level of Frisco or Prosper. The master-planned communities compensate somewhat, but it's worth understanding.

Important: new communities like Sandbrock Ranch, Paloma Creek, and some Providence Village sections carry MUD (Municipal Utility District) or PID (Public Improvement District) fees, adding $0.50–$1.50 per $100 — an extra $1,750–$5,250/year. These fees fund development infrastructure and don't go away. Always confirm the full tax picture by specific address before committing to purchase. Denton ISD area rates may differ slightly.

Yes — Aubrey is a safe community. Niche grades the area a B for Crime & Safety. Property crime rates are below Texas state averages. Some national crime databases (NeighborhoodScout) show higher per-capita violent crime for Aubrey — this is a base rate artifact of a very small city population (~8,500) where one or two incidents in a year can spike the per-capita rate significantly.
