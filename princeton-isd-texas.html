Princeton ISD Overview

11,787 Students (2024–25)
17 Campuses
B TEA Grade (2025)
B+ Niche Grade (3rd-party)
98% Graduation Rate (2024–25)
$1.26 Tax Rate (per $100)

Princeton ISD serves a rapidly expanding community in eastern Collin County — covering 60 square miles and the city of Princeton along with Culleoka, Lowry Crossing, Branch, Climax, and the western shore of Lake Lavon. If you're considering Princeton because it offers more home for your budget than Frisco or Allen, the schools are a natural next question. The honest answer: this is a solid, improving district — not the highest-ranked in DFW — but one that earns a TEA B rating, a 98% graduation rate, and a Niche B+ (third-party), while building aggressively to keep pace with one of the fastest growth rates in Collin County.

The tradeoff families should weigh honestly: Princeton ISD is in the middle of a major build-out. It passed a $350 million bond in 2023 and projects enrollment to nearly double to approximately 19,574 students by 2034. That investment is a good sign for long-term stability, but it also means attendance zones are actively shifting, new campuses are opening, and your child's elementary school may change as the district right-sizes its boundaries. For a family that wants established infrastructure and a stable peer group from kindergarten through graduation, that's a real consideration. For a family looking to get into Collin County early — with solid schools, affordable homes, and upside — Princeton ISD may be exactly the right fit.

The district also runs a distinctive two-high-school model: Princeton Senior High School serves the traditional college-prep pathway, while Lovelady High School offers alternative academic pathways for students who thrive in a different setting. That's worth understanding before you buy, especially if you have a high schooler or a child approaching high school age.

Best Fit For Families Who…

  • Are buying in eastern Collin County where home prices are more accessible than Frisco or Allen
  • Want solid academics with a 98% graduation rate and improving TEA performance
  • Value strong athletic programs and a spirited, tight-knit school community
  • Are comfortable with a district actively growing and building — and understand attendance zone fluidity
  • Have children who would benefit from the Career & Technical Education (CTE) or dual credit pathway

May Not Be the Best Fit If…

  • You prioritize a full IB or advanced magnet program menu (not currently offered districtwide)
  • You want an academically higher-ranked district — Frisco, Allen, McKinney, or Carroll ISD will rank above Princeton on most metrics
  • Stable attendance zones matter — rapid growth means rezoning is ongoing
  • You're buying in a new construction community and assume the nearest school will stay the same — verify before closing
⚠ Boundary Verification

Not every home with a Princeton, TX mailing address falls within Princeton ISD boundaries. The district covers 60 square miles and includes communities that may be partially zoned to a neighboring district. Always verify your specific address using the district's official campus locator at princetonisd.net/attendance-zones before making an offer.

With new campuses opening under the 2023 bond program, attendance zones are actively being redrawn. A home that feeds to one elementary school today may be rezoned to a newer campus within one to two years of purchase. Confirm current and planned boundaries with the district's enrollment office.

TEA Accountability Rating

Princeton ISD earned a B rating (score: 83 out of 100) on the 2025 Texas Education Agency A–F Accountability System — reflecting 2024–25 academic data. The TEA evaluates districts across three primary domains: Student Achievement, School Progress, and Closing the Gaps.

Student Achievement

Domain I

Measures the percentage of students meeting grade-level proficiency on STAAR assessments, graduation rates, and college, career, and military readiness (CCMR). Princeton's 98% graduation rate is a strong signal in this domain.

School Progress

Domain II

Evaluates year-over-year academic growth for individual students and compares the district's growth to similar campuses. This domain rewards improvement — Princeton's trajectory in a high-growth environment is part of its story.

Closing the Gaps

Domain III

Assesses performance across student subgroups — economically disadvantaged, emergent bilingual, and students with disabilities. With 62% of students qualifying as economically disadvantaged and 22% emergent bilingual, this domain reflects the district's work serving a diverse population.

A TEA B rating (score 80–89) in practical terms means the district is performing above the statewide floor and meeting most accountability benchmarks, but has room to close gaps — particularly in advanced academic outcomes and subgroup performance. For families comparing Princeton ISD to an A-rated neighbor like McKinney ISD, the gap is real and worth acknowledging. The meaningful question isn't whether Princeton scores lower than McKinney on paper, but whether the campus your child would attend is strong for their specific needs — and that requires looking at individual campus data, not just the district score.

Princeton ISD also earned a Financial Integrity Rating of A (Superior Achievement) from the FIRST system (2024–25), meaning it is managing public funds responsibly — an important signal in a district that is simultaneously growing rapidly and funding multiple new construction projects.

Academic Performance

The most current academic performance data for Princeton ISD comes from the 2024–25 Texas Academic Performance Report (TAPR) and TEA accountability data. Key indicators:

  • Graduation rate: 98% (2024–25, per district)
  • Princeton Senior High School Class of 2023 on-time graduation: 97.5% (Texas Tribune)
  • Daily attendance rate: 93.1% (2024–25)
  • Economically disadvantaged: 62.14% of students
  • Emergent bilingual: 22.13% of students
  • Special education: 15.01% of students
  • Student-teacher ratio: approximately 18:1 (per Niche; district has 1,497 total staff)

What these numbers mean in practice: Princeton's 98% graduation rate is competitive with districts rated far higher — it tells you that once students are enrolled, the district keeps them on track to finish. The 18:1 student-teacher ratio is average by DFW standards; not the small-class-size feel of a Lovejoy or Highland Park, but manageable. The proportion of economically disadvantaged students (62%) is significantly higher than neighboring Frisco (around 20%) or Allen — which reflects the demographics of eastern Collin County, not a quality problem. It also means state funding covers a larger share of the budget, giving the district more stability than a purely local-tax-dependent district would have during a Texas legislature session.

Princeton ISD does not currently offer an International Baccalaureate (IB) program. Dual credit courses are available for high school students through partnerships with Collin College, allowing students to earn college credit while still in high school. Advanced Placement (AP) course offerings exist at Princeton Senior High School — verify current course availability directly with the campus counselor, as the catalog expands with enrollment growth.

Princeton ISD Campuses

Princeton ISD has grown substantially and as of 2025–26 operates 17 campuses: 2 early childhood centers, 9 elementary schools, 4 middle schools, and 2 high school-level campuses. Two additional campuses are planned to open under the 2023 bond program.

High Schools

Princeton Senior High School (PSHS)

High School ~1,741 students (2023–24) Princeton Panthers

The district's flagship campus, serving the traditional 9–12 pathway with AP courses, dual credit, CTE programs, fine arts (band, choir, visual arts), and a full athletics program. Princeton's marching band hosts the annual Sounds of Fall Marching Classic — a regional invitational that draws bands from across North Texas.

Lovelady High School

High School Alternative Pathways

Lovelady High School provides an alternative academic setting for students who benefit from smaller learning environments, flexible pacing, and non-traditional instruction. It is the second high school-level campus in the district and serves students who may be returning to school, working toward credit recovery, or who thrive outside the traditional comprehensive high school setting. Contact the district enrollment office for current eligibility and intake process.

Middle Schools

Clark Middle School

Middle School Grades 6–8

One of the district's established middle campuses, serving students from several elementary feeder zones in the Princeton community.

Mattei Middle School

Middle School Grades 6–8

Serves students from the newer residential growth corridors east and south of the city center. Opened to relieve capacity as Princeton's population surged.

Southard Middle School

Middle School Grades 6–8

A newer middle campus added to accommodate continued growth; serves subdivisions added to Princeton ISD's footprint in recent years.

Banschbach Middle School

Middle School Grades 6–8 Newest Campus

The fourth middle school campus, opened to serve new construction communities on Princeton ISD's northern and eastern boundary. Attendance zone boundaries for this campus were among the most recently established — verify your home's assignment directly with the enrollment office.

Elementary Schools

Princeton ISD operates nine elementary schools as of 2025–26: Carrell, Godwin, Green, Harper, James, Lacy, Lowe, Mayfield, and Smith. Two additional elementary campuses are expected to open under the 2023 bond program (projected Fall 2026). Elementary campus boundaries are the most fluid in the district — always verify which elementary your specific address is zoned to before purchasing, as new campuses will trigger rezoning of existing feeder zones.

Specialty Programs & Academics

Dual Credit (Collin College)

Available at Princeton Senior High School. Districtwide access for qualifying students. Princeton ISD partners with Collin College to allow high school juniors and seniors to earn college credit tuition-free. Courses count toward both high school graduation and a Collin College transcript. No campus-specific application — access is through your high school counselor based on eligibility (GPA, TSI scores). Transportation is parent-provided for courses held on campus.

Career & Technical Education (CTE)

Districtwide program; primary delivery at Princeton Senior High School. Princeton ISD's CTE department offers pathways in business, technology, health science, agriculture, and trades. Students who complete a CTE pathway may earn industry certifications alongside their diploma. Families specifically targeting CTE programs should confirm current pathway availability with PSHS's counseling office — offerings expand as the district grows.

Gifted & Talented (GT)

Districtwide program; students identified and served at their home campus. Princeton ISD runs a Gifted & Talented program beginning in elementary school. Students are referred for evaluation by teachers or parents; the identification process involves assessment of cognitive ability, achievement, and other criteria per state guidelines. Families moving to Princeton ISD with a child already identified as GT in another Texas district should bring prior GT documentation to facilitate a smooth transition.

Bilingual / ESL Education

Districtwide; available at all campuses. With 22.13% of students classified as emergent bilingual, Princeton ISD runs a robust Bilingual/ESL department. Elementary campuses offer bilingual instruction; secondary campuses offer ESL support. Families with students who receive ESL services in another district should contact the Princeton ISD Bilingual/ESL department when enrolling to arrange appropriate placement.

Fine Arts

Districtwide, with strong program at PSHS. Princeton ISD's fine arts offerings include band, choir, visual arts, and dance (Claw Crew). The PSHS marching band hosts the annual Sounds of Fall Marching Classic — a competitive invitational that draws widespread participation. If extracurricular fine arts are a priority for your family, Princeton's program is more developed than its overall district ranking might suggest.

Athletics

Districtwide; UIL competition at all levels. Princeton Panthers athletics is a central pillar of community identity. The district competes in UIL and fields teams across football, basketball, baseball, softball, track, soccer, and more. Jackie Hendricks Stadium serves as the primary football venue. Families relocating from competitive athletic programs elsewhere in Texas will find Princeton ISD's athletics program well-resourced for a district of its size.

No IB Programme

Princeton ISD does not currently offer an International Baccalaureate (IB) programme. Families for whom IB is a priority should look at districts such as Plano ISD, McKinney ISD, or Richardson ISD, which have established IB campuses in the DFW area.

Enrollment & Registration

Princeton ISD enrollment is managed through the district's online registration portal, Infinite Campus. The steps below reflect the 2025–26 enrollment process — always verify current windows and required documents directly with the district.

Step-by-Step Enrollment (2025–26)

  1. Determine your campus. Use the district's attendance zone map at princetonisd.net/attendance-zones to confirm which elementary, middle, and high school your address is zoned to. Do this before submitting any enrollment paperwork.
  2. Gather required documents. Typically: proof of residency (current utility bill or lease), child's birth certificate, immunization records, most recent report card or transcript, and any prior IEP/504 documentation.
  3. Create an account in Infinite Campus. Visit Princeton ISD's parent portal to begin the online registration process. New families create an account; returning families log in to complete annual re-enrollment.
  4. Contact your campus registrar. After submitting online paperwork, schedule an in-person appointment with your campus registrar to submit original documents. A list of registrars by campus is at princetonisd.net/registrars.
  5. Kindergarten Roundup. Families enrolling kindergartners participate in Kindergarten Roundup — check the district calendar for the 2025–26 date. Pre-K (eligibility-based, free, full-day) is available at Canup and Perkins Early Childhood Centers; enrollment opens April 1 annually.

Transfer Policy

Intra-district transfers (moving your child to a different campus within Princeton ISD) are subject to campus capacity. Submit a Transfer Request through the district website — princetonisd.net/transfer-requests — and expect decisions to be capacity-dependent, particularly as new campuses reach capacity quickly.

Inter-district transfers (enrolling in Princeton ISD from a different district, or requesting to attend a neighboring district) require approval from both districts. Princeton ISD's rapid growth means inbound transfer approvals may be limited by capacity at specific campuses. Contact the enrollment office directly for current availability.

IEP / 504 Transfers

Under Texas law, Princeton ISD must hold an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting within 30 calendar days of a transfer student's enrollment to review and adopt, adjust, or develop a new IEP. Bring all prior documentation — existing IEP, most recent evaluation reports, 504 plan if applicable — when you enroll. Contact Princeton ISD Special Programs at princetonisd.net/special-programs before enrollment if your child has complex needs; early contact helps ensure a smooth transition.

Boundary Verification — What Families Must Know

Do not skip this step

Making an offer on a home without verifying your specific address against Princeton ISD's current attendance zone map can mean your child attends a different school than you planned — and boundary maps do not always match common-sense geography. Always verify your address before closing.

Communities Served

Princeton ISD covers approximately 60 square miles in eastern Collin County, encompassing the city of Princeton and surrounding unincorporated communities including Culleoka, Lowry Crossing, Branch, Climax, and the western shore of Lake Lavon. Not all addresses in these areas are exclusively within Princeton ISD — some rural parcels on district edges may be zoned to an adjacent district.

Common Boundary Risks

New construction communities are the highest-risk area for boundary confusion. Princeton, TX is among the fastest-growing cities in Collin County, with dozens of active master-planned developments. Homes built in 2023–2025 in some subdivisions are being phased into newer campus attendance zones as Banschbach Middle School and upcoming elementary campuses come online. The subdivision your builder sold you into last year may have a different school assignment today than at the time of contract.

Lake Lavon-area rural properties on the western shore may have Princeton ISD zoning but should be verified individually — road geometry and parcel shapes in lake communities do not always follow intuitive boundaries.

Northern and eastern fringe areas where Princeton's growth corridor meets neighboring districts require extra verification. Some addresses with a Princeton ZIP code (75407) are not in Princeton ISD. A 75407 ZIP code is not a reliable proxy for school district assignment.

How to Verify

  • Princeton ISD Attendance Zones: princetonisd.net/attendance-zones — the official source for current campus assignments by address
  • TEA School Locator: tea.texas.gov/apps/school-directory — state-level address lookup tool
  • Call the district directly: 469-952-5400 — enrollment office can verify your specific address and inform you of any pending rezoning due to new campus openings

Finances & Tax Rate

Princeton ISD's 2024–25 certified total tax rate is approximately $1.26 per $100 of assessed value (M&O + I&S combined). This is one of the higher tax rates among Collin County districts — a direct result of the significant bond debt Princeton has taken on to build new schools to serve its rapidly growing population.

What This Means for Your Mortgage

On a $400,000 home in Princeton ISD (a reasonable entry-level price for the area), a $1.26 rate translates to approximately $5,040 in annual property taxes on the school district portion, or roughly $420 per month added to your total housing cost (before Texas's $100,000 homestead exemption, which reduces the taxable value). After the homestead exemption, the effective tax on that $400,000 home falls to approximately $3,780/year ($315/month) on the school district portion. Texas offers no state income tax — a meaningful offset for families relocating from California, New York, or Illinois.

2023 Bond Program

Princeton ISD voters approved a $350 million bond in 2023 to fund eight new campuses (including elementary, middle, and support facilities) to handle projected growth to nearly 19,574 students by 2034. This bond is reflected in the I&S (Interest and Sinking) portion of the tax rate. Bond-funded construction is a multi-year commitment — new campuses coming online will continue to reshape attendance zones through the late 2020s.

Community & School Culture

Princeton, TX has a genuinely small-town feel that persists even as new subdivisions spring up rapidly. The school district is the civic center of community life — Friday night football at Jackie Hendricks Stadium draws the whole town, and booster clubs for athletics, fine arts, and academics are active and well-supported. For families relocating from larger, more anonymous suburban districts in other states, the level of parent involvement and community visibility around schools here often comes as a pleasant surprise.

One distinctive cultural note: Princeton ISD hosts its own annual Sounds of Fall Marching Classic — a competitive invitational for high school marching bands from across North Texas. For a district this size, running a named, regional invitational speaks to the investment the community makes in the fine arts program. Families with children in band, choir, or visual arts will find that these programs punch above Princeton ISD's overall district weight class.

The honest friction points that come up among current parents: with 62% of students qualifying as economically disadvantaged, classroom dynamics and resource distribution vary more across campuses than they do in wealthier Collin County districts. Some campuses have higher Title I allocations and parent engagement levels than others. The district's rapid growth also means that not every new campus has the same established culture and staff continuity as older campuses — some newer schools are still finding their footing, staffing up, and building traditions. Families who are campus-specific in their priorities — not just district-level — will want to research individual schools before finalizing a neighborhood choice.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does a Princeton, TX address guarantee enrollment in Princeton ISD? ▾
No — not automatically. Princeton ISD covers 60 square miles across the city of Princeton and surrounding communities, but not every address within those communities is within district boundaries. Additionally, a 75407 ZIP code is not a reliable proxy for Princeton ISD enrollment. Always verify your specific property address using the official attendance zone map at princetonisd.net/attendance-zones or by calling the district enrollment office at 469-952-5400. This is especially important for new construction homes, where attendance zones are actively being redrawn as new campuses open.
What is the difference between Princeton Senior High School and Lovelady High School? ▾
Princeton Senior High School (PSHS) is the district's comprehensive, traditional high school serving grades 9–12 with AP courses, dual credit, CTE programs, full athletics, and fine arts. It is the campus most students in the district attend. Lovelady High School is an alternative campus designed for students who thrive in a smaller, non-traditional learning environment — including students working toward credit recovery, those who need flexible scheduling, or those who learn better outside a large comprehensive campus setting. Families with high schoolers should confirm their child's intended campus during enrollment and ask the district counseling office which campus best fits their student's situation.
How does Princeton ISD handle IEP and 504 transfers from another school district? ▾
Under Texas state law, Princeton ISD must convene an ARD (Admission, Review, and Dismissal) meeting within 30 calendar days of enrollment for any student transferring with an existing IEP. At the ARD, the team reviews the prior IEP and decides whether to adopt it as-is, make adjustments, or develop a new one based on the Texas student's needs. For 504 plans, the process is similar — Princeton ISD's 504 coordinator will review the prior plan and determine appropriate accommodations. Bring all prior documentation (current IEP, evaluation reports, progress reports, 504 plan) when you enroll. Contact Princeton ISD Special Programs in advance — early communication helps ensure the right supports are in place from your child's first day. Visit princetonisd.net/special-programs for contact information.
Why does Princeton ISD have a B from TEA but a B+ from Niche? ▾
TEA and Niche measure different things using different methodologies. TEA's A–F system is a state accountability rating based on STAAR proficiency data, graduation rates, student growth metrics, and performance across subgroups — it is an official, government-issued academic accountability measure. Niche's grades incorporate TEA data but also factor in college readiness signals, student-teacher ratios, parent and student reviews, and other factors. The slight difference (TEA B vs. Niche B+) is not unusual and reflects the fact that Niche weights certain community and outcome factors differently than TEA's accountability formula. For academic performance comparisons across districts, TEA's data from txschools.gov is the authoritative source.
What is Princeton ISD's transfer policy for families who want to attend a different campus? ▾
Intra-district transfers — requesting a different campus within Princeton ISD — are processed through the district's Transfer Request system at princetonisd.net/transfer-requests. Approval is subject to campus capacity, which is a real constraint in a fast-growing district where many campuses are already running near enrollment limits. Inter-district transfers — enrolling in Princeton ISD from outside the district, or requesting to attend a neighboring district — require approval from both districts and are evaluated case-by-case. Contact the enrollment office at 469-952-5400 for current availability and timelines, as policies and capacity shift with each new campus opening.
How does Princeton ISD's $1.26 tax rate compare to nearby districts? ▾
Princeton ISD's $1.26 combined tax rate (M&O + I&S) is on the higher end for Collin County, reflecting the significant bond debt the district has taken on to build eight new campuses to handle projected growth to nearly 19,574 students by 2034. For comparison: McKinney ISD is approximately $1.17, Frisco ISD is approximately $1.10, and Grapevine-Colleyville ISD is notably lower at approximately $0.92. However, Princeton's home values are substantially lower than those districts — so even with a higher rate, the absolute dollar amount of property taxes is often lower than a Frisco or Allen home at a higher price point. Run the math on your specific purchase price and compare apples to apples.
Will my child's school change after we buy a home in Princeton? ▾
Possibly — and this is an important question to ask before closing. Princeton ISD is actively opening new campuses under its 2023 bond program, and each new campus triggers a rezoning of existing attendance zones. A home purchased in 2024 that is currently zoned to one elementary may be reassigned to a new campus in 2025 or 2026 as enrollment is redistributed. The district provides advance notice of boundary changes, and families have some transition time, but the school assignment at the time of purchase is not permanently guaranteed. Families for whom a specific campus is critical should call the district enrollment office and ask directly whether that campus's boundaries are expected to change in the next two to three years.
