Getting Your Bearings

Where Is University Park, Texas?

University Park is a fully incorporated city of 2.3 square miles tucked inside Dallas — surrounded by Dallas proper on nearly every border, with Highland Park as its immediate neighbor to the south and west. It sits about 6 miles north of downtown Dallas via US-75 (Central Expressway), making it one of the closest true municipalities to the Dallas central business district in all of DFW. Together with Highland Park, it forms what locals call the Park Cities — a distinct, self-governed enclave with its own police, fire department, and city services operating independently of the City of Dallas.

Geographically, University Park is bounded by Lovers Lane on the south, Northwest Highway (Loop 12) on the north, the Dallas North Tollway on the west, and Greenville Avenue / US-75 on the east. Southern Methodist University occupies the southeast corner of the city, giving the neighborhood its name and adding significant cultural depth. Getting around relies on US-75 for regional access and Preston Road or Hillcrest Avenue for north-south movement within the Park Cities. There is no DART light rail in University Park, but DART bus service does run through the area.

Transit reality: University Park has DART bus service but no light rail connection — and that's unlikely to change in the near term. You'll need a car (or two) for daily life. The good news is that UP's location inside Dallas puts most of DFW's major employment within 30 minutes by car — a commute advantage most northern suburbs simply can't match. Many residents who previously lived in Frisco or McKinney are surprised by how much shorter their drive is after moving to the Park Cities.
Education

Highland Park ISD — Schools Deep Dive

Every family that moves to University Park is making the same calculation: the premium is real, but so is the school district. Highland Park ISD serves both University Park and neighboring Highland Park — and in the 2025 TEA accountability cycle, HPISD earned a score of 96 out of 100 and an "A" rating, ranking #1 among all 5A/6A public school districts in Texas. There is one high school for the entire district (Highland Park High School), which means your kids join the same community regardless of which street in University Park they live on. That single-school structure creates an unusually tight-knit, multi-generational community feel that's rare in DFW.

8 Campuses

HPISD operates 8 campuses serving approximately 6,500 students — an unusually small district by Texas standards, which keeps class sizes manageable and parent involvement high.

One High School

Highland Park High School is the only high school in HPISD — all students from both University Park and Highland Park attend together, creating one of the most cohesive school communities in North Texas.

Verify Your Feeder

University Park streets feed to Bradfield, Hyer, or University Park Elementary depending on exact address. All then flow to McCulloch Intermediate → HP Middle → HP High. Always verify at hpisd.net before making an offer.

Where to Live

Best Neighborhoods in University Park for Families

University Park is a compact 2.3-square-mile city, so you're not choosing between master-planned communities with HOA amenity centers — you're choosing streets, tree canopy, proximity to parks, and walkability to Snider Plaza or SMU. The character across most of UP is consistent: tree-lined sidewalks, well-maintained older homes (many from the 1930s–1970s), and quiet residential blocks that feel genuinely neighborly. Price and lot size vary more by micro-location than by named subdivision. Here are the areas that families most often focus on:

$1.5M–$3.5M · Established Walkable

Snider Plaza Corridor

The most walkable part of UP — streets around the Hillcrest and Lovers Lane intersection put you a short walk from Snider Plaza's 100+ shops, coffee, and restaurants. Homes here tend to be 1940s–1970s traditional-style with mature trees and generous lots by Park Cities standards. The blend of daily errands on foot and outstanding school access makes this the entry point for many Park Cities buyers.

Turtle Creek Corridor

The streets along Turtle Creek Boulevard and the northern belt of UP represent the most prestigious addresses in University Park — larger lots, older established homes (many completely rebuilt as high-end new construction), and direct access to Caruth Park and the Turtle Creek walking paths. If you want the most established feel and the largest footprints, this is where to look. Turnover is low and competition is high.

Northwest UP / Lovers Lane West

The northwest quadrant of University Park between Lovers Lane and Northwest Highway offers some of the most accessible price points in the city. This is where many Park Cities buyers get their start.

$700K–$2M · Urban-Adjacent

SMU / Southeast University Park

The southeast corner of UP borders SMU's campus and offers the city's most urban character — higher walkability, proximity to Mockingbird Lane's dining corridor, and a mix of older single-family homes and the city's limited condo supply. Condos here can represent an entry point below $1M, while single-family homes range from starter Park Cities prices up to the mid-millions. Graduate students, young professionals, and families all share these blocks.

New Construction Pockets

Throughout University Park, older homes are being torn down and replaced by architect-designed modern builds on the same lots. These infill projects are scattered across every neighborhood and offer smart-home technology, contemporary layouts, and premium finishes on parcels that would cost twice as much to replicate in Highland Park. If you want modern construction in the Park Cities, this is where to look.

Mid-Rise & Condo Options

University Park has a limited but meaningful condo market — roughly 21 condo sales per year — concentrated near the SMU area and along Lovers Lane. These are not large high-rise towers; they're smaller multi-story buildings that offer Park Cities access at a lower price point than single-family. For empty nesters downsizing from a larger home, or relocating buyers who want HPISD schools without a $2M+ entry price, this segment is worth exploring carefully.

Cost of Living

University Park vs. Other High-End Markets

University Park is not a "cheaper than California" story — it's an elite-market story. The $2.76M median puts it squarely in luxury territory alongside Southlake and Highland Park. What it offers that equivalent-dollar markets in California, New York, or Chicago can't match: the same prestigious school district as its more expensive neighbor Highland Park, a genuine walkable neighborhood feel, and Texas's complete absence of state income tax. For high-income families relocating from coastal markets, the after-tax math is compelling even at this price point.

The income tax calculation: A family earning $500,000 in California pays approximately $47,000+ in state income tax. In Texas, that's $0. Even with University Park's ~$43K property tax bill at the median price, a high-earning California family often comes out even or ahead — while getting a walkable neighborhood and the #1-rated public school district in Texas. The math doesn't always work at every income level, but for executive-level relocators, it frequently does.
