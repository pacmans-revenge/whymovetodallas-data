Getting Your Bearings

Where Is University Park, Texas?

University Park is a fully incorporated city of 2.3 square miles tucked inside Dallas — surrounded by Dallas proper on nearly every border, with Highland Park as its immediate neighbor to the south and west. It sits about 6 miles north of downtown Dallas via US-75 (Central Expressway), making it one of the closest true municipalities to the Dallas central business district in all of DFW. Together with Highland Park, it forms what locals call the Park Cities — a distinct, self-governed enclave with its own police, fire department, and city services operating independently of the City of Dallas.

Geographically, University Park is bounded by Lovers Lane on the south, Northwest Highway (Loop 12) on the north, the Dallas North Tollway on the west, and Greenville Avenue / US-75 on the east. Southern Methodist University occupies the southeast corner of the city, giving the neighborhood its name and adding significant cultural depth. Getting around relies on US-75 for regional access and Preston Road or Hillcrest Avenue for north-south movement within the Park Cities. There is no DART light rail in University Park, but DART bus service does run through the area.

Many residents who previously lived in Frisco or McKinney are surprised by how much shorter their drive is after moving to the Park Cities.
Education

Highland Park ISD — Schools Deep Dive

Every family that moves to University Park is making the same calculation: the premium is real, but so is the school district. Highland Park ISD serves both University Park and neighboring Highland Park — and in the 2025 TEA accountability cycle, HPISD earned a score of 96 out of 100 and an "A" rating, ranking #1 among all 5A/6A public school districts in Texas. There is one high school for the entire district (Highland Park High School), which means your kids join the same community regardless of which street in University Park they live on. That single-school structure creates an unusually tight-knit, multi-generational community feel that's rare in DFW.

The character across most of UP is consistent: tree-lined sidewalks, well-maintained older homes (many from the 1930s–1970s), and quiet residential blocks that feel genuinely neighborly. Price and lot size vary more by micro-location than by named subdivision. Here are the areas that families most often focus on:

University Park vs. Other High-End Markets

University Park is not a "cheaper than California" story — it's an elite-market story. The $2.76M median puts it squarely in luxury territory alongside Southlake and Highland Park. What it offers that equivalent-dollar markets in California, New York, or Chicago can't match: the same prestigious school district as its more expensive neighbor Highland Park, a genuine walkable neighborhood feel, and Texas's complete absence of state income tax. For high-income families relocating from coastal markets, the after-tax math is compelling even at this price point.

Family Lifestyle in University Park

University Park lives like a small, tight-knit town with genuine big-city access. The walkability to Snider Plaza, the SMU arts calendar, and the proximity to Turtle Creek make daily life feel distinctly different from the suburban master-planned communities further north — more urban park, less pool-and-amenity-center.

Snider Plaza & Walkable Retail

The Hillcrest and Lovers Lane intersection anchors daily walkable life — 100+ locally owned shops, coffee roasters, wine bars, and restaurants. Weekend mornings at Snider Plaza feel like a neighborhood ritual. No car needed for errands if you live nearby.

SMU Arts & Events

Southern Methodist University's Meadows Museum (one of the largest Spanish art collections outside Spain), the Maguire Theatre, and Moody Performance Hall are steps from residential streets. Lecture series, student performances, and the Dallas Symphony campus events are part of the fabric here.

Turtle Creek & Park Access

Caruth Park borders the northern edge of UP and feeds into the Turtle Creek walking and biking corridor — a linear park that extends into Highland Park and the Katy Trail. Families use it for morning runs, weekend bike rides, and dog walks. No membership required.

Community Events Calendar

University Park runs an active city events calendar — the Yule Tide Christmas Parade, Park Cities Historic and Cultural Society events, Caruth Park summer concerts, and HPISD school traditions (the Dell Diamond game, Homecoming on the field) give the year genuine structure. This feels like a town, not a neighborhood.

Dining Scene

Preston Center and the Lovers Lane / Greenville Avenue corridors anchor a dining scene that goes well beyond suburban strip-center options. José, Beverley's, Uchi Dallas, Nick & Sam's, and The Rustic are all within a short drive. Snider Plaza itself holds long-standing local favorites — it's where UP families eat on school nights.

Private Clubs & Recreation

The Dallas Country Club (one of Texas's most historic), Brook Hollow Golf Club, and the University Park Recreation Center give families multiple options. The rec center offers pools, fitness facilities, and youth programs without requiring country club membership — an underrated amenity for new residents still finding their footing.

Honest tradeoff: University Park's lifestyle is genuinely urban-adjacent — which is exactly what many Park Cities buyers want, but can surprise families expecting the pool-and-playground amenity model of Frisco or Flower Mound. There's no community rec center with lazy rivers and sports courts. The "amenity" is the city itself — walkable errands, neighbor relationships on tree-lined streets, SMU's campus next door. If that's your style, UP is exceptional. If you want organized HOA amenities, look north.

Parks, Trails & Recreation

University Park is one of the most walkable communities in all of DFW — a rarity in a metro known for car dependency. Its 29 parks within 2.3 square miles, the Turtle Creek trail corridor, and proximity to the Katy Trail create a park-access density that no master-planned suburb can match.

Parks & Trails

Caruth Park

University Park's signature green space — a 10-acre park along Turtle Creek Boulevard with open meadows, mature oaks, a playground, and easy connection to the Turtle Creek trail. Used daily by residents for dog walks, soccer kicks, and weekend picnics. The most beloved park in the city.

Goar Park

A central neighborhood park with athletic fields, a playground, and picnic facilities. Used for youth baseball, soccer, and community gatherings. Located in the heart of the city, within walking distance for most UP families.

Turtle Creek Trail Corridor

A linear greenbelt connecting University Park and Highland Park along Turtle Creek — extending north to the Katy Trail, one of Dallas's premier urban running and biking paths. Families use this for morning runs, weekend bike rides, and connecting to wider Dallas trail network without a car.

Burleson Park & Neighborhood Parks

University Park maintains 29 parks spread across its 2.3 square miles — an extraordinary density. Most residential blocks are within two or three streets of a park. Burleson Park on the south side, Williams Park near SMU, and a dozen pocket parks round out the system. There's usually a park near wherever you land.

Safety & Crime Rates

University Park maintains its own fully-staffed police department as an independent city — one of the advantages of its unique municipal structure. Response times are among the fastest in the Dallas metro, and the UPPD is a frequent presence on residential streets. The city's safety profile reflects both its socioeconomic character and the attentiveness of a small, independent police force.

Pros & Cons of University Park

University Park is exceptional at what it does — and it does very specific things. Knowing what it's not is as important as knowing what it is.

The Pros

  • Highland Park ISD — one of the best districts in Texas. A+ Niche grade, A TEA rating (96/100), 97%+ college-going rate, exceptional fine arts and athletics. HPISD is the reason most families move to the Park Cities specifically.
  • True walkability — rare in DFW. Walk Score 61, Bike Score 55. Snider Plaza, Caruth Park, SMU campus — all reachable on foot or by bike from most addresses. This level of walkability is nearly unique in the Dallas metro.
  • Independent city with own services. University Park has its own police, fire, public works, and parks departments. Streets are well-maintained, response times are fast, and city government is responsive because it's small. This is what $230/100 in city taxes buys.
  • Park density is extraordinary. 29 parks in 2.3 square miles — more park access per household than almost anywhere in DFW. Combined with the Turtle Creek and Katy Trail corridors, outdoor access is genuinely excellent.
  • SMU next door as a cultural amenity. Meadows Museum, Maguire Theatre, SMU athletics, free campus walking — the university is a genuine neighborhood amenity for arts, continuing education, and events.
  • Strong community identity. The Park Cities have a distinct, tight-knit identity that residents are proud of. School events, neighborhood associations, and civic traditions give it a small-town feel despite being inside a major metro.
  • No state income tax. Texas has zero state income tax — relevant for high-income households relocating from California, New York, or Illinois.

The Cons

  • $2.76M median is the entry for most families. This isn't a "starter suburb" — it's one of the most expensive zip codes in Texas. Budget $1.5M+ for a move-in-ready home with reasonable square footage, more like $2M–$4M for anything considered typical for the market.
  • Very limited inventory. University Park is built out — no new development land, 179 closed SFR sales in a full year, months supply of 2.6. Competition for good homes is real and fast-moving. If you need to be in UP specifically, plan to move quickly.
  • No master-planned community amenities. No community pools, splash pads, amenity centers, or HOA-organized social events. The lifestyle is urban-adjacent, not suburban-amenity. Families expecting a Frisco or Flower Mound recreation model will be surprised.
  • Property taxes are high in dollar terms. The ~1.64% combined rate on a $2.76M home works out to ~$45K/year before exemptions. The homestead exemption reduces this meaningfully, but the dollar amount is significant regardless of the rate.
  • Traffic on key corridors. Lovers Lane, Northwest Highway, and Hillcrest Ave carry heavy traffic during peak hours. The city's small size works against you on east-west routes — there aren't many alternatives. Budget extra time during school dropoff windows.
  • Competitive school culture. HP High's academic and athletic culture is competitive in the best sense — and in the stressful sense. Families moving in during high school years should be prepared for a high-performance environment that not every student thrives in.

Frequently Asked Questions — University Park, TX

No — University Park and Highland Park are two separate, independent cities that together make up the "Park Cities," an enclave completely surrounded by the City of Dallas. They share one school district (Highland Park ISD) and a similar high-income character, but they're distinct municipalities with separate city governments, police departments, and tax rates. HP sits in the south, UP in the north, with SMU's campus as a rough dividing landmark. Real estate agents and school enrollment staff treat them as distinct — make sure you're clear on which city a specific home address falls in.

Highland Park ISD serves both University Park and Highland Park — a combined population of roughly 50,000 — with one high school (HP High), two middle schools, and six elementary schools. It consistently earns TEA "A" ratings (96/100), top Niche grades (A+), and 97%+ college-going rates. The district spends well above the state average per pupil, has low student-to-teacher ratios, and offers exceptional fine arts, athletics, and AP programming. The district's size (one high school) also creates a community cohesion that larger ISDs can't replicate — every family shares one HP High experience. This is the single biggest reason families pay Park Cities prices.

The combined property tax rate in University Park is approximately $1.64 per $100 of assessed value (2024 certified rates), broken down as: Highland Park ISD ~$0.8669, City of University Park ~$0.23, Dallas County ~$0.2167, Parkland Hospital District ~$0.2337, and Dallas County Community College ~$0.0966. On a $2.76M home, that's roughly $45,000/year before the homestead exemption. The Texas homestead exemption (including HPISD's $100K exemption) reduces your taxable value — typical savings are $870–$1,460/year. University Park has no MUD, PID, or HOA fees.

The median single-family sale price in University Park is approximately $2.76M based on trailing 12-month NTREIS data. Entry-level homes (typically older, smaller footprints) start around $1.2M–$1.5M, while the mid-market runs $2M–$4M, and trophy properties on premium lots or with full rebuilds can reach $6M–$10M+. The condo and townhome market offers a lower entry point — median around $663K — for buyers seeking HPISD access without the SFR price tag. Inventory is limited (179 SFR closed sales in a full year), so competition for well-priced homes is real.

University Park is 12–15 minutes from downtown Dallas by car — one of the shortest suburban commutes in DFW. The Mockingbird Lane / Central Expressway route is the most common, though traffic on the Mockingbird corridor can add 5–10 minutes during morning peak hours. UT Southwestern and Parkland Medical Center on Harry Hines Boulevard are a direct 10-minute drive via Lovers Lane. Love Field Airport is about 10 minutes via Mockingbird. For everything north — Plano/Legacy Drive, Frisco/Legacy West — budget 25–35 minutes. The UP location puts you much closer to central Dallas employers than any suburb north of LBJ Freeway.

By DFW standards, yes — Walk Score 61 (Somewhat Walkable), Bike Score 55. Homes near Snider Plaza in the Hillcrest/Lovers Lane corridor can walk to coffee, restaurants, dry cleaning, and groceries. SMU's campus is walkable from much of the city. Caruth Park and the Turtle Creek trail are accessible on foot or bike from most addresses. That said, a car is still needed for the majority of errands — UP is walkable relative to DFW, not relative to a Northeastern city. Families who value being able to walk somewhere have real options here that most DFW suburbs can't offer.

University Park has 29 parks within its 2.3 square miles — an extraordinary density for a DFW city. Caruth Park is the signature green space, with meadows, mature trees, a playground, and access to the Turtle Creek trail. Goar Park has athletic fields for youth leagues. The Turtle Creek trail corridor connects to the Katy Trail, one of Dallas's premier running and biking paths. Most residential blocks are within 2–3 streets of a park. This park density is one of the underrated advantages of UP compared to larger suburbs that feel park-poor relative to their population.

University Park's price range overlaps with Bay Area and coastal LA markets, so it's not the "buy more for less" story that Frisco or Flower Mound represents. However, families coming from Palo Alto, San Francisco proper, or Manhattan often find that $2M–$3M in UP buys meaningfully more home than the same budget would in their origin market — and comes with no state income tax, lower cost of living for everyday expenses, and a school district that rivals any private school in the country. The comparison isn't "UP is cheaper than California" — it's "UP gives you everything you're looking for, and the total cost of living is substantially lower." For households earning $400K–$800K/year, the math often works very well.

Very competitive. University Park has 2.6 months of supply (seller's market territory), 36-day average days on market, and only 179 SFR closed sales in a full year. Correctly priced homes attract multiple offers. New listings are often previewed by agents before hitting MLS. Cash buyers and buyers with large down payments are common. If you're serious about University Park, you need to be pre-approved, ready to move quickly, and working with an agent who has relationships in the Park Cities market. Don't assume you have weeks to deliberate — good homes move in days.
