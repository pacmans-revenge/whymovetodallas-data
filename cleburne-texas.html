Getting Your Bearings

Where Is Cleburne, Texas?

Cleburne is the county seat of Johnson County, located about 33 miles south of downtown Fort Worth and 51 miles southwest of Dallas. It sits at the southern end of the Chisholm Trail Parkway (CTP), a NTTA-operated toll road that connects directly to I-30 in Fort Worth's central business district. US-67 runs through the center of town and provides a free alternative route north through Burleson to Fort Worth. SH-174 connects Cleburne southeast toward Hillsboro and I-35W, while SH-171 heads northeast. The city is geographically in the rolling Cross Timbers landscape — more trees, more elevation change, more rural feel than the flat prairie suburbs north of Dallas.

The Chisholm Trail Parkway is Cleburne's single biggest commuter advantage. For families with a Fort Worth employer — Lockheed Martin, American Airlines, Cook Children's Medical Center, Bell Textron — the drive is 33 miles and about 35–40 minutes with a TollTag during off-peak hours. US-67 is free but adds 20–35 minutes through traffic lights and school zones in Burleson and Crowley. Dallas is a longer haul — 51 miles via US-67 — making Cleburne a strong fit for Fort Worth-centric households but not ideal if both spouses commute to different parts of the metro.

Transit reality: Cleburne has no DART rail, no DCTA service, and no meaningful public transit connection to Fort Worth or Dallas. The Chisholm Trail Parkway is excellent for Fort Worth commuters but it's a toll road — budget ~$6/day roundtrip with a TollTag, or $12+ without one. US-67 is free but significantly slower. If your job is in North Dallas, Plano, or Frisco, Cleburne's distance is a real trade-off to weigh carefully against the home price advantage.
Education

Cleburne ISD — Schools Deep Dive

Cleburne ISD is a solid, mid-tier district for families relocating from out of state — not the elite performance of Frisco or Carroll ISD, but a well-managed district with a 94% graduation rate, genuine STEAM programming, and seven campuses that earned A or B ratings in the 2024–2025 TEA accountability cycle. For families prioritizing affordability over maximizing school rankings, Cleburne ISD offers real educational value at a fraction of what you'd spend on housing in a top-rated North DFW district. The honest caveat: test score proficiency (35–46%) lags behind upper-tier districts, and feeder pattern verification before making an offer is essential — zone boundaries matter here.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Cleburne

John Warren Sports Complex

The nearly 90-acre sports complex is Cleburne's youth sports hub — baseball, softball, soccer, and football fields all in one location. Weekend tournaments bring families from across Johnson County together. ISD athletics programs are competitive at the regional level, and rec leagues run year-round through the city's parks department.

Cleburne State Park & Lake Pat

Cleburne has something most DFW suburbs can't claim: a genuine Texas State Park within city limits. Cedar Lake at Cleburne State Park is a shaded, peaceful escape — hiking trails, fishing, and camping without a long drive. Lake Pat Cleburne adds 1,500 acres of reservoir for boating, bass fishing, and weekend paddling. This level of outdoor access at these price points is rare in the metro.

Dining & Everyday Shopping

Cleburne ranks #12 of 65 DFW suburbs for restaurants per resident — a genuine surprise given the city's size. HEB anchors the grocery landscape, and the Walmart Supercenter covers big-box needs. Downtown Cleburne has a small but real dining scene: Texas Roadhouse, local BBQ, Mexican food staples, and a handful of local lunch spots. Major retail is available locally; specialty shopping means a drive to Fort Worth or Burleson.

Community Events & Small-City Culture

Cleburne hosts the Annual Chisholm Trail Round-Up (one of Texas's longest-running rodeos), weekly farmer's markets in season, and Fourth of July festivities that draw families from across Johnson County. The Layland Museum offers local history programs. Life here has a small-city calendar — not a packed suburban events schedule, but a genuine community identity that some families specifically move here to find.

The RV Capital of Texas

Fun Town RV, one of Texas's largest RV dealerships, calls Cleburne home. That's not just a quirky local fact — it signals something about the community's character: outdoor recreation, family camping, and a lifestyle that prioritizes experiences over status. If your family loves weekend camping trips and outdoor adventure, the culture here matches. The Chisholm Trail's western heritage runs deep in how residents see themselves.

Tight-Knit & Genuinely Welcoming

Cleburne still has the feel of a place where neighbors know each other. The city's size — about 33,000 residents — means you're not anonymous. School sports, church, and neighborhood events are the social glue here. Families who've moved from larger cities often comment that it took them by surprise how quickly they felt at home. The community is conservative, faith-centered, and genuinely hospitable to newcomers who engage.

Honest tradeoff: Cleburne doesn't have the curated restaurant rows of Frisco's Hall Park or the dense suburban activity infrastructure of Southlake. The nightlife scene is limited — Niche gives it a B+ rating, but that reflects the city's bar-to-resident ratio, not a sophisticated dining culture. If you're coming from a walkable urban neighborhood in California and expecting to replicate that lifestyle, Cleburne will be an adjustment. The trade you're making is lower cost, more space, outdoor access, and a slower pace — and for the right family, that's not a sacrifice at all.
Getting Outside

Recreation, Walkability & City Amenities

Cleburne is a car-dependent city — that's the honest starting point. A personal vehicle is not optional here; it's essential. What makes Cleburne different from other car-dependent DFW suburbs is the quality of what you're driving to: a genuine Texas State Park, a 1,500-acre reservoir, 24 city parks, and a large multi-sport complex. Outdoor recreation in Cleburne is legitimately strong for the price point. Urban walkability is not.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Cleburne has 24 parks mapped within city limits — a solid baseline for a city of 33,000. More importantly, the quality of the anchor parks is exceptional for the price tier. Cleburne State Park and Lake Pat Cleburne offer experiences you simply can't find within city limits of most DFW suburbs at any price. For families who spend weekends outdoors, this is a meaningful quality-of-life factor that doesn't show up in any Niche grade.

Cleburne State Park

A Texas State Park with Cedar Lake at its center — shaded hiking trails, fishing piers, swimming, and camping. It's a genuine outdoor escape accessible without leaving the city. Families relocating from the coasts are often stunned that something like this exists inside a DFW-area city.

Lake Pat Cleburne

A 1,500-acre reservoir managed by the City of Cleburne for fishing, boating, and recreation. Bass fishing is serious here — tournaments run year-round. The lake also provides some of Cleburne's most distinctive real estate, with lakefront and lake-view homes at prices that would be unimaginable near DFW's other metro-area lakes.

John Warren Sports Complex

Nearly 90 acres of youth sports infrastructure — baseball diamonds, softball fields, soccer pitches, and football fields. This is where most of Cleburne's organized youth athletics happen, and it's one of the best facilities in Johnson County. Weekend tournaments are a regular feature from spring through fall.

Hulen Park

One of the city's primary community parks with playground equipment, picnic areas, and open green space. Well-maintained and centrally located. A reliable neighborhood park for younger children when you don't want to drive to the state park.

McGregor Park

A neighborhood park on the east side offering sports courts, walking paths, and green space. Popular with local families for weekend activities and informal pickup sports. Anchors the recreational infrastructure on Cleburne's more recently developed east side.

Splash Station

Cleburne's city-operated waterpark with slides, a pool, and a spray pad. Open seasonally from late May through August. It's the go-to summer destination for families with young children — affordable, close, and consistently popular on hot Texas afternoons.

Crime & Safety

How Safe Is Cleburne?

Cleburne's crime picture is mixed. The violent crime rate (3.77 per 1,000) is roughly comparable to the U.S. national average of 4.0 — not alarming, but not the near-zero rates you'd see in Southlake or Colleyville. Property crime is the bigger concern: 18.99 per 1,000 is above the national average, which places Cleburne in the lower tier for safety among DFW suburbs. CrimeGrade.org rates Cleburne at D+ overall and puts it in the 28th percentile for safety. The southeast part of town is consistently cited by residents as the safest area. Most established residential neighborhoods — Chisholm Hills, Nolan River Estates, the lake area — have a comfortable, safe feel day-to-day. The data reflects city-wide averages that include commercial corridors and higher-crime pockets on the north side.

Pros & Cons of Living in Cleburne

I won't sell you on Cleburne. I'll give you the real picture so you can decide if it's right for your family.

✓ What Cleburne Does Well

  • DFW's affordability leader — $288K median SFR with an Affordability Score of 88/100. This is the lowest price point of any DFW suburb with direct highway access to a major employment center.
  • Chisholm Trail Parkway access — Direct CTP connection to downtown Fort Worth in 35–40 min. For families with Fort Worth employers, this is genuinely competitive with most North DFW suburbs.
  • Cleburne State Park + Lake Pat Cleburne — Exceptional outdoor access for the price tier. A Texas State Park and 1,500-acre reservoir within city limits is rare in the DFW metro.
  • Authentic small-city character — 33,000 residents, historic downtown, genuine community identity. Not a subdivision that looks like every other DFW suburb.
  • Low competition for desirable homes — 5.1 months supply in a balanced market means you have time to make thoughtful decisions without waiving contingencies or overbidding.
  • No state income tax — Meaningful savings, especially for high earners relocating from CA, NY, or IL.

— What to Weigh Carefully

  • Safety is below DFW average — D+ from CrimeGrade (28th percentile). Property crime runs higher than most North DFW suburbs. Neighborhood selection matters — southeast Cleburne is significantly safer than the north side.
  • Schools are solid but not top-tier — Cleburne ISD rates B- overall. For families where a top-rated ISD (A/A+) is non-negotiable, North DFW suburbs like Frisco, Allen, or Prosper offer better ratings at higher price points.
  • Long commute to Dallas / North DFW — If your job is in Plano, Frisco, or downtown Dallas, plan for 55–85 miles and 60–90 minutes each way. This is a Fort Worth suburb, not a Dallas suburb.
  • Car-dependent — No meaningful public transit. A personal vehicle is essential. Not ideal if you're accustomed to public transportation.
  • Limited private school options — Fewer choices than larger North DFW suburbs. Primary private options include Cleburne Adventist Christian School and Cleburne Christian Academy.
  • Newer suburb amenity gap — The polished restaurant/retail/entertainment infrastructure of Frisco or Southlake doesn't exist here. Specialty dining and upscale shopping mean a drive.

Cleburne, Texas — Frequently Asked Questions

Cleburne is a strong fit for families who prioritize affordability, outdoor access, and a tight-knit small-city community over top-ranked schools and polished suburban amenities. With a $288K median home price, Cleburne State Park, Lake Pat Cleburne, and direct CTP access to Fort Worth, it offers a quality of life that's hard to match at this price point in the DFW metro. Niche rates the city a B overall for families. It's not Southlake — but for many families, it's genuinely the better choice.

Cleburne ISD earns a B- overall from Niche and serves approximately 6,900 students across 11 campuses. The district has a 94% graduation rate, a B+ college-prep rating, and seven campuses that earned A or B ratings in the 2024–2025 TEA accountability cycle. STEAM programs operate at elementary and middle school levels, and dual credit partnerships with Hill College are available to high school students. Gerard Elementary is the district's highest-rated campus at 7/10 on GreatSchools. Always verify the feeder pattern for any specific address before making an offer.

The combined base property tax rate in Cleburne is approximately $2.2537 per $100 of assessed value (2024 certified). On the $288K median home, that works out to roughly $6,490 per year. The rate includes Cleburne ISD ($1.2119), City of Cleburne ($0.6125), Johnson County ($0.3293), Johnson County Lateral Road ($0.0500), and Hill College ($0.0500).

New construction communities may carry additional MUD or PID assessments of $0.25–$0.75 per $100 — always verify the full rate for any specific address before closing. Apply for the Texas Homestead Exemption through Johnson County CAD after closing to reduce your taxable value by at least $100,000 for the school district portion.

The financial case for Cleburne is compelling if you're coming from California or New York. Texas has no state income tax (vs. 9.3%+ in California), and Cleburne's $288K median home price is a fraction of what you'd pay in a comparable Bay Area or NY Metro suburb — often $1M+ less. That means a dramatically lower mortgage payment, no state income tax, and a larger home on a larger lot.

The honest trade-offs: Cleburne's property taxes (~2.25%) are higher than California's Prop 13-capped rates, the school district rates B- rather than A+, and the cultural amenities don't compare to major metros. But most California families I work with who move to Cleburne save $20,000–$30,000+ per year in combined housing and tax costs. That's a meaningful change in how your family lives financially.

The neighborhoods I walk families through most often are: Chisholm Hills (established, mature trees, good CTP access, $250K–$375K); Lake Pat Cleburne area (waterfront access, larger lots, $280K–$500K); Nolan River Estates (tree-lined, parks nearby, $280K–$410K); Courtland Place/Legado (new construction, modern floor plans, $290K–$460K); Cooper Valley (entry-level, solid value, $225K–$330K); and the Old Town area (historic character, walkable to downtown, $189K–$280K). Neighborhood choice should be driven by school zone verification first — feeder patterns matter in Cleburne ISD.

Renting first makes sense in Cleburne specifically because school feeder patterns matter — a few blocks can determine which elementary school your kids attend, and the campuses vary meaningfully. With a balanced market (5.1 months supply) and 60-day median DOM, you're not in a situation where you must buy immediately to compete. I generally advise families relocating from out of state to rent for 3–6 months when possible, especially if they haven't visited Cleburne multiple times. It also gives you time to calibrate which neighborhoods feel right for your family's lifestyle.

Cleburne's violent crime rate (3.77 per 1,000) is close to the U.S. national average of 4.0 — not alarming, but higher than most North DFW suburbs. Property crime is more of a concern at 18.99 per 1,000, placing Cleburne in the 28th percentile for safety nationally (D+ from CrimeGrade.org). The southeast part of town is consistently considered the safest by residents. Most established family neighborhoods — Chisholm Hills, the lake area, Nolan River Estates — feel comfortable and safe day-to-day. The city-wide averages include commercial corridors and pockets on the north side that pull the numbers up. As with any city, neighborhood selection matters significantly.

The local economy skews toward manufacturing, distribution, and healthcare.

Cleburne works well for Fort Worth employers; it's a long haul to Plano, Frisco, or Las Colinas.

Traffic within Cleburne is light — the city is small enough that internal congestion isn't a real issue. The commute story is about the two routes north: the Chisholm Trail Parkway (CTP) is a 27.6-mile toll road that connects directly to downtown Fort Worth at I-30. With a TollTag, the full trip costs about $6 each way — $12/day roundtrip. It runs freely at highway speeds during most hours; Fort Worth rush hour can add 15–20 minutes.

US-67 is the free alternative but adds 20–35 minutes through Burleson traffic lights and school zones. Dallas-bound commuters take US-67 north — a slower, toll-free route through Mansfield and Grand Prairie. Bottom line: if your employer is in Fort Worth, traffic is manageable. If it's in North Dallas, Plano, or Frisco, the daily drive will be a real factor in your quality of life.
