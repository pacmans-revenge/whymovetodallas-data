Where Is Crandall?

Where Is Crandall, Texas?

Crandall sits in Kaufman County, roughly 27 miles southeast of Downtown Dallas along the US-175 corridor. It's positioned between Interstate 20 to the south and US Highway 175 — two direct routes into the heart of Dallas that make the drive feel less painful than the raw mileage suggests. The city itself has just a handful of intersections, but the broader Crandall area — including Heartland and Wildcat Ranch — has been exploding. Developers have announced plans for more than 30,000 new homes in and around Crandall by the end of the decade, making this one of the fastest-transforming zip codes in the metro.

The southeast position is a genuine trade-off that families need to price in. Most DFW employment corridors — Plano/Legacy West, Las Colinas, DFW Airport, Frisco/The Star — are on the north and west sides of the metro. From Crandall, you're essentially driving through the city to reach them, which means real exposure to I-20 and US-175 rush hour traffic. The sweet spot here is for families where at least one partner works in Dallas proper, in Mesquite, or works remotely. If both adults have long drives to the northwest suburbs, account for that commute reality before you fall in love with the new construction prices.

This is true of most DFW suburbs, but it's worth stating plainly here because Crandall is more isolated than many.
Education

Schools in Crandall, Texas

Every family considering Crandall needs to have an honest conversation about Crandall ISD before signing a purchase contract. The district serves all of Crandall and the master-planned communities — there is no private school or charter escape hatch nearby. Crandall ISD received a D rating (score: 69/100) from the Texas Education Agency in its most recent accountability cycle, with 26% of students proficient in math and 44% in reading. Those are below state averages, and they're numbers worth taking seriously.

The Niche composite picture is somewhat brighter — C+ overall, with a B- in academics and a B+ in college prep — reflecting a 96% graduation rate and a student body that increasingly includes families from higher-income households moving into the master-planned communities. The district has also been building new campuses and adding programs as enrollment grows rapidly from ~4,500 to nearly 7,000 students. The trajectory is upward, and the district knows it's in a race between new families moving in and infrastructure catching up. Whether it's there yet is a judgment call every family has to make for themselves.

Where to Live

Best Neighborhoods in Crandall for Families

Crandall's residential story is really two stories: the master-planned communities that have transformed the area over the past decade, and the older, quieter established sections of the city. Heartland and Wildcat Ranch are where most relocating families land — and for good reason. Both offer amenities at a price point that doesn't exist anywhere else in DFW. If you're moving from California and used to neighborhood pools, trails, and clubhouses, these communities will feel surprisingly familiar.

$270K – $610K

The flagship master-planned community in Crandall — 2,000 acres with 400 dedicated to open space, a 35-acre stocked lake with fishing pier, resort pools, waterpark, fitness center, and community event programming year-round. The community's sense of organized activity — HOA events, movie nights, pumpkin patches, summer sports leagues — creates an unusually tight-knit feel for a development this large. HOA: $552/year.

Family Life

What Life Actually Looks Like in Crandall

Athletics & Youth Sports

Crandall High's Bulldogs carry a serious athletic tradition in the Kaufman County community — football and basketball games are real community events. At the youth level, CAAYSA (Crandall Area Athletic Youth Sports Association) runs recreational leagues in baseball, softball, soccer, and basketball. Both Heartland and Wildcat Ranch add their own HOA-organized sports programs, meaning kids in the master-planned communities often have organized recreation within walking distance of home.

Lakes, Trails & Outdoors

Heartland's 35-acre stocked lake is the outdoor centerpiece of the Crandall community — families fish from the pier, kayak on weekends, and let kids explore the creek paths. The Wildcat Ranch Trail loops 4.2 miles along Buffalo Creek. For bigger adventures, Cedar Creek Lake is about 30 minutes east — one of the best boating and fishing lakes in the region. John Bunker Sands Wetland Center (40 min) is a standout for families who like birdwatching and nature walks with kids.

Shopping & Dining

Be honest with yourself here: Crandall proper has limited restaurant and retail options. Most families drive 10–15 minutes to Forney for the Chick-fil-A, Whataburger, Walmart, Home Depot, and the growing restaurant strip on US-80. Kaufman and Terrell have additional options. For specialty grocers, Target, or a real mall, you're heading toward Mesquite or Dallas. Families who accept this as a normal part of suburban DFW life adapt quickly. Families who want walkable dining will struggle.

Community Events

Heartland's HOA runs a genuine community calendar — pumpkin patches in fall, family movie nights at Heartland Hall, Fourth of July festivities, summer sports leagues, and spring festivals. Wildcat Ranch follows a similar model. The Crandall city events calendar is quieter, but Friday night football at Crandall High is a genuine community ritual that unites residents across the neighborhoods. Families moving from isolated California suburbs often remark that the community connection here feels surprisingly real.

The Rare Combination

New construction. Master-planned amenities. Sub-$300K pricing. That combination has essentially vanished from Frisco, Prosper, Allen, and McKinney. In Crandall, it still exists. Buyers from California are often genuinely disoriented — a 4-bedroom in Heartland with a community pool, trails, and a lake view that would cost $1.3M in the Bay Area sells for $320K here. If value is your top priority and you're flexible on school performance metrics, Crandall offers something you won't find anywhere else in DFW at this price.

Neighbor-First Culture

One consistent thing families report after moving to Heartland or Wildcat Ranch: they met their neighbors within a week. The HOA-organized events and the community app culture means the social infrastructure is built in. Kids are outside on bikes. Adults gather at the amenity center and the lake. It's the kind of "know your neighbors" energy that larger suburbs have lost. For families coming from California or the Northeast who want community more than they want nightlife, this aspect consistently surprises people in a good way.

Honest tradeoff: Crandall has almost no standalone dining or nightlife within the city limits. If a Friday night spontaneously becoming dinner and a movie within 5 minutes of home matters to you, plan a 15-minute drive to Forney at minimum. This is a non-issue for most families who've settled into suburban DFW life, but worth naming for those coming from denser urban environments.

If you're an outdoor family who lives for weekend trails, lake access, and neighborhood pools over walkable coffee shops, Crandall checks those boxes at a fraction of the price.

Parks, Trails & Green Space

Crandall's park and trail infrastructure is concentrated in the master-planned communities rather than spread across city parks. What's there is genuinely impressive for this price range — the Heartland open space and lake system alone rivals what you'd pay $600K+ to access in Frisco. Beyond the community amenities, Cedar Creek Lake and the Bunker Sands Wetland Center give families regional outdoor options that larger north-side suburbs don't have nearby.

Heartland Lake & Open Space

400 acres of dedicated open space within Heartland, anchored by a 35-acre stocked lake with a fishing pier. Families fish, kayak, and paddle here. Multiple trail connections around the lake perimeter. The Heartland trail network connects neighborhoods to the lake and amenity center — the closest thing to a walkable community that exists in Crandall.

Wildcat Ranch Trail

A 4.2-mile two-loop trail running alongside Buffalo Creek through the Wildcat Ranch community. Both loops are paved and suitable for walking, jogging, and biking. The creek corridor provides shade and a natural feel that distinguishes it from the typical concrete path through a development. Well-used by residents and genuinely pleasant in the cooler months.

Crandall Community Park

The city's main public park — sports fields (baseball, soccer, football), walking trails, playgrounds, and covered picnic pavilions. The park serves youth sports leagues through CAAYSA and is the hub for city-organized outdoor events including Fourth of July celebrations. Open to all residents regardless of HOA affiliation.

Cedar Creek Lake (Regional)

About 30 miles east of Crandall — the regional outdoor escape for boating, fishing, kayaking, and waterfront dining. One of the most accessible large lakes from the southeastern DFW area. Families from Heartland and Wildcat Ranch make regular weekend trips here. Not a substitute for nearby parks but a genuine regional asset that north-side suburbs don't have.

John Bunker Sands Wetland Center

A 300-acre wetland preserve about 40 minutes northwest of Crandall in Seagoville — one of the best birdwatching destinations in North Texas with 270+ documented species. Families with nature-interested kids find this a standout destination. Guided tours, nature walks, and educational programming available. A hidden gem that most DFW residents don't know about.

Forney Community Park (Nearby)

Just 10 minutes north in Forney — a well-maintained park with a 1.2-mile paved loop trail, playgrounds, sports fields, and picnic areas. Crandall families supplement their local options here regularly. Worth factoring in as a practical nearby resource when evaluating how outdoor-accessible the Crandall area really is.

Crandall carries a C+ crime and safety grade from Niche — below average for DFW, but important to put in context. The older sections of the city near downtown show more variability. The northeast areas of the city are generally considered the safest.

Common Questions

FAQ — Living in Crandall, Texas

Are schools good in Crandall, Texas?

Crandall ISD carries a D rating (score: 69/100) from the Texas Education Agency, with 26% math proficiency and 44% reading proficiency — both below state averages. Niche gives the district a C+ composite grade and a B+ for college prep, reflecting a 96% graduation rate. The honest answer is that the district is below average academically, with marked improvement needed. Families who prioritize top-tier school ratings should consider Rockwall ISD, Forney ISD, or other options nearby.

How is the commute from Crandall to Dallas?

Without traffic, Crandall is about 27 miles and 35 minutes from Downtown Dallas via US-175 West. During morning rush hour, that stretches to 55–75 minutes on a typical weekday. DFW Airport is 46 miles (about 48 minutes without traffic). Mesquite — the closest significant employment hub — is just 14 miles and 18 minutes. If your job requires daily commuting to Plano, Frisco, or Las Colinas, budget 45–75 minutes each way.

What is the Heartland community in Crandall, TX?

Heartland is a 2,000-acre award-winning master-planned community located 25 miles southeast of Downtown Dallas in Kaufman County. It features 400 acres of open space, a 35-acre stocked lake with fishing pier, resort pools, two water slides, a fitness center, and on-site elementary schools (Barbara Walker and Hollis T. Dietz). New homes range from the $270s to $610s from builders including Highland Homes, KB Home, and Chesmar. HOA fees are $552/year.

Is Crandall, Texas safe?

Crandall carries a C+ crime and safety grade from Niche. Violent crime runs about 2.3 per 1,000 residents — below the U.S. average of 4.0. Property crime is 13.8 per 1,000, below the Texas average of 22.4. Residents in the master-planned communities (Heartland and Wildcat Ranch) consistently report high levels of day-to-day safety. The northeast sections of Crandall are considered the safest; variability is higher in older parts of the city.

How much do homes cost in Crandall, Texas?

The median single-family home sale price in Crandall is approximately $262,000 (NTREIS, 12-month rolling). New construction in Wildcat Ranch starts in the $240s; Heartland new homes range from the $270s to over $600K from premium builders. Resale homes in the older city areas are generally $180K–$320K. Days on market average 34.5 days with a balanced supply of 3.4 months — not a frenzy but not a buyer's market either.

What are property taxes like in Crandall, TX?

The base combined rate is approximately $2.12 per $100 of assessed value — Crandall ISD ($1.2869), City of Crandall (~$0.55), and Kaufman County ($0.2777). The $100,000 school homestead exemption reduces the ISD portion. Many new construction homes in Heartland and Wildcat Ranch sit inside Municipal Utility Districts (MUDs) that add $0.35–$1.00+/$100, pushing effective rates significantly higher. Always verify MUD status before making an offer on a new build.

Is Crandall Texas affordable compared to other DFW suburbs?

Yes — Crandall is one of the most affordable entry points to new construction master-planned community living in the entire DFW metro. With a median SFR at ~$262K and new builds starting in the $240s, it compares to Frisco ($692K median), Allen ($580K), McKinney ($535K), and even Forney (~$360K). For families prioritizing value over school ratings and willing to commute southeast, Crandall offers a combination that no longer exists at this price in most of the metro.

What are the best neighborhoods in Crandall for families?

For families moving to Crandall, Heartland is the top choice for amenities — 35-acre lake, waterpark, resort pools, and on-site elementary schools (Barbara Walker Elementary rated B- on Niche). Wildcat Ranch is a close second with a 4.2-mile trail and affordable new construction from $240K. Historic downtown Crandall offers larger lots, no HOA, and lower overall cost for families who prefer established neighborhoods without the master-planned structure.

Is Crandall TX a good place to raise a family?

It depends on what your family prioritizes. For families where value is the top priority — brand-new homes, community amenities, quiet neighborhood, and ~30 minutes to Dallas — Crandall genuinely delivers. For families where school academic performance is the top priority, the Crandall ISD ratings (D from TEA) are a real concern. The community is safe, growing, and family-oriented in feel; the school situation requires honest research before committing.

How is Crandall TX different from Forney TX?

Both are in Kaufman County and attract value-oriented DFW buyers, but they differ meaningfully. Forney (~$360K median, Forney ISD) has more developed local amenities — restaurants, retail, and a more established commercial corridor on US-80. Forney ISD carries stronger ratings than Crandall ISD. Crandall offers lower home prices and more abundant new construction, but less local infrastructure. For families where school ratings and local convenience matter more, Forney typically wins. For the lowest price on new construction in a master-planned community, Crandall wins.
