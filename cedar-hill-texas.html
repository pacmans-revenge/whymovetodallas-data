Getting Your Bearings

Where Is Cedar Hill, Texas?

Cedar Hill sits in the southwest quadrant of the Dallas–Fort Worth metroplex, about 19 miles from Downtown Dallas via US-67 North. It's perched on some of the highest terrain in the DFW area — around 800 feet elevation — which gives it rolling hills, cedar-covered ridgelines, and views you simply don't get in the flatlands north of the city. The city hugs the western edge of Joe Pool Lake and is bordered by Grand Prairie to the north, Duncanville to the northeast, and the more rural Ellis County communities to the south.

The primary arteries are US Highway 67 (running northwest into Dallas and southeast toward Midlothian) and Interstate 20, which cuts across the northern edge of Cedar Hill and connects west to Fort Worth and east toward Dallas. US-67 is the daily commute route for most Cedar Hill residents — it feeds directly into Downtown Dallas, and from I-20 you can reach Las Colinas, DFW Airport, and Grand Prairie in 20–30 minutes. One honest note: US-67 does not have HOV lanes, and peak-hour traffic on the Dallas-bound segment can stack up. If your role puts you in Downtown Dallas between 7:30–9 AM, plan for 35–45 minutes on tough mornings.

Transit reality: Cedar Hill has no DART light rail service. The closest DART rail stations are in Dallas (around the Bishop Arts/Westmoreland area), accessible via park-and-ride connections, but a personal vehicle is essential for daily life. Budget for two cars if you have a two-adult household. The city does have a local shuttle (Cedar Hill CityLink) for limited routes, but it is not a commuter transit solution.
Education

Cedar Hill ISD — Schools Deep Dive

Cedar Hill ISD is a mid-sized district serving roughly 6,900 students across 13 campuses. The district carries a C overall rating from Niche and a TEA accountability score of 74 out of 100 (C) in 2025 — up from 71 the year before. That score is honest: this is not one of the top-rated ISDs in DFW, and families moving from highly-rated districts should know that going in. What the ratings don't fully capture is the district's standout collegiate pathway — a specialized program that allows motivated students to earn up to 60 college credit hours, or an associate's degree, before graduating from high school. For families who have a student ready to take advantage of that kind of opportunity, Cedar Hill ISD offers something genuinely exceptional. For families with younger kids just looking for solid fundamentals, it's a working-class community district doing its best in a challenging environment.

Where to Live

Best Neighborhoods in Cedar Hill for Families

Cedar Hill's geography — rolling hills, lakefront terrain, and established suburbs blending into newer development — creates real variety in what's available. Lake Ridge is in a class by itself for outdoor access and custom-home character. High Pointe is the go-to for families who want a traditional suburban neighborhood at a practical price point. Newer communities near US-67 cater to buyers looking for modern finishes without the premium of north DFW suburbs.

$350K–$1.2M+ · Lakeside Master-Planned

Lake Ridge

Cedar Hill's most distinctive neighborhood — a 3,400-acre lakeside community overlooking Joe Pool Lake. Seven gated sub-communities, 1,200+ custom homes, a golf course, stocked fishing pond, sport courts, and direct access to DORBA mountain bike trails and the lake. This is where buyers come when they want space, privacy, and lake country living without leaving DFW. Prices range widely by lot and view — entry-level custom homes start around $350K, with lakefront estates topping $1M+.

$280K–$420K · Established Family

High Pointe

High Pointe is Cedar Hill's signature family neighborhood — an established community with mature trees, cul-de-sacs, and the feel of a place where kids actually play outside. It feeds High Pointe Elementary and sits on the southeast side of Cedar Hill close to the lake corridor. Homes here are predominantly 1990s–2000s builds with brick exteriors, larger lots than you'd find in comparable price-point suburbs north of Dallas, and active neighborhood HOA events through the year.

The Hills of Lake Ridge

A newer development in the Lake Ridge area, The Hills offers larger lots on the elevated terrain that Cedar Hill is known for. Homes here were built predominantly in the 2000s–2010s and offer more square footage than many same-priced suburbs. The lake is nearby but not within walking distance — you're buying the land and the peace, with quick access to Cedar Hill State Park trails and Joe Pool Lake boat ramps a few minutes away.

$230K–$360K · Central / Near Uptown Village

Beltway Park & Central Cedar Hill

The neighborhoods surrounding Uptown Village — Cedar Hill's main commercial corridor — offer the city's most walkable experience and the most accessible price points. These are established 1980s–2000s subdivisions with solid bones, reasonable lot sizes, and quick access to Walmart, Target, and the restaurants and services along US-67. Commutes are straightforward. This is where first-time buyers and value-focused families tend to land.

First Texas Homes builds new construction in Cedar Hill starting in the low $400s — among the more affordable new-build options in the Dallas metro.
